## 0. THE ONE-LINE TARGET *(corrected)*

> **Rogue Fable IV's** movement-is-everything, positioning-over-stats grid tactics +
> **Cyberpunk 2077's** systemic build/role layer (cyberware, quickhacks, netrunners,
> the time-slow hero move) — on Bohemia's 120bpm beat, resolved through the Dead Eye Dial.

Bohemia is a **rhythm roguelike tactics** game. Rogue Fable IV is the skeleton (turn,
grid, movement, target priority). Cyberpunk is the flesh (roles, builds, tech verbs).
The beat and the dial are what make it ours.

## 1. WHAT ROGUE FABLE IV ACTUALLY DOES  *(grounded — this is the core study)*

A fast, turn-based, grid traditional roguelike. Dense, highly varied ~1-hour runs.
"Coffee-break roguelike": deep but finishable. **Player skill matters vastly more than
raw stats.** The design lessons, in priority order:

1. **MOVEMENT IS THE PRIMARY SKILL.** You move to dodge slow projectiles, sidestep
   telegraphed melee, avoid explosions, escape deadly clouds, run circles around slow
   brutes, and dive into the enemy back line. Positioning and timing win fights, not DPS.

2. **AVOIDABLE ATTACKS EXIST TO FORCE MOVEMENT.** Slow-moving projectiles, telegraphed
   attacks, and channeled spells that resolve over several turns are all dodgeable by
   simply moving. *The point is not what they do on hit — it's the movement they force
   you into.* They push you off the tile you wanted, into positions you didn't want,
   creating unpredictable, ever-shifting board states. **This is the single biggest
   lesson for Bohemia.**

3. **TARGET SELECTION IS A PUZZLE.** 350+ monsters, each with attack patterns and
   abilities. **Support monsters (heal, buff, shield) are top-priority kills** — e.g.
   *Force Shield* makes an ally invulnerable for 3 turns. Reading the room and choosing
   the right target in the right order IS the skill.

4. **BOTH SIDES REPOSITION.** Player and monsters both have movement abilities that
   snap them around the board, multiplying the configurations and the tactics. A fight
   is a moving puzzle, not a static trade.

5. **THE BOARD IS ALIVE.** Terrain effects, traps, magical auras, hazardous zones,
   cover, ambushes — the tiles themselves are part of the fight.

6. **DENSE VARIETY.** 12 classes, 9 races, 60+ talents (three upgrade levels each),
   300+ items with unique effects beyond stats. The run is a build you assemble live.

**The transferable core:** *a fight is a movement puzzle where the threats force you to
reposition, and choosing WHO to kill WHEN is the skill.* Bohemia already has the grid,
the beat, the dial, cover, and one-tile movement. What it lacks is **threats that force
movement** and **target-priority that matters.** That is the gap RF4 exposes.

## 2. WHAT CYBERPUNK 2077 ADDS  *(grounded — the systemic flesh)*

- **Cyberware is the build spine.** Everything hangs off your loadout.
- **Quickhacks + RAM:** pre-fire manipulation — shock, blind (Reboot Optics), poison
  spread (Contagion), turn-them-on-each-other (Cyberpsychosis). Reshape the fight before
  a shot. RAM is the resource, regenerates.
- **Sandevistan:** slow time ~80%, act while the world crawls. The hero-fantasy burst.
- **Weapon archetypes as VERBS:** Power (ricochet), Tech (shoot through cover), Smart
  (homing). Not stat swaps — different verbs.
- **Enemies have ROLES you scan and prioritize:** muscle (rush), shooters (cover +
  flank + snipe), netrunners (debuff from range — kill first). This is the SAME lesson
  as RF4's "kill the support first," in a gunfight skin.
- **Builds:** netrunner / solo / stealth / hybrid — your kit is your identity.
- **The trap to avoid:** at its worst, enemies read as bullet sponges, not a thinking
  challenge. RF4's answer (avoidable, movement-forcing threats) is the cure.

**Where the two GAMES agree** (and so Bohemia should double down):
**kill the support/netrunner first**, **positioning beats raw power**, **the enemy makes
you move**. RF4 says it with slow projectiles; Cyberpunk says it with flanking and
grenades. Same truth.

## 4. THE BIG REFRAME RF4 FORCES ON US

Right now Bohemia's movement is **optional** — you pop, you shoot, you occasionally step.
RF4 says that's backwards: **the enemy should make you move, and moving well should be
how you win.** The single highest-value direction this research points to:

> **Give enemies telegraphed, beat-counted threats that punish standing still, so the
> tile board becomes a live dodging puzzle — and make the player's mobility verbs
> (dash/vault/retreat/suppress) the answer to that pressure.**

This is why Paolo's ruled mobility features (suppress, hand-peek, cover-to-cover dash,
vault) are exactly right and should ship first: they are the player's half of the
movement puzzle. The enemy's half — movement-forcing telegraphs + priority supports —
is the next system after.

## 5. PAOLO'S RULINGS (still hold, RF4 confirms them harder)

1. **FLANKING GATED BY ENEMY SMARTS.** Per-archetype intelligence drives flank +
   reposition + (later) support-caster behavior. RF4: both sides reposition; dumb things
   don't.
2. **SUPPRESSING FIRE + HAND-PEEK COVER + MICRO-GREEN.** Cover is not all-or-nothing;
   peek only hands+gun over the edge (standing OR ducking) for a smaller profile, and
   suppress to force heads down and crack a tiny green even under 8 guns. *(In build now.)*
3. **COVER-TO-COVER DASH** + **VAULT over ducking cover** — greenlit, in build (v54).
4. **STAMINA MODEL (Fable/AP intuition):** a stamina action does NOT end your turn.
   Suppress / dash / vault spend stamina and let you still take your shot or move the
   same beat. This is the fluid, non-locking feel. *(In build now.)*

## 6. PROPOSED BUILD ORDER *(recommendation)*

1. **v54 mobility verbs** — stamina spine + suppress + hand-peek + cover-to-cover dash +
   vault-over-ducking. The player's half of the movement puzzle. *(In progress.)*
2. **Movement-forcing enemy threats** — one telegraphed, beat-counted attack first (a
   grenade or a charged sniper lane) that makes vacating a tile mandatory. This is the
   RF4 keystone; it makes v54's mobility MATTER.
3. **Support/priority enemies + SMARTS** — a shield-caster or netrunner you must kill
   first; flanking gated by smarts. Now target selection becomes a real puzzle.
4. **Hazard tiles + living board** — fire/gas/sightline auras.
5. **DEAD EYE burst** — the hero moment, on the hero-beat.
6. **The RIG + tactical verbs (BLIND/JAM/GLITCH)** — the roguelite build engine.
7. **Companions** — the last, biggest axis.

Rationale: RF4 proves the loop is *movement under pressure + target priority.* Build the
player's mobility (1), then the pressure that demands it (2-4), then the build depth (5-7).

## 8. PAOLO'S DESIGN DIRECTIONS UNDER EXPLORATION (7/24, not yet locked)

### 8a. "Enemy bullets take turns to reach you, by distance" + RF4's STRAFE-DODGE

**What RF4 actually does (grounded, the key find):** ranged attacks are slow and target
a TILE. The **strafe-dodge rule**: if you move perpendicular (strafe), the projectile is
aimed at the square you *moved from* — so movement inherently dodges. Exception: if you
start OUTSIDE the enemy's range and end INSIDE it, they hit where you ended up. If you
don't move and it reaches you, normal evasion applies; if you evade it passes through to
max range. So: **movement is the dodge, closing INTO range is the risk.**

**The hole in "all bullets are slow":** bullets are instant in reality, and if every
shot crawls, point-blank becomes un-dodgeable anyway while long-range becomes a trivial
walk-off. Full slow-bullets flattens both ends.

**The synthesis that keeps Paolo's instinct AND the realism:** grade travel time by
distance and only make the COMMITTED return-fire beat dodgeable.
- **Close shooters hit instantly** (no travel — realistic, closing distance is deadly).
- **Distant shooters' return fire resolves one beat LATER, aimed at the TILE you popped
  from** (RF4 strafe-dodge in our clothes). So if you **dash / vault / retreat before it
  lands, the far shots miss** — you left the tile they aimed at.
- Result: **range = reaction window. Distance is safety; closing is commitment.** And it
  makes the v54 mobility verbs the literal counter to being shot at.
- **Plus heavy telegraphed threats travel visibly and land on a counted beat** regardless
  of range: grenades (leave the tile), a charging sniper lane (break the line), a
  molotov/gas cloud (vacate). These are the pure RF4 movement-forcers.

This is the biggest single decision on the board and it reshapes the return-fire model.

### 8b. WEAPON = killshots-per-turn CEILING x dial width (Paolo's idea)

Weapon identity becomes a matrix, not a damage number:

| Weapon | Killshots/turn | Dial | The fantasy |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sniper | 1 (hard cap) | fattest/easiest, longest range | one perfect, unhurried shot |
| Bow | 1 (locked, ignores perks) | medium | the purist — guaranteed, ignores your build |
| Pistol | up to your skill | medium | the all-rounder |
| SMG | 2+ (up to skill) | narrowest/hardest | spray — rewards rhythm mastery |
| Shotgun | 1-2, close only | wide but short | brutal up close |

**The balance hole Paolo already half-answered:** if the bow ignores your perks/skill,
nobody picks it UNLESS its one shot is correspondingly huge. So lock the PRINCIPLE:
**killshot-ceiling is inversely tied to per-shot payoff.** Bow = 1 shot but guaranteed
lethal / armor-ignoring. Sniper = 1 shot but easiest dial + pierce + range. SMG = many
shots, each smaller, hardest dial. Low ceiling ↔ big single hit; high ceiling ↔ small
hits that demand execution. That keeps every weapon a real choice, not a strict upgrade.

Interacts with the existing chainSkill (killshots/turn, default 2): the WEAPON sets the
cap, your skill fills toward it, and some weapons (bow, sniper) cap BELOW your skill on
purpose — that IS their identity.

### 8c. RF4 ability-design lesson (grounded, validates our direction)

RF4's dev deliberately makes abilities **weaker but cheaper / lower cooldown** so they're
used constantly: "almost never a turn where the player isn't either using an ability or
moving to set up one." This validates the **stamina model** (cheap, frequent, non-
turn-ending verbs) and the beat's I-MOVE-YOU-MOVE. Rule of thumb: **many small decisions
beat a few big ones.** Keep suppress/dash/vault cheap and frequent, not rare ultimates.
Also RF4 talents: 4x tier-1, 3x tier-2, 1x tier-3, upgrades change how a talent plays —
the template for the RIG's draft depth.

### 8d. Other RF4 steals worth queuing

- **Strafe-dodge** (8a) as the core "movement is the dodge" rule.
- **Support-first target priority:** shield-caster (Force Shield = ally invuln 2-3 beats),
  healer, netrunner (shrinks your green). Kill order is the puzzle.
- **Movement-forcing telegraphs:** slow projectile-to-a-tile, multi-beat channels, growing
  hazard clouds (RF4's Inferno-Staff = a 4-range flaming cloud lasting 2 turns).
- **"Evade passes through to max range"** — a missed shot keeps going and can hit what's
  behind you (or an enemy behind you). Friendly-fire and line geometry become tactical.

## 9. THE IDENTITY QUESTION (Paolo 7/24): does RF4 movement fight our cover + dial?

Paolo's sharp pull-hole: RF4 combat is amazing, but does it **go against the cover
idea**, what does it **mean to shoot an enemy**, and **what is the Dead Eye Dial for** if
we bolt RF4-style movement on? This is the crux of the game's identity. Honest answer:

### There are THREE layers, and they are orthogonal (this is the resolution)

1. **BOARD position** — where you stand, tile to tile. *RF4's whole game.* Kiting,
   flanking, dodging telegraphs, breaking sightlines.
2. **ENGAGEMENT timing** — WHEN you break cover to shoot. *Bohemia's cover cycle + green
   window.* Reading the enemy peek/fire rhythm, picking your moment.
3. **SHOT execution** — HOW WELL the shot lands. *The Dead Eye Dial.* The rhythm/timing
   commit that grades kill/vital/hit/miss.

RF4 has layer 1 and *nothing* for layer 3 — you click, it hits. **The dial is the layer
RF4 completely lacks.** So RF4 movement does not threaten the dial; they are different
skills on different axes. RF4 decides IF/WHEN/WHERE you shoot; the dial decides HOW WELL.
Remove the dial and Bohemia is just a roguelike. Remove the board and it's just a rhythm
game. **The fusion IS the identity: RF4 gives us the board, the dial gives us the beat.**

### Cover vs movement must defend DIFFERENT threats, or one kills the other

The real danger (naming it honestly): if movement becomes your universal defense,
players kite forever and the cover cycle we built over 50 versions goes vestigial. Bolt
RF4 onto a cover shooter carelessly and you get incoherence — is my defense hiding or
moving? The fix is to split the threats:

- **COVER defends against aimed gunfire** — the ready gun-line with a bead on you.
  Standing in the open against 8 guns is death. Cover is home base.
- **MOVEMENT defends against what cover CAN'T stop** — telegraphed/committed threats:
  grenades landing on your tile, a charging sniper lane, the delayed distant return-fire
  aimed at your last tile, a blade closing. You can't hide from these; you reposition.

So the enemy roster makes you **flow between hiding and darting**: hunker against the
gun-line, then BURST out and reposition when they lob a grenade or a blade closes. Cover
and movement stop being rivals and become a rhythm of their own.

### The ratio is the identity decision — PAOLO RULED (7/24)

**Cover is ONE of the tools of safety, not the whole game.** (Paolo 7/24: "i def want
cover to be one of the tools of safety fs.") So Bohemia leans **movement-forward with a
SAFETY TOOLKIT**, and cover is one instrument in it, not the sole home base. The toolkit:

1. **Cover** — a pillar stops aimed gunfire from a direction. One tool, always available.
2. **Movement / reposition** — dash, vault, retreat, step. Dodge telegraphs, break locks,
   change your angle, dive the back line.
3. **Suppress** — force heads down, manufacture a window even under many guns.
4. **Hand-peek** — a smaller profile the moment you do expose.
5. **Distance / range** — far = reaction time (the graded travel-time model, 8a).
6. **Green-window timing** — pop when the read says safe.
7. *(future)* **Dead Eye burst** — slow the beat to act safely.

Design job: make each tool answer a different pressure so they all stay alive, and keep
the whole thing **fun, tactical, rewarding, and SNAPPY** (Paolo's four words, 7/24). No
single tool is a turtle button; the fun is FLOWING between them on the beat.

### What it MEANS to shoot an enemy (under all three layers)

You (1) survive to a window by reading cover + dodging what cover can't stop [board +
engagement], (2) commit while exposed — the risk [engagement], (3) NAIL the dial —
execution [dial]. The shot is *earned* by position and *paid off* by rhythm. RF4 movement
is how you manufacture the window and dodge the un-hideable; the dial is why the shot is
skill and not a free click. They need each other.

### 9b. AMMO as a resource axis (Paolo's bow insight)

Paolo, correcting my "bow needs a huge single hit": people pick the bow because **it's
cool and it still kills — and it could have UNLIMITED ammo.** That's the real draw, and
it opens an axis we hadn't discussed:

- **Guns run on limited ammo** (a scavenged, scarce resource — fits the post-economic
  Vegas world, RF4/Cyberpunk resource management). Reloads, pickups, scarcity decisions.
- **Bow / melee = unlimited** (no ammo economy). The bow's identity is *sustainable* — 1
  killshot/turn, still lethal, never runs dry. You bring it for the long grind, not the
  burst.
- This makes weapon choice a resource decision, not just a damage/dial one: burst-now
  (SMG, ammo-hungry) vs sustain-forever (bow, free). A whole new tactical + roguelite
  economy layer. [Ammo table: MECHANISM-MINE / CONTENTS-PAOLO'S — build empty, he tunes.]
