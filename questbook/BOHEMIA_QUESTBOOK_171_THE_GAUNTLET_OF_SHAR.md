**Game:** Baldur's Gate 3 (2023)
**Studio:** Larian Studios
**Quest:** The Gauntlet of Shar — the temple-trial dungeon where Shadowheart proves herself to the goddess of loss by passing tests that reward embracing darkness, forgetting pain, and finally killing a captive Nightsong — a dungeon whose every puzzle is a small step toward erasing yourself.
**Type:** INDIVIDUAL-QUEST DEEP DIVE (mined fresh 7/17 from the Baldur's Gate 3 catalogue; corpus anchor Q16/Q165 for the game's larger machines)
**FORMAT:** v2 — cast + conversation node trees + branch map.
**Filed:** 7/17/26
**Why pulled:** Bohemia needs a dungeon whose MECHANICS embody a theology — trials that teach you to erase yourself one puzzle at a time — as the physical prelude to the House of Grief (#165) and the Nightsong choice (#16). The Gauntlet is the specimen: a temple where the "correct" solution to each trial is to embrace darkness, distrust the light, forget your pain, so that by the time you reach the final test you have been trained, mechanically, to do the unthinkable. ## 0 CORE IDEA

**A temple of the goddess of loss tests the faithful with trials whose every "correct" answer is a small self-erasure — walk into darkness, distrust the light, forget your pain — so that by the final trial the pilgrim has been trained, room by room, to kill the one they were sent to free.**

Shadowheart must prove herself to Shar, goddess of loss, darkness, and forgetting, by completing the Gauntlet of Shar — a temple-dungeon of trials. And the trials are theology made into puzzles. The Self-Same Trial pits you against dark reflections of yourself. The Soft-Step Trial rewards moving through shadow and punishes the light. The Faith-Leap Trial demands you step off a ledge into apparent nothingness, trusting Shar's darkness over your own senses — the light-based path is a trap, the leap of faith into the dark is the "solution." Each trial teaches, mechanically, that the goddess's way is to distrust what you see, embrace what erases you, and forget what hurts. And the Gauntlet's destination is the Nightsong: a captive the pilgrim is sent to kill as the final proof of devotion — a killing the trials have spent the whole dungeon training you to be able to do.

The machine is a level that indoctrinates through its own rules. The player does not just hear Shar's theology; they PRACTICE it — every puzzle solved by embracing darkness is a rep, a small conditioning, so that the final ask (kill the captive) arrives to a pilgrim who has been mechanically rehearsed into the mindset that makes it thinkable. And the Nightsong reveal detonates it: the captive is not a monster but Dame Aylin, an immortal being held and tortured by Shar's cult for centuries, and killing her is the atrocity the trials disguised as devotion. The player, at the end, can complete the indoctrination (kill the Nightsong, prove faith, take Shar's reward) or break it (spare her, defy the goddess, reject everything the dungeon trained) — and the choice's difficulty is the whole point: the Gauntlet spent hours making the atrocity feel like the natural next step.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** a doctrine that erases you does it one small acceptable step at a time, each rewarded, so that the unthinkable thing at the end feels like the natural conclusion of everything you already agreed to.

## 1 CAST + WHAT EACH ONE WANTS

### THE GAUNTLET (Shar's trials) — the level that indoctrinates
- **What it wants:** to train the pilgrim, trial by trial, into the goddess's mindset — distrust the light, embrace the dark, forget the pain.
- **What it'll trade:** progress and reward, for each small self-erasure.
- **Function:** THEME-THROUGH-LEVEL: a dungeon whose rules ARE a theology, conditioning the player toward the final atrocity.

### SHADOWHEART — the pilgrim being trained
- **What she wants (stated):** to prove herself to Shar, to complete the trials, to earn the goddess's favor.
- **What she wants (real):** depends on her arc — to belong, or to break free; the Gauntlet is the pressure that decides which.
- **Function:** THE INDOCTRINATED: the one the trials are training toward the killing, her fate the dungeon's real stake.

### DAME AYLIN / THE NIGHTSONG — the captive the trials train you to kill
- **What she wants:** freedom; she is an immortal held and tortured by Shar's cult for centuries.
- **Function:** THE ATROCITY at the end: the "final trial" revealed as a person, whose killing the whole dungeon disguised as devotion.

### THE PLAYER — the one who can break the training
- **What they want:** to help Shadowheart, and to decide whether to complete or reject the indoctrination.
- **Function:** THE POSSIBLE REFUSAL: the one who can spare the Nightsong and defy everything the Gauntlet trained.

### THE FAITH-LEAP — the training in one trial
- The trial demanding you step into the dark, trusting the goddess over your senses.
- **Function:** THE CONDITIONING made explicit: the "solution" is to distrust what you see and embrace what erases you.

## 4 THE BRANCH MAP

**COUNT: 2 terminal outcomes — kill the Nightsong (indoctrination completed) or spare her (training broken) — reached through a dungeon of trials that mechanically condition the player toward the kill, gated at the end by Shadowheart's arc.**

- **KILL THE NIGHTSONG:** the trials completed as designed; the captive killed; Shar's devotion proven; the dungeon's conditioning brought to its intended conclusion. WRITES: the atrocity done; the training accepted; Shadowheart deeper in Shar's hold.
- **SPARE THE NIGHTSONG:** the training rejected; the goddess defied; Dame Aylin freed. WRITES: the indoctrination broken; the atrocity refused; Shadowheart's path toward freedom (feeding the House of Grief, #165).
- **THE CONDITIONING CONSTANT:** in both branches, the trials spent hours training the player toward the kill — the choice's difficulty is the dungeon's whole point, whichever way it breaks.
- **THE THROUGH-LINE:** the branch is whether the player completes or rejects an indoctrination they practiced room by room — two answers to "will you do the atrocity the doctrine trained you for," and the Gauntlet made refusing it as hard as it could.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — THE TRIALS' CONDITIONING IS THEMATIC, NOT MECHANICALLY BINDING.** The trials teach Shar's mindset, but a player can pass them while mentally rejecting the theology the whole time — the "conditioning" is narrative dressing, not a mechanic that actually shifts the player's disposition, so the final kill isn't really made easier by the trials, just contextualized by them.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a dungeon's TRIALS are meant to condition the player toward a final act, the conditioning must have real mechanical or psychological grip — small commitments that accrue, a disposition the game tracks — so the final ask genuinely feels like the trained next step, not just a themed one. Indoctrination-through-mechanics must actually condition (the #29 slippery-slope law, applied to a dungeon).

**F2 — THE NIGHTSONG'S PERSONHOOD ARRIVES LATE.** Dame Aylin is revealed as a tortured prisoner only at the final trial, so the "atrocity disguised as devotion" lands as a twist rather than a dawning horror; a player who felt her presence earlier would experience the trials as a mounting dread instead of a late reveal. The victim is a reveal, not a build.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** the person a doctrine trains you to harm should be FELT before the final ask — glimpsed, heard, sensed through the trials — so the conditioning is a mounting dread, not a late twist. The atrocity's victim built across the dungeon, not sprung at its end (the #155/#157 humanize-before-the-choice law).

**F3 — THE REWARD FOR COMPLETING SKEWS THE CHOICE.** Killing the Nightsong grants a significant power reward, so the "complete the indoctrination" path is mechanically incentivized — the game pays you to do the atrocity, which can tip the choice toward the kill for reasons that have nothing to do with the theology the dungeon built. The doctrine's endpoint is bribed.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a doctrine's final atrocity carries a power reward, that bribe must be a RECORDED moral act with weight — the fold marking that the dynast took the power for the killing — so completing the indoctrination for the reward is felt as a stain, not laundered as loot (the #31/#164 cruelty-incentivized law).

## 6 WHY IT WORKS (W1-W10)

W1. THE LEVEL IS A THEOLOGY. Every trial is Shar's doctrine made a puzzle — the player doesn't hear the faith, they PRACTICE it. Theme-through-level-design (#35) aimed at self-erasure, room by room.
W2. THE SOLUTIONS ARE SELF-ERASURES. Step into the dark, distrust the light, forget the pain — each "correct" answer is a small self-abandonment, so solving the dungeon IS accepting the goddess.
W3. THE CONDITIONING BUILDS TO THE ATROCITY. The trials train the pilgrim, rep by rep, into the mindset that makes the final kill thinkable — the dungeon spends hours making the unthinkable feel like the next step.
W4. THE FAITH-LEAP IS THE THESIS. Stepping into apparent nothingness, trusting the goddess over your senses, is the doctrine in one trial: distrust what you see, embrace what erases you.
W5. THE FINAL TRIAL IS A PERSON. The Nightsong is revealed as a tortured immortal, not a monster — the "devotion" the trials built toward is an atrocity, disguised as the natural conclusion of faith.
W6. THE CHOICE'S DIFFICULTY IS THE POINT. The Gauntlet spent hours making the kill feel natural, so refusing it means rejecting everything the dungeon trained — the design's whole purpose is to make the right choice hard.
W7. THE DOCTRINE HAS NO CLEAN COMPLETION. The final trial IS the killing; there is no way to keep the faith without the crime — the theology's endpoint is the atrocity, and the dungeon is honest about it.
W8. SPARING HER BREAKS EVERYTHING. Defying Shar means rejecting the trials, the training, the reward, the goddess — the refusal is a repudiation of the whole dungeon, which is why it's hard.
W9. IT IS THE PHYSICAL PRELUDE TO THE COMPANION ARC. The Gauntlet feeds the House of Grief (#165) — the dungeon's conditioning is the pressure that Shadowheart's whole reclaim-or-kneel arc is measured against.
W10. THE SLIPPERY SLOPE IS A DUNGEON. Frostpunk's decree-by-decree drift (#29) made spatial: each small acceptable step, rewarded, leading to the monstrous conclusion. The doctrine's gradualism, walkable.
