# BOHEMIA QUESTBOOK #220 — "THE DEMANDS OF THE QUN (A SPY MUST CHOOSE BETWEEN THE MEN WHO TRUST HIM AND THE ORDER THAT GIVES HIM A SELF, KNOWING ONE CHOICE ERASES HIM)"
**Game:** Dragon Age: Inquisition (2014)
**Studio:** BioWare
**Quest:** The Iron Bull's loyalty mission (Demands of the Qun) — a mercenary who is secretly a spy for a rigid collectivist society faces a battlefield choice where saving his own loyal crew requires disobeying an order from the society that defines his entire identity, forcing him to decide between the men who trust him and the belief system that tells him who he is, with no way to keep both.

**Why pulled:** Bohemia needs the quest where a person must choose between the people who trust them and the ideology that gives them a self, where one choice saves lives and the other preserves identity, and where choosing the men means being cast out and renamed by the order forever. ## 0 CORE IDEA

**A mercenary captain who is secretly a lifelong agent of a rigid, collectivist society — one that subordinates the individual entirely to the whole, and which has given him not just orders but his name, his purpose, and his entire sense of self — faces a battlefield moment where the society's command requires him to sacrifice his own loyal mercenary crew to preserve a strategic alliance, and he must choose between the men who have trusted and followed him for years and the belief system that defines who he is, knowing that saving the crew means disobeying the Qun, and disobeying the Qun means being cast out, declared broken, and stripped of the identity it gave him — that there is no choice that keeps both the people and the self.**

The Iron Bull is a Qunari, and more: he is a Ben-Hassrath, a spy and enforcer for the Qun, the totalizing philosophy of his people that assigns every individual a role, a purpose, and a place within the collective, subordinating personal desire entirely to the good of the whole. The Qun does not just employ Bull; it constitutes him — his name is a role, his self is a function, his sanity (as the Qun frames it) depends on belonging to the whole. For years, though, he has also captained a company of mercenaries, the Chargers, a crew of misfits who trust him, follow him, and love him as their leader. Demands of the Qun brings these two belongings into direct collision: on the battlefield, the Qun's command comes down that Bull must let the Chargers die to hold a position and preserve a critical alliance between the Inquisition and the Qunari. The collective demands the sacrifice of the few. And Bull must choose, in the moment, with the player's counsel: obey and let his loyal crew be slaughtered (keeping his place in the Qun, his identity intact, his self preserved as a good agent of the whole), or save the Chargers by disobeying (keeping the men who trust him, at the cost of being declared Tal-Vashoth — broken, cast out, no longer of the Qun, stripped of the self it gave him).

The machine is that the collective and the few are both legitimate claims, and the self is an institution, so choosing the people you love costs you the identity the order gave you — and there is no way to keep both. The quest refuses to make the Qun simply evil or the Chargers simply right. The Qun's demand is coherent: the alliance matters, the strategic logic holds, and Bull's entire functioning self is built on trusting that the whole knows better than the individual. The Chargers' claim is equally real: they are loyal men who trusted their captain, and letting them die to satisfy a doctrine is a betrayal of the human bond. And the cruelty is that Bull's identity is not separable from the Qun — to disobey is not just to break a rule but to lose the framework that tells him who he is, to become, in his own people's eyes and possibly his own, broken. So the choice is between two kinds of self-loss: sacrifice the men and keep the identity but carry the betrayal, or save the men and keep the bond but lose the self the order constructed. The player's counsel tips it, and the honesty is that when your identity is given to you by an institution, loyalty to the people in front of you can require the destruction of who you were told you are — and that there is no belonging that doesn't, at some point, demand you choose between the collective that defines you and the individuals who trust you.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** when your identity is handed to you by an institution — a faith, an order, a collective — being loyal to the actual people in front of you can require destroying the self that institution gave you, and there is no belonging that never forces the choice between the whole that defines you and the few who trust you; both claims are legitimate, the self is not separable from the order that built it, and choosing the people you love is sometimes indistinguishable from losing who you were told you are.

### THE IRON BULL — the spy whose self is the order
- **What he wants (stated):** to serve the Qun; to keep his crew; to not have to choose.
- **What he wants (real):** for the collective and the few to not be opposites — which the battlefield makes impossible.
- **What he'll lose either way:** the men, or the self the Qun gave him.
- **Function:** THE IDENTITY-IS-AN-INSTITUTION: the man whose entire self was constructed by the order he may have to defy.

- The totalizing philosophy that gives Bull his name, role, and self, and now demands the sacrifice.
- **Function:** THE WHOLE THAT CONSTITUTES HIM: the order whose command is coherent and whose loss is self-erasure.

### THE CHARGERS — the crew who trust him
- Bull's loyal mercenary company; misfits who followed him for years; the few the collective would sacrifice.
- **Function:** THE FEW WHO TRUST HIM: the human bond the doctrine demands he betray.

### THE PLAYER (the Inquisitor) — the counsel that tips the choice
- **What they want:** to help Bull face a choice that erases one belonging or the other.
- **Function:** THE TIPPING COUNSEL: the one whose word shapes whether Bull keeps the men or the self.

### THE TAL-VASHOTH FATE — the self-loss of disobedience
- To be declared broken, cast out, stripped of the Qun-given identity; the price of saving the crew.
- **Function:** THE ERASURE: the loss of the constructed self that saving the few would cost.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — THE QUN CAN READ AS OBVIOUSLY WRONG, MAKING "SAVE THE CHARGERS" THE CLEAR CHOICE.** If the collectivist order is framed as plainly oppressive, sacrificing the crew reads as obviously evil and the dilemma collapses — the power depends on the Qun's demand being genuinely coherent and Bull's belonging to it being a real, sympathetic self, not a cult to escape.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest pits a collective against the few, the COLLECTIVE'S claim must be made legitimately coherent — the order's logic and the self it gives shown as real — so sacrificing the few is a genuine choice, not an obvious evil to reject (#191's both-sides-legitimate law).

**F2 — BULL'S IDENTITY-LOSS CAN BE UNDERSOLD, MAKING TAL-VASHOTH JUST A LABEL.** The cost of disobeying is that Bull loses the self the Qun constructed; if becoming Tal-Vashoth reads as merely a status change rather than a genuine unmaking of who he is, the "the self is an institution" idea flattens and saving the crew looks costless.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest's cost is losing an institution-given self, that unmaking must be FELT — the erasure of identity shown as real self-loss, not a label swap — so disobeying carries the true weight of becoming un-who-you-were (#214's the-self-loss-must-be-real law).

**F3 — THE CHARGERS CAN BE ABSTRACT, WEAKENING THE BOND'S CLAIM.** The few's claim rides on the Chargers being a real, loved crew whose trust in Bull the player has felt; if they're generic mercenaries, sacrificing them is an abstraction and the human-bond side of the dilemma loses to the vivid ideology side.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest weighs a collective against the few, the FEW must be vivid enough to weigh — the bond and their trust made real — so the human claim balances the ideological one rather than being an abstraction (#207's the-few-must-be-real law).

## 6 WHY IT WORKS (W1-W10)

W1. THE SELF IS AN INSTITUTION. The Qun gave Bull his name, role, and identity — disobeying isn't breaking a rule but losing who he is, which is the whole engine.
W2. BOTH CLAIMS ARE LEGITIMATE. The collective's command is coherent and the crew's trust is real — neither side is the obvious right.
W3. NO CHOICE KEEPS BOTH. The removed "keep the crew and the Qun both" verb is the spine: the battlefield forces the men or the self, never both.
W4. CHOOSING THE FEW ERASES THE SELF. Saving the Chargers means becoming Tal-Vashoth — the people you love bought with who you were told you are.
W5. OBEYING KEEPS THE SELF, BREAKS THE BOND. Sacrificing the men preserves the identity and carries the betrayal — the other kind of self-loss.
W6. THE COLLECTIVE VS THE FEW IS ETERNAL. Every belonging eventually demands the choice — the quest names a permanent tension, not a one-off.
W7. THE QUN CONSTITUTES, NOT JUST EMPLOYS. Bull's sanity and self depend on belonging — which makes the choice existential, not tactical.
W8. THE CHARGERS ARE LOVED. A real crew who trust their captain — the human bond given weight against the ideology.
W9. THE PLAYER COUNSELS, BULL CHOOSES. The Inquisitor tips it but the choice is Bull's — his self, his loss.
W10. IT IS THE-COLLECTIVE-VERSUS-THE-FEW IN ONE SPY. An institution-given self against the people who trust you — Bohemia's whole faction question of belonging and identity, on one battlefield.
