# BOHEMIA QUESTBOOK #181 — "KLAASJE AND THE HANGED MAN (THE KILLER YOU CAN'T CHARGE AND THE WITNESS YOU CAN'T KEEP)"
**Game:** Disco Elysium (2019)
**Studio:** ZA/UM
**Quest:** The central murder investigation's Klaasje thread — the prime suspect, a woman living under a false name in the room beneath the hanging body, gives you the truth that clears her and simultaneously admits she tampered with the crime scene, leaving you holding a witness you could arrest for the wrong crime, let walk, or turn over, none of which is justice and all of which are on you.
**Type:** INDIVIDUAL-QUEST DEEP DIVE (mined fresh 7/17 from the Disco Elysium catalogue; corpus anchor Q11 for the game's larger machines)
**FORMAT:** v2 — cast + conversation node trees + branch map.
**Filed:** 7/17/26
**Why pulled:** Bohemia needs the quest where the person who gives you the truth is also guilty of something else, and telling that truth costs them everything while charging them would be the wrong crime — where a witness's honesty makes them arrestable and their arrest would be a lie about what happened. Klaasje is the specimen: a suspect who didn't do the murder but did tamper with the scene, whose candor clears the case and convicts her of a lesser real thing, and whom the detective must let walk, hold, or hand over with no clean option. It is the unrecorded-ledger (#26) as an interrogation, and the "the truth-teller is guilty of the wrong thing" machine the conduct-book can carry.

## 0 CORE IDEA

**The prime suspect in the murder you are investigating — a woman living under a false identity in the room directly beneath the hanging body — tells you a truth that clears her of the killing and, in the same breath, admits she tampered with the crime scene and has fled crimes before; her honesty is what breaks the case open and what makes her arrestable, and the detective is left holding a witness he can let walk, charge for the wrong crime, or turn over, none of which is justice.**

You are a detective investigating a lynching: a man found hanging behind a hostel in a district run by a union on the edge of open war. Klaasje — not her real name — rented the room beneath the body and was the dead man's lover. She is the obvious suspect, and for most of the case she is the case. When you finally break her, she does not confess to murder. She tells you the truth: she did not kill him, but she found the body, panicked, and tampered with the scene — moved things, cleaned things, protected herself, because she is a woman who has spent her life running under false names from powerful people and knows exactly how a woman like her ends up convicted of a crime she did not commit. Her candor is total and it is fatal: it clears her of the murder, it advances the real investigation, and it hands you a person who has genuinely obstructed justice, genuinely lied about her identity, and will genuinely run the moment your back is turned. She asks, effectively, to be let go.

The machine is that the truth-teller is guilty of the wrong thing, and every response the detective has is a distortion. Arrest her, and you charge a witness for scene-tampering while the real killer is still loose — a true charge that functions as a lie about what happened. Let her walk, and you release someone who obstructed a murder investigation and will vanish under a new name, trading the case's honesty for a private mercy the badge didn't authorize. Turn her over to the people hunting her, and you feed a woman to powers worse than the law. The detective's own mind (the game's chorus of internalized voices) argues every side — pity, duty, suspicion, attraction — and none of them resolves it, because the situation is built so that the just outcome and the legal outcome and the humane outcome point three different directions. And whatever you choose, it is on you: the game does not adjudicate, it records.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** the person who finally tells you the truth is often guilty of something smaller and realer than the thing you were chasing, and honesty doesn't earn mercy or deserve punishment cleanly — it just puts the whole weight of what to do with a candid, compromised human being onto the one holding the badge, where the law, justice, and decency refuse to agree.

## 1 CAST + WHAT EACH ONE WANTS

### KLAASJE (NOT HER NAME) — the truth-teller guilty of the wrong thing
- **What she wants (stated):** to be let go; to disappear under a new name before the powers hunting her arrive.
- **What she wants (real):** to survive, as she always has — by running, by candor when candor is the only card left.
- **What she'll trade:** the whole truth of the case, in exchange for being allowed to walk.
- **What she will never say out loud:** which parts of even this confession are another survival-shaped edit.
- **Function:** THE CANDID SUSPECT: the one whose honesty clears the big crime and convicts her of the small real one.

### THE DETECTIVE (YOU) — the one holding the badge and the choice
- **What they want:** to solve the murder; to do right by a person the law and justice disagree about.
- **What they will never say out loud:** that pity, duty, and attraction are all voting, and none of them is clean.
- **Function:** THE DECIDER: the one on whom the un-adjudicated choice lands.

### THE INTERNALIZED VOICES — the chorus that argues every side and settles nothing
- The detective's skills (empathy, logic, authority, drama) each pulling a different way about Klaasje.
- **Function:** THE UN-RESOLVING JURY: the proof that the choice has no clean answer even inside one head.

### THE DEAD MAN / THE REAL KILLER — the crime her tampering obscured
- The lynched mercenary whose scene Klaasje disturbed; the actual murderer still at large.
- **Function:** THE WRONG-CHARGE RISK: the reason arresting the truth-teller would be a lie about what happened.

### THE POWERS HUNTING HER — the worse-than-law she's running from
- The people from Klaasje's past who want her, against whom the law is the lesser danger.
- **Function:** THE THIRD-DOOR HORROR: the reason turning her over is feeding a witness to something worse.

## 4 THE BRANCH MAP

**COUNT: 3 terminal calls on the truth-teller — arrest her (wrong charge), let her walk (unauthorized mercy), or turn her over (feed her to worse) — with the constant that her honesty is what makes her arrestable, and none of the three is justice; the game records the choice and refuses to adjudicate it.**

- **ARREST HER:** the truth-teller charged with tampering while the killer walks; a true charge that lies about the crime. WRITES: a conviction that isn't justice; her candor punished; the case's honesty spent on the easy body.
- **LET HER WALK:** the obstructor released under a new name; a private mercy the badge didn't authorize. WRITES: a decency outside the law; a witness gone; the detective's own conscience over the job's demand.
- **TURN HER OVER:** the witness handed to the powers hunting her; the law's lesser danger declined. WRITES: a woman fed to something worse than a cell; the case advanced at the cost of the one who broke it open.
- **THE THROUGH-LINE:** the branch is what-you-do-with-a-candid-compromised-human — three distortions of justice, and the game's chorus of inner voices refuses to resolve which is right, leaving the whole weight on the badge.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — KLAASJE'S CANDOR CAN READ AS ANOTHER MANIPULATION, WHICH DEFLATES THE DILEMMA.** Because she is written as a survivor who lies to live, a suspicious player reads even her "truth" as one more play — which is thematically rich but can collapse the dilemma into "she's conning me, arrest her," letting the player skip the actual weight by disbelieving the confession wholesale.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a truth-teller is also a known liar, the quest must give the player some ANCHOR that the core confession is real — a corroborating fact, an involuntary tell — so the dilemma survives a suspicious read instead of collapsing into "she's lying, done." A confession the player can dismiss entirely is a dilemma with an escape hatch (#177's the-wound-must-be-verifiable law).

**F2 — THE THREE OUTCOMES HAVE UNEVEN NARRATIVE WEIGHT.** Letting Klaasje walk is the most developed, emotionally supported path, while arrest and hand-over feel thinner — so the game gently steers toward mercy, and the "three distortions, none clean" balance tips toward one the game clearly prefers, softening the un-adjudicated ideal.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a choice is meant to be genuinely un-adjudicated, all outcomes must carry equal NARRATIVE weight — each given its full case and consequence — so the game isn't quietly voting. An un-adjudicated choice with one well-lit door is an opinion pretending to be neutral (#180's equal-articulation law, applied to outcomes).

**F3 — THE CONSEQUENCES OF THE CHOICE ARRIVE LATE AND FAINT.** What actually happens to Klaasje after each choice is mostly off-screen or deferred, so the weight the game puts on the detective isn't fully paid off — the decision feels heavy in the moment and then evaporates, undercutting "the choice is on you and it matters."
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest loads a heavy un-adjudicated choice onto the player, the CONSEQUENCE must return with weight — the fate of the person decided-upon seen, not deferred — so the burden the game assigned is honored. A heavy choice with a faint aftermath is a weight the game forgot to make real (#170's the-cost-must-return law).

## 6 WHY IT WORKS (W1-W10)

W1. THE TRUTH-TELLER IS GUILTY OF THE WRONG THING. Klaasje's honesty clears the murder and convicts her of tampering — the person who breaks the case open is arrestable for a smaller, realer crime.
W2. HONESTY IS FATAL, NOT REDEMPTIVE. Her candor doesn't earn mercy — it makes her the easy conviction, which is the quest's sharp inversion of "the truth sets you free."
W3. EVERY RESPONSE IS A DISTORTION. Arrest is the wrong charge, walking is unauthorized mercy, hand-over is feeding her to worse — the just, legal, and humane point three ways.
W4. THE CHORUS SETTLES NOTHING. The detective's own inner voices argue every side and resolve none — the un-adjudicated choice, proven un-resolvable even inside one head.
W5. SHE ASKS TO BE LET GO. Klaasje's plea makes the mercy a temptation and the duty a cruelty — the pull is real in both directions.
W6. THE GAME RECORDS, IT DOESN'T JUDGE. There is no "correct" outcome flagged — the choice is banked to the detective, which is the unrecorded-ledger (#26) as an interrogation.
W7. HER RUNNING IS A WHOLE LIFE. The false name, the powers hunting her, the survival-candor — Klaasje is a full person, not a puzzle, which is what makes the weight real.
W8. THE THIRD DOOR IS WORSE THAN THE LAW. Turning her over feeds her to something crueler than a cell — the choice isn't just mercy-vs-duty, it's mercy-vs-duty-vs-horror.
W9. THE WRONG CHARGE WOULD BE TRUE. Arresting her for tampering is factually correct and functionally a lie about the murder — the sharpest form of "true and unjust at once."
W10. IT IS THE LEDGER IN ONE ROOM. What you do with a candid, compromised human being, un-adjudicated — Bohemia's whole question of who the record convicts and who decides, in one interrogation.
