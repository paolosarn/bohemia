**Game:** Planescape: Torment (1999)

**Quest:** Ravel Puzzlewell — the confrontation with the night hag who made the Nameless One immortal. To recover his past he must find and face the mad, deadly, ancient witch who loved him, and answer her riddle: what can change the nature of a man — the game's whole thesis, spoken by a monster who will kill him if he answers wrong.

## 0 CORE IDEA

**To recover his lost past, an immortal amnesiac must find and face the ancient night hag who made him deathless and who loved him — a mad, murderous witch in a maze — and survive her by answering the question the whole game is built on, which she asks not to test him but because it is the only thing she has ever wanted to know.**

The Nameless One seeks the source of his immortality, and the trail leads to Ravel Puzzlewell — a night hag, ancient and insane, imprisoned in a maze of her own making, who long ago performed the ritual that made him unable to die. She is deadly; a wrong turn in the conversation ends in a fight against one of the most dangerous creatures in the game. But Ravel is not merely an obstacle. She loved the Nameless One, in her monstrous way; she is the reason he cannot die; and buried in her puzzle-conversation is the question she has spent her whole long existence turning over: "What can change the nature of a man?" It is the game's title question, its thesis, its entire subject — and Ravel asks it here, in the middle of a maze, of a man she made immortal, with her claws ready if he answers falsely.

The machine is that the game's deepest truth is spoken by a monster and gated behind engagement. Ravel does not reward the correct answer with treasure; she rewards HONESTY and thought with the truest line in the game — her own answer, offered to the player who actually converses: "Anything can change the nature of a man. What matters is that you change." A player who fights her, or skims her dialogue, or picks answers to win rather than to think, never hears it; a player who engages, who treats the mad hag's riddle as a real question about the self, receives the thematic key to the entire game from the last mouth you would expect. The buried climax is the point: the most important thing Planescape has to say is not delivered in a cutscene or by a wise mentor, but earned, in a maze, from a witch who might kill you, by the player willing to think about her question instead of surviving it.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** the truest answer to the question your whole life is asking may come from the monster who loved you, in the last place you would look, and only if you are willing to actually think about the question instead of just trying to survive it.

## 1 CAST + WHAT EACH ONE WANTS

### RAVEL PUZZLEWELL — the hag who asks the only question
- **What she wants:** to know what can change the nature of a man — the question she has turned over for centuries, and asks the Nameless One because she loved him and made him and cannot stop wondering.
- **What she'll trade:** the truth of his immortality, and the game's thematic key, to the player who ENGAGES honestly.
- **What she will never say out loud:** how much her love ruined them both; the ritual that made him deathless is the shape of her devotion.
- **Function:** THE MONSTER WHO HOLDS THE THESIS: the buried climax, the truest line spoken by the least likely mouth.

### THE NAMELESS ONE — the man seeking his own unmaking
- **What he wants:** his past, the source of his immortality, and — under it — the answer to what he is.
- **What he'll trade:** honesty, thought, engagement with a mad witch's riddle.
- **Function:** THE SEEKER whose real quest is the question, not the fact.

### RAVEL'S MAZE — the engagement gate
- The labyrinth of her making, where wrong turns in the conversation mean death.
- **Function:** THE FILTER: the maze rewards the thinker and kills the skimmer; the truth is guarded by attention.

### THE RITUAL / THE IMMORTALITY — the wound love made
- The deathlessness Ravel gave the Nameless One, the shape of her ruinous devotion.
- **Function:** THE COST of being loved by a monster: an immortality that is a curse, made in tenderness.

### THE QUESTION — the game itself, in a sentence
- "What can change the nature of a man?"
- **Function:** THE THESIS made a riddle: the whole game asked by a character, with death on the wrong answer.

## 3 THE CONVERSATIONS (THE ACTUAL MACHINE)

### NODE R-1 — Ravel's question. Entry: stage 3. The monster asking the only thing she wants to know.
The hag, mad and ancient, turning over the question of her existence.
> "Nothing can change a man's nature." [gate: none] -> she is disappointed; the conversation narrows toward the fight; the cynic's answer closes her
> "Belief can. Regret can. Love can." [gate: none] -> she leans in; the thinker's answer opens her
> "What do YOU think, Ravel?" [gate: engagement] -> THE ONE LINE DOING THE WORK, hers, the game's truest: "Anything can change the nature of a man. What matters is that you change" — the thematic key, offered to the player who asked her back
> (attack her) [gate: none] TRAP -> the fight; one of the game's deadliest, and the answer lost forever; violence is the skim-read

### NODE R-2 — the love that ruined them. Entry: stage 3-4. The wound under the riddle.
Ravel, who made him immortal out of a monster's devotion.
> "Why did you make me deathless?" [gate: none] -> because she loved him; the ritual was tenderness; the immortality is the shape of that love, and its curse
> "You ruined us both." [gate: none] -> she knows; the hag's grief, ancient and unhealed; the monster mourns
> (say nothing; hear her) [gate: none] SILENCE -> her account continues into its saddest register; the silence buys the version of her the fight would never show

### NODE R-3 — the truth or the claws. Entry: stage 5.
Ravel yields or kills.
> (engaged honestly, thought about the question) [gate: engagement] -> she gives the truth of his immortality and the thematic key; the buried climax, earned
> (skimmed, or answered to win) [gate: none] -> she withholds the depth; the player leaves with facts and no truth
> NOVERB "Make her tell me the answer" — the answer cannot be extracted, only earned by real engagement; the truth is not loot, it is a gift to the thinker. The removed verb is the quest's law: the deepest thing the game has to say rewards attention, and cannot be forced.

## 4 THE BRANCH MAP

**COUNT: 2 terminal postures — engage honestly (receive the thematic key and the truth) or skim/attack (get facts or a deadly fight, miss the truth) — gated on ENGAGEMENT, not stats.**

- **ENGAGE HONESTLY:** treat Ravel's riddle as a real question, ask her back, think — and receive her answer, the game's truest line, plus the truth of the immortality. WRITES: the thematic key earned; the buried climax reached; the whole game reframed.
- **SKIM OR ANSWER-TO-WIN:** get through the exchange for the facts without engaging — leave with information and no truth; the depth withheld. WRITES: the plot advanced; the meaning missed.
- **ATTACK:** fight Ravel, one of the game's deadliest creatures — winnable, and the answer lost forever; violence is the skim-read made lethal. WRITES: the monster dead; the truth she held gone with her.
- **THE THROUGH-LINE:** the branch is engagement-versus-extraction — the quest's whole design is that its deepest truth is guarded by whether the player thinks or just survives, and the game refuses to hand it to the skimmer.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — THE ENGAGEMENT GATE IS INVISIBLE, SO THE THINKER CAN STILL MISS IT.** The reward for honest engagement — Ravel's answer — is gated behind specific dialogue choices that a genuinely thoughtful player might not happen to pick; the "thinker gets the truth" design isn't reliable, so a player doing real reflection can leave without the key by choosing the wrong honest option.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** the reward for engagement must be reachable by ANY genuine engagement, not one specific path — the buried truth offered to the player who thinks, however they phrase it — so reflection reliably earns it. A truth gated behind exact wording punishes the thinker for not guessing the flag (the #158 unmarked-but-fair law, applied to theme).

**F2 — THE FIGHT OPTION IS TOO EASY TO STUMBLE INTO.** Ravel is deadly and a wrong answer triggers combat, but the line between "answer that opens her" and "answer that starts the fight" is not always legible — so a player can lose the entire buried climax to a misread of a mad hag's mood, which feels like a gotcha rather than a consequence of skimming.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a wrong answer forecloses the deepest content, the danger must be LEGIBLE — the player able to sense they are provoking rather than engaging — so losing the truth is a consequence of choosing not to think, not of misreading an obscure trigger. The stakes of the conversation must be readable (the #26/#160 fair-deduction law, applied to dialogue).

**F3 — RAVEL'S LOVE IS UNDERBUILT RELATIVE TO ITS WEIGHT.** The revelation that Ravel loved the Nameless One and made him immortal out of devotion is the emotional core of her tragedy, but the game delivers it in dense, easily-missed text; a player can reach the thematic key without ever feeling the ruined love underneath, so the monster's grief lands lighter than her thesis.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** a monster whose tragedy is love must have that love SHOWN with enough weight to be felt, not buried in optional density — so the creature's grief carries the same force as its thesis. The buried climax's emotion must be as reachable as its idea (the #155/#159 show-the-wound law).

## 6 WHY IT WORKS (W1-W10)

W1. THE THESIS IS SPOKEN BY A MONSTER. The truest line in the game — "anything can change the nature of a man; what matters is that you change" — comes from a mad, deadly hag in a maze, the last mouth you would expect. The buried climax's power is its source.
W2. THE TRUTH IS GATED BEHIND ENGAGEMENT. Ravel rewards thought and honesty, not stats or violence — the player who converses receives the key; the skimmer never hears it. Attention is the currency.
W3. THE QUESTION IS THE GAME. Ravel asks the title question with death on the wrong answer — the whole game's thesis made a riddle in a single conversation, stakes and meaning fused.
W4. VIOLENCE IS THE SKIM-READ. Attacking Ravel wins a fight and loses the truth forever — the design makes brute force the way to miss everything, so the player who reaches for the sword reaches past the point.
W5. THE MONSTER LOVED YOU. Ravel made the Nameless One immortal out of devotion; the curse is the shape of her love — the antagonist is a griever, and her tragedy is that her tenderness ruined them both.
W6. THE ANSWER IS A GIFT, NOT LOOT. Ravel gives her truth to the honest thinker; it cannot be extracted or forced — the deepest thing the game says is a gift to the attentive, which is why it lands.
W7. ASKING HER BACK IS THE KEY. The player who says "what do YOU think, Ravel?" — who treats her as a mind, not an obstacle — unlocks the answer; engagement is literally turning the question around.
W8. THE MAZE IS THE FILTER. Ravel's labyrinth rewards the thinker and kills the skimmer — the truth is guarded by the player's willingness to engage, made spatial.
W9. THE FIGHT IS REAL. Ravel is one of the deadliest creatures in the game; the danger is genuine, so choosing to think instead of fight is choosing to risk the honest path over the safe one.
W10. THE KEY REFRAMES EVERYTHING. Ravel's answer is the lens for the whole game's central question — reaching it changes how the player understands every prior and later beat. The buried climax pays off across the entire game.
