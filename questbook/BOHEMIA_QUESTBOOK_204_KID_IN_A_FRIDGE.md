# BOHEMIA QUESTBOOK #204 — "KID IN A FRIDGE (A CHILD SURVIVED 200 YEARS SEALED IN A BOX AND HIS PARENTS AGED A CENTURY WAITING)"

**Quest:** Kid in a Fridge — a ghoul child, sealed in a refrigerator when the bombs fell, is found still alive two centuries later, still a frightened kid, and the player must reunite him with parents who somehow also survived as ghouls and have spent lifetimes searching, while navigating a slaver who wants to buy the boy and a town that fears what he now is.

## 0 CORE IDEA

**A child who was sealed inside a refrigerator at the moment the bombs fell is discovered, impossibly, still alive two centuries later — turned ghoul by the radiation but otherwise still a frightened kid, unchanged in mind, calling for parents he last saw a lifetime of lifetimes ago — and the player must decide what to do with him: reunite him with a mother and father who also survived as ghouls and have spent the entire two hundred years searching, sell him to a slaver who offers real caps for the novelty, or leave him to a town that fears what he has become, so an absurd premise resolves into a real reckoning with time, waiting, and a love that aged into monstrousness while its object stayed a child.**

Billy Peabody was hiding in the fridge, playing, when the world ended. The lead-lined box and the radiation preserved him; the radiation also made him a ghoul. Two hundred years later the player finds him: still a kid, still scared, still asking for his mom and dad. And here the absurdity turns to ache, because his parents also became ghouls and survived, and they did not spend two centuries at peace — they spent it searching for the son who vanished into a refrigerator, aging (in spirit if not body) through lifetimes of grief, never knowing he was alive a few miles away in a box. The player holds the reunion in their hands. But it is not free: a slaver has heard about the impossible child and offers good money to buy him as a curiosity, and the town where Billy was found is uneasy about the ghoul kid in their midst. So the player must decide whether to carry Billy home to the parents who never stopped looking, sell a frightened child for caps, or abandon him — and the quest quietly prices what a reunion is worth against what a person can be sold for.

The machine is that the child stayed a child while the love that waited for him aged into something monstrous, and time is the real horror, not the fridge. The premise is a joke until you follow it: a kid untouched by two hundred years, and a mother and father who lived every one of those years without him, searching. Billy is exactly who he was; his parents are what two centuries of unresolved grief makes of people. The reunion is joyous and unbearable at once — the boy gets his parents back in an afternoon; the parents get back a son they mourned for longer than any human should have to mourn anything, unchanged, as if no time passed, when for them all the time passed. And the slaver's offer is the quest's blade: it converts this into a transaction, forcing the player to hold a real number against a real reunion, to feel what it means that a frightened child preserved by accident is, to someone, merchandise. The player can honor the waiting (carry him home), betray it for cash (sell the kid), or fail it through neglect (leave him). And the honesty under the gimmick is that waiting does not preserve the ones who wait the way the fridge preserved Billy — the child was saved by time stopping; the parents were ruined by it continuing.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** the ones who wait are not preserved the way the thing they wait for sometimes is — a child can be saved by time stopping while the love that searches for him is aged and ruined by time continuing, and the cruelty of a long-delayed reunion is that one side stayed exactly who they were while the other spent lifetimes becoming grief; and there is always someone ready to price a person's return, to turn even a miracle into merchandise.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — THE COMIC PREMISE CAN SWALLOW THE ACHE, SO THE TIME-HORROR NEVER LANDS.** "Kid survives in a fridge" is played partly for absurdity, and the joke can so dominate that the real gut-punch (a love that aged two centuries searching) passes by — the quest risks being remembered as a gag rather than a meditation on waiting and time.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest hides an ache under a comic premise, one beat must let the TIME-HORROR through — the parents' aged grief, the gap between the preserved and the ruined, made felt — so the ache reaches the player who came for the joke (#185's the-comedy-must-crack law).

**F2 — THE PARENTS' 200 YEARS OF SEARCHING IS TOLD, NOT FELT.** The devastating idea (they spent lifetimes looking) is delivered as backstory rather than shown, so the asymmetry (child unchanged, love aged) stays abstract — the reunion would cut far deeper if the player glimpsed the weight of the parents' long grief before Billy is handed back.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest's ache is a long, asymmetric waiting, the WAITERS' toll must be shown, not summarized — the years of grief made present before the reunion — so the asymmetry lands as devastation, not exposition (#187's the-waiting-must-be-shown law).

**F3 — THE SLAVER OPTION IS SO CLEARLY EVIL IT ISN'T A REAL TEMPTATION.** Selling a frightened child is cartoonishly wrong, so the "price a miracle" blade doesn't cut — a genuine test would make the caps matter (a desperate player, a real need) so the transaction is a temptation, not an obvious villainy to reflexively refuse.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest prices a person to test the player, the price must be a real TEMPTATION — the need for it made plausible — so refusing costs something, rather than being a reflexive no to an obvious evil. A transaction no one would take isn't a test, it's set dressing (#180's the-temptation-must-tempt law).

W1. THE CHILD STAYED, THE LOVE AGED. Billy is unchanged; his parents spent two centuries becoming grief — the asymmetry of time, which is the whole engine.
W2. TIME IS THE HORROR, NOT THE FRIDGE. The survival is a gimmick; the two hundred years of parental waiting it implies is the ache.

W4. THE REUNION IS JOYOUS AND UNBEARABLE. The boy gets his parents in an afternoon; the parents get a son they mourned for lifetimes, unchanged — both at once.
W5. THE MIRACLE GETS PRICED. The slaver converts the reunion into a number — the blade that forces the player to hold cash against a homecoming.
W6. THE ABSURD OPENS INTO THE REAL. A joke premise resolves into a meditation on waiting, time, and grief — the gimmick as a door.
W7. HE ASKS FOR A HOME THAT'S GONE. Billy wants a world two centuries dead — the child's need is for something time erased.
W8. THE WAITING CAN BE HONORED, SOLD, OR FAILED. Three fates for the reunion — carry, sell, or abandon; none un-spends the years.
W9. THE TOWN FEARS WHAT HE BECAME. The ghoul child unsettles the settlement — even the miracle is met with discomfort, another way to fail him.
W10. IT IS TIME-AND-WAITING IN ONE FRIDGE. A child preserved while love was ruined by the years — Bohemia's whole fold question of time, grief, and reunion, in an absurd box.
