# BOHEMIA QUESTBOOK #217 — "CUNO (A FERAL CHILD PERFORMS INVULNERABILITY BECAUSE THE ALTERNATIVE IS ADMITTING NO ONE IS COMING)"
**Game:** Disco Elysium (2019)
**Studio:** ZA/UM
**Quest:** the Cuno encounters — a foul-mouthed, drug-addled, aggressively hostile street kid who torments the investigation turns out to be a neglected child whose father is a passed-out addict, whose vicious armor of not-caring is the only thing protecting him from the unbearable fact that no adult has ever come for him, and the player can try to reach the child under the performance or let the armor win.
**Type:** INDIVIDUAL-QUEST DEEP DIVE (mined fresh 7/18 from the Disco Elysium catalogue; corpus anchor Q11 for the game's larger machines)
**FORMAT:** v2 — cast + conversation node trees + branch map.
**Filed:** 7/18/26
**Why pulled:** Bohemia needs the quest where the most annoying, hostile, dismissible character is a wounded child performing invulnerability, whose cruelty is armor over abandonment, and where the player's choice is whether to see the kid under the act or take the act at face value and write him off. Cuno is the specimen: a feral, abusive street child, a neglectful addict father, and a performance of not-caring that is the only defense against the fact that no one is coming. It is the fold's the-armor-is-the-wound machine (#162) fused with the child-nobody-came-for engine, and the "the ones who perform not-needing anyone need it most" law our ambient life can carry.

## 0 CORE IDEA

**A foul-mouthed, drug-addled, aggressively hostile street child spends the investigation hurling abuse, throwing rocks, lying, and performing a swaggering invulnerability so obnoxious that the easiest thing in the world is to write him off as a little monster — but the truth under the performance is that he is a badly neglected kid whose only parent is an addict passed out in the next room, who has never had an adult reliably come for him, and whose vicious armor of not-caring, not-needing, and not-hurting is the single fragile thing protecting him from the unbearable fact that no one is coming and no one ever has, and the player can either take the performance at face value and dismiss him, or do the harder work of seeing and reaching the child underneath.**

Cuno is instantly, deliberately unlikeable. He curses the detective, mocks the dead, brags about drugs, lies constantly, and radiates a hostility calibrated to make everyone keep their distance. He is the character players most want to ignore or shut down. And that is exactly the point: the hostility is not the truth of him, it is the defense of him. Cuno is a child — young, small, poor — whose father is a barely-conscious addict incapable of caring for him, who has grown up with the specific wound of a kid no adult ever reliably showed up for. His swaggering invulnerability, his elaborate performance of not caring about anything, is the armor a neglected child builds when caring has only ever led to being let down: if he needs no one, no one can fail him; if nothing hurts him, the fact that no one comes cannot hurt him. The player, moving through the district, can engage him at the level of his performance — trade insults, threaten him, dismiss him as a delinquent — or can catch the cracks in the armor, the moments the child shows through, and try, gently and without any guarantee of success, to reach the kid who is performing not-needing-anyone precisely because he needs someone most.

The machine is that the armor IS the wound, and the more aggressively someone performs invulnerability the more certainly they are protecting an unbearable hurt — so the most dismissible person is often the most wounded, and seeing them requires refusing to take the performance at face value. Cuno is designed to be written off, and the quest makes writing him off easy and even satisfying — he earns it, moment to moment. But the game quietly indicts that reflex. The player who takes the hostility as the truth misses the child; the player who reads the armor as armor sees a neglected kid whose entire personality is a fortress around the fact that no one is coming. The reaching is not guaranteed to work — Cuno is not a puzzle that unlocks a grateful hug; the armor is thick, earned, and load-bearing, and the child may not be reachable at all. But the choice to try, to see the wound under the performance, is the whole moral test the character presents. And the honesty is that the people who most loudly perform not needing anyone are usually the ones who need it most, that a child's cruelty is often the shape of a child's abandonment, and that the easiest person to give up on is frequently the one for whom your giving up confirms everything they've armored themselves against.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** the more aggressively someone performs that they need no one and nothing can hurt them, the more certainly they are armoring an unbearable wound — the loudest not-caring is the deepest hurt, and a child's cruelty is often just the shape of a child's abandonment; the most dismissible, most-earned-your-contempt person is frequently the one who most needs someone to refuse the performance and see the kid underneath, and giving up on them is exactly the confirmation their armor was built to expect.

## 1 CAST + WHAT EACH ONE WANTS

### CUNO — the child performing invulnerability
- **What he wants (stated):** to be left alone; to be feared; to be the toughest, hardest, least-needing thing around.
- **What he wants (real):** for an adult to come for him — the one thing his whole performance denies needing.
- **What he'll never admit:** that the not-caring is a fortress around the fact that no one is coming.
- **Function:** THE ARMOR THAT IS THE WOUND: the neglected child whose cruelty defends an unbearable abandonment.

### THE FATHER (CUNO SR.) — the parent passed out in the next room
- An addict incapable of caring for his son; the absence that built the armor.
- **Function:** THE ONE WHO NEVER CAME: the neglect that is the wound under Cuno's performance.

### THE PLAYER (the detective) — the one who sees or dismisses
- **What they want:** to get through the district; and to decide whether to take Cuno's act at face value or reach under it.
- **Function:** THE ONE WHO REFUSES THE PERFORMANCE OR DOESN'T: the adult who sees the child or writes him off.

### THE PERFORMANCE — the hostility that earns dismissal
- Cuno's abuse, lies, swagger; the armor calibrated to keep everyone at distance and make writing him off easy.
- **Function:** THE FORTRESS: the not-caring that is designed to be taken at face value and to hide the wound.

### THE UNREACHABLE POSSIBILITY — the armor that may not open
- The real chance that Cuno cannot be reached; the wound too defended, the child too armored.
- **Function:** THE NO-GUARANTEE: the honest fact that seeing the wound doesn't promise you can heal it.

## 4 THE BRANCH MAP

**COUNT: 2 postures the player can take — refuse the performance and try to reach the child (the harder work, the wound seen, no guarantee it opens the armor) or take the act at face value and write him off (the easy dismissal, confirming everything the armor expected) — with the constant that the hostility is armor over abandonment, the loudest not-caring is the deepest wound, and reaching is real but not guaranteed.**

- **REFUSE THE PERFORMANCE:** the player sees the child under the armor and tries, gently, to reach him; the wound acknowledged. WRITES: the neglected kid seen; a real reaching offered; the armor met as armor, though it may not open.
- **WRITE HIM OFF:** the player takes the hostility as the truth and dismisses him; the performance believed. WRITES: the easy contempt earned and given; the child unseen; the giving-up that confirms no one comes.
- **THE ARMOR-IS-THE-WOUND CONSTANT:** in both, Cuno's cruelty is the shape of his abandonment; the performance is designed to be dismissed, and dismissing it is exactly what it was built to expect.
- **THE THROUGH-LINE:** the branch is see-him-or-write-him-off — refuse the performance or take it at face value, and the quest insists the loudest not-caring is the deepest wound, a child's cruelty is often a child's abandonment, and giving up confirms the armor was right.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — THE PERFORMANCE IS SO EFFECTIVE MANY PLAYERS NEVER LOOK UNDER IT.** Cuno is written to be genuinely repellent, which means the "write him off" reflex is strong and satisfying, and many players will take the act at face value and never discover the wounded child — so the profound reversal (the armor is the wound) reaches only players already inclined to look, which is both the point and a reach problem.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a character's hostility is armor the player is meant to see under, the CRACKS must be visible enough that looking is invited, not only rewarded for the already-gentle — a glimpse of the child offered even to the dismissive player — so the reversal is reachable, not gated behind pre-existing empathy (#202's the-value-must-be-signaled law).

**F2 — REACHING CUNO CAN READ AS A PUZZLE THAT "SOLVES" HIM, BETRAYING THE NO-GUARANTEE.** If seeing the child unlocks a clean redemption (grateful kid, healed in a scene), the honest truth (the armor may not open, one kindness doesn't undo neglect) is lost — the power depends on the reaching being real but uncertain, not a lock that pops.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest reaches a deeply armored person, the reaching must stay UNCERTAIN — a real offer that may not land, not a puzzle that solves them — so "one kindness doesn't heal a childhood" holds and the child isn't a lock to pick (#186's the-wound-doesn't-pop-open law).

**F3 — THE FATHER'S NEGLECT CAN BE PLAYED FOR DISGUST RATHER THAN AS THE WOUND'S SOURCE.** Cuno Sr. is grotesque, and if the game leans into revulsion at the addict father, the player's contempt can extend to the whole situation rather than landing as understanding of what built Cuno's armor — the neglect needs to read as the son's wound, not just a repellent tableau.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a child's armor is built by a parent's neglect, that neglect must be framed as the WOUND'S SOURCE, not just a disgusting scene — the connection to the child's defense made legible — so the player understands the armor rather than recoiling from the whole picture (#217-adjacent: the-neglect-must-explain-the-armor law; cf. #162).

W8. HE'S A CHILD. Young, small, poor — the cruelty is a kid's, which makes taking it at face value a kind of failure of the adult, not the child.
W9. THE FATHER IS THE ABSENCE. The passed-out addict is the wound's source — the neglect that made the fortress.
W10. IT IS THE-ARMOR-IS-THE-WOUND IN ONE STREET KID. A neglected child performing not-needing-anyone — Bohemia's whole question of the hostile-seeming who most need seeing, in the most dismissible character on the map.
