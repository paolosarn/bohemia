## 0 CORE IDEA

**Beneath a gleaming, tiered city whose wealthy upper levels never look down, the outcast and impoverished are confined to a disease-ridden undercity where a plague turns the infected into feral, mindless monsters that prey on the trapped population — and a scientist living among them has developed a serum that cures the plague, but intends to keep it scarce and sell it for profit rather than distribute it to the dying who cannot pay, so the player, moving through this buried misery, must decide what to do with a cure that exists: purchase it on the maker's terms, take it by force or persuasion, or ensure it reaches the poor who need it and cannot afford it, weighing whether a life-saving remedy is a commodity to be sold or a right to be given.**

Taris is a world of tiers: the rich live in shining towers, the middle in the streets, and the poor and outcast are exiled to the Undercity, a lightless slum beneath everything, cut off, forgotten, and ravaged by the rakghoul plague — a disease that transforms its victims into feral monsters, turning the sick into a threat to the well and making the undercity a place of both poverty and horror. The upper city does not care; the poor are out of sight and out of mind. In this misery, a scientist has done something remarkable: developed a working serum that cures the plague. But the cure, in his hands, is a business. He guards the formula, doles out doses for payment, and treats a remedy for mass suffering as a scarce commodity to be monetized. The player, needing to move through or out of the undercity, encounters both the plague and the cure, and gains the power to determine the serum's fate: pay the maker's price and take a dose for themselves, leaving the system intact; seize or extract the cure by force or cunning; or act to get the serum to the dying poor who cannot pay, breaking the maker's monopoly on a remedy that could end the suffering. The question the quest poses is whether a cure belongs to the one who made it, to be sold, or to the suffering who need it, to be given.

The machine is that a cure for mass suffering exists and is hoarded from the poor for profit, so the player must decide whether a life-saving remedy is a commodity or a right — and the shining city above makes the buried plague a matter of who is allowed to be seen. The rakghoul serum crystallizes a permanent injustice: the remedy for a great suffering is not absent but withheld, available to those who can pay and denied to those who can't, in a world engineered to keep the sufferers invisible. The maker is not wrong that he made it and could profit; the poor are not wrong that they are dying while a cure sits in a locked case a few feet away. The player's choice is a small enactment of a vast question — does a cure belong to its owner or its need — and the tiered city is the frame that makes it sharp: the plague festers precisely where the powerful have arranged not to look, and the cure's scarcity is a choice, not a fact. The player can uphold the commodity (pay, leave the system), break it (free the cure to the poor), or something between, and the quest lets the buried misery be either perpetuated or interrupted by a single decision about who a remedy is for. And the honesty is that the worst suffering is often not the absence of a solution but the hoarding of one, that a shining surface is usually built on a hidden underclass it has arranged not to see, and that whether a cure is sold or given is a decision about whose lives count.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** the worst suffering is often not the absence of a solution but the hoarding of one — a cure that exists and is withheld from the poor for profit is a choice, not a fact, and a shining surface is usually built on a buried underclass it has arranged not to see; whether a life-saving remedy is a commodity to be sold or a right to be given is a decision about whose lives count, and the medicine sitting in a locked case a few feet from the dying is the sharpest form of an injustice that is engineered, not inevitable.

## 1 CAST + WHAT EACH ONE WANTS

### THE SCIENTIST (THE SERUM-MAKER) — the one who'd sell the cure
- **What he wants:** to profit from the serum; to keep it scarce; to be paid for what he made.
- **What he holds:** a working cure for a mass plague, doled out for payment, hoarded from those who can't pay.
- **Function:** THE CURE-AS-COMMODITY: the maker who treats a remedy for suffering as a business.

### THE UNDERCITY POOR — the dying who cannot pay
- The outcast, impoverished victims of the plague, trapped beneath the city, denied the cure they need.
- **Function:** THE PRICED-OUT SUFFERERS: the dying to whom the cure is a right, not a commodity.

### THE PLAGUE (THE RAKGHOUL DISEASE) — the suffering the cure could end
- The disease that turns the infected into feral monsters; poverty and horror fused.
- **Function:** THE ENGINEERED MISERY: the suffering that persists because the cure is withheld, not absent.

### THE PLAYER — the one who decides who the cure is for
- **What they want:** to move through the undercity; and to decide the serum's fate.
- **Function:** THE ARBITER OF THE REMEDY: the one who upholds the commodity, breaks it, or frees the cure.

### THE TIERED CITY — the shining surface over the buried plague
- The wealthy towers above, arranged not to see the diseased slum below.
- **Function:** THE ARRANGED BLINDNESS: the surface that keeps the sufferers invisible and the injustice comfortable.

- **UPHOLD THE COMMODITY:** the player pays the maker's price and takes a dose; the profit-model left intact; the poor still dying. WRITES: the cure kept a commodity; the hoarding preserved; the buried misery perpetuated by a decision to leave the system.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — THE CURE CAN BECOME A FETCH ITEM, DRAINING THE MORAL WEIGHT.** If the serum is treated primarily as a quest object the player needs (to cure themselves or pass a gate), the deeper question (who a hoarded cure belongs to) can recede behind the mechanical need — the injustice becomes background to a fetch rather than the point.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest's core is a hoarded remedy, the MORAL question (commodity or right) must stay foregrounded over the fetch — the injustice made the point, not the errand — so the player reckons with who the cure is for, not just whether they got it (#196's the-question-stays-central law).

**F2 — THE MAKER CAN BE A CARTOON PROFITEER OR A STRAWMAN, TIPPING THE CHOICE.** The dilemma sharpens if the maker has a real claim (he made it, he took the risk); if he's simply a greedy villain, freeing the cure is obviously right and the "does a cure belong to its maker or its need" tension collapses into robbing a bad guy.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest weighs a maker's claim against the needy, the MAKER'S claim gets real weight — the labor and risk of making it acknowledged — so freeing the cure is a genuine moral choice, not obviously robbing a villain (#180's both-sides-must-weigh law).

**F3 — THE UNDERCLASS CAN STAY A FACELESS MASS, MAKING THE SUFFERING ABSTRACT.** The stakes cut only if the dying poor are vivid enough to weigh; if the undercity is a monster-filled dungeon rather than a community of specific sufferers, freeing the cure is abstract and the "whose lives count" weight is lost.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest's stakes are a suffering underclass, at least some of the poor must be VIVID — specific sufferers, a named dying person — so freeing the cure has faces and the injustice weighs (#190's the-many-need-a-face law).
