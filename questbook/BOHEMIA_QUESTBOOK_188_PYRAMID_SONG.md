# BOHEMIA QUESTBOOK #188 — "PYRAMID SONG (A DAY AT THE BEACH WITH SOMEONE WHO IS ABOUT TO STOP EXISTING)"
**Game:** Cyberpunk 2077 (2020)
**Studio:** CD Projekt Red
**Quest:** Judy Alvarez's "Pyramid Song" — after tearing down the abusive world she came from, Judy takes the player diving into a flooded, drowned town that used to be her home, and the whole quest is a quiet, submerged day of grief and intimacy that is really a goodbye, because the person you're falling for is deciding, in front of you, that she cannot stay in the city that keeps taking everything from her.
**Type:** INDIVIDUAL-QUEST DEEP DIVE (mined fresh 7/18 from the Cyberpunk 2077 catalogue; corpus anchor Q10 for the game's larger machines)
**FORMAT:** v2 — cast + conversation node trees + branch map.
**Filed:** 7/18/26
**Why pulled:** Bohemia needs the quest that is entirely a quiet day and entirely a goodbye — where nothing "happens" mechanically and everything happens emotionally, where the intimacy the player is being offered is inseparable from the loss it's preparing them for, and a drowned town underwater is a whole life the water took. Pyramid Song is the specimen: a dive into a submerged past, a real romance offered honestly, and a decision to leave that the player cannot argue with because it is correct. It is the fold's grief-and-departure machine, and the "the closeness and the farewell are the same scene" law our companions can carry.

## 0 CORE IDEA

**Having finally destroyed the exploitative structure that trapped her, a woman takes the player on a diving trip into a lake that drowned her hometown decades ago, and the whole quest is a slow, quiet, underwater day of vulnerability, memory, and the first real intimacy between them — which is simultaneously, and inseparably, her way of showing the player who she is right before she tells them she is leaving the city for good, because it has taken everything from her and staying would take the rest.**

Judy has spent her arc fighting the people who exploited the women she loved; by now the fight is won and it cost her almost everyone. She invites the player to a lake in the hills — under it lies the town she grew up in, flooded to build a reservoir, her childhood literally submerged. They dive. She shows the player the drowned buildings, the church, the places that made her. It is the most open she has ever been, and depending on the player's choices, the day becomes the beginning of a genuine romance — honest, tender, un-cynical, rare in this world. And it is a goodbye. Judy has decided she cannot stay in Night City. The place keeps taking the people she loves and grinding down everyone who tries to do right; she has looked at it clearly and concluded that staying is a slow death, and she is going to leave. The intimacy is not a prelude to a future; it is her showing the player the whole of herself before she goes, so that whatever this was, it was real and complete, even if it doesn't get to continue.

The machine is that the closeness and the farewell are the same scene, and the quest refuses to let the player have one without the other. Every game trains players to expect that romance is a reward that unlocks a companion, a continuation, a happy state you get to keep. Pyramid Song offers the real thing — and attaches it, honestly, to loss. Judy's decision to leave is not a problem the player can solve; it is correct. The city IS killing her; staying WOULD take the rest. The player can go with her (if the arc allows) or stay and let her go, but the quest does not let anyone pretend the intimacy comes without the goodbye. The drowned town is the image of it: a whole life underwater, beautiful and gone, that you can visit but not live in. The day is precious precisely because it is ending, and the quest's honesty is that some of the most real connections you get are the ones you get to have exactly once, offered fully by someone who is already, correctly, leaving.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** the closeness and the goodbye are often the same moment, and someone showing you the whole of themselves can be how they say farewell — the most real connections are sometimes the ones that don't get to continue, offered completely by a person who has looked clearly at what a place is doing to them and decided, correctly, that they have to go.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — THE "GO WITH HER" FANTASY CAN SOFTEN THE GOODBYE THE QUEST IS BUILT ON.** For players whose arc permits leaving together, the romance can read as a straightforward happy unlock, which quietly undoes the quest's hardest truth (intimacy attached to loss) — the goodbye becomes optional, so the profound "closeness and farewell are the same scene" idea only fully lands for players who stay.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest's truth is that a connection is inseparable from loss, even the "happy" continuation must carry the COST — what was left behind, what the leaving took — so the goodbye isn't fully erased by a reward path. A loss made optional by a happy unlock was never the quest's spine (#173's the-cost-survives-the-happy-ending law).

**F2 — THE CITY'S UN-SOLVABILITY IS ASSERTED MORE THAN FELT AT THIS MOMENT.** Judy's "the city is killing me" is true across her whole arc but, in this quiet quest, is stated rather than freshly demonstrated, so a player who hasn't tracked the cumulative toll can read her departure as sudden pessimism rather than a correct, earned conclusion — weakening "her leaving is right, not a problem to solve."
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a character's departure must read as correct rather than defeatist, the reason must be freshly PRESENT at the goodbye — the toll visible in the moment, not just assumed from earlier — so the leaving lands as clarity, not mood. A correct departure that isn't shown correct reads as giving up (#182's the-reason-must-be-present law).

**F3 — THE QUIET CAN READ AS "NOTHING HAPPENS" FOR PLAYERS TRAINED ON STAKES.** The quest is deliberately low-event — a dive, a talk, a day — and players conditioned to expect mechanical stakes can disengage, missing that the entire point is emotional, so the intimacy-as-goodbye can pass as filler for the wrong audience.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest's whole content is emotional rather than mechanical, its stillness must be FRAMED as the event — the quiet made legibly the point, not dead air — so players trained on stakes understand the day itself is the stake. A tender quest that reads as filler failed to signal what it was (#185's the-quiet-must-be-framed law).

## 6 WHY IT WORKS (W1-W10)

W1. THE CLOSENESS IS THE GOODBYE. The intimacy and the farewell are one scene — the quest's whole engine, refusing to separate the connection from the loss.
W2. THE INTIMACY IS REAL AND HONEST. Judy offers the genuine thing, un-cynical and complete — rare in this world, which is what makes the goodbye hurt.
W3. THE DEPARTURE IS CORRECT. The city is genuinely killing her; leaving is right, not a problem to solve — the removed "talk her into staying" verb is the spine.
W4. THE DROWNED TOWN IS THE IMAGE. A whole life underwater, beautiful and unlivable — the loss made a place you can dive into but not live in.
W5. IT IS THE ONCE. A real connection offered exactly once, complete because it doesn't continue — the quest's honesty about impermanent intimacy.
W6. SHOWING YOU EVERYTHING IS THE FAREWELL. Judy's total vulnerability is how she says goodbye — being fully known once, on the way out.
W7. THE QUIET IS THE EVENT. Nothing mechanical happens; everything emotional does — the stillness is the stake, which is rare and brave.
W8. LEAVING WITH HER STILL COSTS. Even the continuation is attached to what the city took — the loss present even in the happy path.
W9. THE PLAYER CAN'T FIX IT. Judy's decision isn't a puzzle; the loving act is to let the goodbye be real — the impotence that is the truth of some departures.
W10. IT IS THE FOLD'S GRIEF IN ONE DAY. A real connection that doesn't get to last, honored as complete — Bohemia's whole question of loss and departure, in a drowned town and one afternoon.
