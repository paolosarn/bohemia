# BOHEMIA QUESTBOOK #163 — "THE SILVER SHROUD (THE COSTUME THAT BECOMES REAL)"
**Game:** Fallout 4 (2015)
**Studio:** Bethesda Game Studios
**Quest:** The Silver Shroud — a Goodneighbor quest. A ghoul obsessed with a pre-war radio hero asks the player to put on the costume and BE the Silver Shroud, a vigilante playing a fictional character to real killers — and the role-play becomes a real question of who the mask makes you.

**Why pulled:** Bohemia's dynasty is a role the player performs across generations, and this is the genre's cleanest quest about a costume that becomes real — a player putting on a fictional hero's identity to answer real crimes, given custom in-character dialogue, and made to decide whether the Shroud's justice is theatre or execution. ## 0 CORE IDEA

**A grieving ghoul asks the player to become the Silver Shroud — a pre-war radio vigilante, a fictional character — and hunt real killers in his costume, and the quest's whole engine is that playing a hero to genuine murderers turns the costume into a real identity with a real body count.**

Kent Connolly, a ghoul in Goodneighbor, has spent two hundred years loving the Silver Shroud, a masked crime-fighter from pre-war radio serials. He wants the hero back — really back, in the flesh — and asks the player to put on the costume, take up the Shroud's persona, and clean up the city's worst predators. The game leans all the way in: the player is given CUSTOM in-character dialogue, delivered in the Shroud's theatrical noir register, to use on targets — you don't just fight criminals, you PERFORM the Silver Shroud at them, delivering the vigilante's grim one-liners before you act. The targets are real: killers, extortionists, the genuinely predatory. And the question the quest quietly builds is whether the Shroud is a performance that does good, or a costume that licenses the player to become an executioner and call it justice.

The machine is a fiction made flesh, and the cost of a mask that stops being pretend. Kent needs the Shroud to be real because his hope is; the player's targets die in earnest whether the one-liners are camp or menace; and when Kent himself is taken hostage by a genuine threat (Sinjin, a killer who is NOT playing), the quest's climax forces the register to resolve: does the player deliver the Shroud's theatrical justice for real, saving Kent, and does saying the hero's lines make you the hero or just a killer in a costume? The best beat is that the performance can be sincere OR cynical, and the game lets the player choose the tone — camp it up, mean it, or drop the act — and each is a different answer to whether the mask is a hero or a permission slip.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** a role you put on to do good becomes who you are when the stakes stop being pretend, and the difference between a hero and an executioner is sometimes only whether you believe the lines you're saying.

## 1 CAST + WHAT EACH ONE WANTS

### KENT CONNOLLY — the fan who needs the hero real
- **What he wants:** the Silver Shroud back — a real vigilante to make Goodneighbor safer, and a fiction he loves made flesh.
- **What he'll trade:** the costume, the scripts, the hope; he is the quest's heart and, eventually, its hostage.
- **What he will never say out loud:** how much his two-hundred-year love of the Shroud is a way of surviving what the world became.
- **Function:** THE BELIEVER: the one whose hope makes the fiction need to be real, and whose life makes it matter.

### THE PLAYER-AS-SHROUD — the costume becoming real
- **What they want:** to answer the crimes, and to decide who the mask makes them.
- **What they'll trade:** their own identity, temporarily, for the Shroud's.
- **Function:** THE PERFORMANCE that turns real: the player delivering a fiction's lines to genuine killers, choosing the register.

### THE TARGETS — the real crimes under the theatre
- Killers, extortionists, predators — genuinely bad, genuinely dying.
- **Function:** THE STAKES that make the costume more than cosplay: the Shroud's justice has a body count.

### SINJIN — the killer who isn't playing
- **What he wants:** to break Kent and mock the Shroud's fantasy; a real threat, no costume.
- **Function:** THE CLIMAX: the genuine menace that forces the performance to resolve into real justice or real failure.

### THE SILVER SHROUD (the fiction) — the mask itself
- The pre-war radio hero, his register, his lines.
- **Function:** THE IDENTITY on offer: a role the player inhabits, that inhabits the player back.

## 4 THE BRANCH MAP

**COUNT: 1 core outcome (be the Shroud, answer the crimes) times the REGISTER axis (sincere / camp / cynical) times whether Kent survives Sinjin — the branch is tonal and moral, not mechanical.**

- **THE SHROUD, SINCERE:** the player believes the lines; the mask becomes a real hero; Goodneighbor gets a vigilante who means it. WRITES: the fiction made flesh; a hero by choice.
- **THE SHROUD, CAMP:** the player performs ironically; the killings are real, the belief is not; a body count in a costume. WRITES: justice as theatre; the mask as a permission slip.
- **THE SHROUD, DROPPED:** no performance, just the job; the costume without the identity. WRITES: an executioner who didn't bother with the lines.
- **KENT LIVES / DIES:** the climax measures the chosen register against a real life — the believer saved or lost by whether the mask held when it mattered.
- **THE THROUGH-LINE:** every path answers "who does the mask make you" — the branches are three registers of the same real justice, and the game refuses to tell you which is heroic.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — THE MORAL QUESTION ISN'T MECHANICALLY WEIGHTED.** The sincere/camp/cynical registers are pure flavor — the killings, the outcomes, and Kent's fate don't meaningfully differ by which tone the player picks, so the profound "hero or executioner" question is a costume the game never actually charges the player for wearing.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a role's REGISTER is a moral question, it must have mechanical or consequential weight — the sincere hero and the cynical executioner should reap different worlds (how the district sees the mask, what the role costs the player) — or the question is decoration. A tone with no consequence is not a choice (the #33 agency-twist law: performance must matter).

**F2 — THE COSTUME NEVER COSTS THE PLAYER THEIR OWN IDENTITY.** The player puts on the Shroud and takes it off freely; there is no beat where the mask threatens to REPLACE them, no cost to inhabiting a fiction. The "role becomes real" theme is stated but the player is never actually in danger of losing themselves to it.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a role can become real, inhabiting it must RISK the self — a beat where the mask starts speaking for the player, where taking it off is harder than putting it on — so the theme is felt, not just narrated. The dynasty-as-role should be able to consume the person (the #32/#35 identity-loss law).

**F3 — KENT'S GRIEF IS BACKSTORY, NOT PRESENCE.** Kent's two-hundred-year love of the Shroud, his survival-through-fiction, is the quest's emotional core but is delivered mostly in a few lines; the player experiences him as a quest-giver more than a person whose hope they're carrying, so the hostage climax lands lighter than the setup earns.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** the believer whose hope the player is asked to embody must be a PRESENT person — witnessed, known, their stake felt — so that becoming their hero and risking their life carries weight. The fold builds the believer the mask is for (#158's specific-person law).

## 6 WHY IT WORKS (W1-W10)

W1. THE COSTUME BECOMES REAL. A player puts on a fictional hero to answer genuine crimes; the mask accrues a real body count. The fiction-made-flesh is the quest's whole engine and it lands.
W2. THE PLAYER PERFORMS THE HERO. Custom in-character dialogue lets the player DELIVER the Shroud's lines at targets — you don't just fight, you act, which makes the role-play literal and the identity question real.
W3. THE REGISTER IS THE PLAYER'S. Sincere, camp, or cynical — the game lets the player choose the tone of their justice, so the mask means what the player decides it means.
W4. THE TARGETS ARE REAL. The Shroud's justice kills genuine predators; the theatre has a body count, so the costume is never just cosplay.
W5. THE BELIEVER NEEDS THE HERO REAL. Kent's two-hundred-year hope makes the fiction NEED to become flesh — the emotional stakes are a man's survival-through-story.
W6. THE CLIMAX MEETS THE REAL. Sinjin, a killer who isn't playing, forces the performance to resolve — theatre confronted by genuine menace, with a life on it.
W7. THE HERO WAS A VIGILANTE. The Shroud's justice is execution; playing him means killing, so the role-play carries an edge — the fiction the fan loves is a license to kill dressed as heroism.
W8. THE MASK ASKS WHO YOU ARE. The quest's quiet question — hero or executioner — is Bohemia's exact question about the dynasty-as-role, made playable.
W9. THE TONE IS A RELEASE VALVE WITH A BLADE. The camp register relieves; the real killings sharpen — the quest swings between fun and grim, and the swing is the point.
W10. THE FICTION SURVIVES THE PLAYER. Kent's hope, if the player honors it, outlives the quest — a fiction made real by someone willing to wear it, which is the most Bohemian idea in the game.
