## 0 CORE IDEA

**At a derelict rocket test facility, a small congregation of ghouls led by a charismatic preacher believes that the old rockets can carry them off the ruined earth to a promised paradise among the stars — the "Far Beyond" — and they have spent years preparing for a launch that the laws of the world guarantee will kill them; a rival group of hostile ghouls has overrun part of the site, and the player is drawn in to help ready the rockets, and in doing so gains the power to fuel the doomed dream, ground it to save their lives, or reveal that the heaven they aim for may be nothing at all.**

Jason Bright leads the Bright Brotherhood, ghouls who have organized their whole existence around a coming exodus: they will board the launch-ready rockets and ascend to the Far Beyond, a paradise Jason preaches with total conviction. The rockets are real, ancient, and — with work — can actually be made to launch. But everyone who understands physics knows what "launch" means for a rocket aimed at nowhere with a hold full of living passengers: it means dying in the sky, or in the vacuum, or in the cold, far from any paradise. A faction of feral-tending ghouls has infested the site and threatens the Brotherhood. The player is recruited to clear them and ready the launch. That work hands the player the dream's throttle. They can complete the rockets and send the Brotherhood off in a glorious, faithful, fatal ascent toward a heaven no one can confirm exists; they can quietly sabotage or delay the launch to keep them earthbound and alive but faithless; or they can confront Jason with the truth — that the Far Beyond may be empty, that the launch is a death, that the faith is aimed at vacuum.

The machine is that the player holds the throttle of a beautiful, doomed faith aimed at a heaven that cannot be confirmed, and every option is a different kind of cruelty or mercy. Fueling the launch honors the Brotherhood's belief and their agency — they want this, they chose it, and sending them lets them die inside their faith rather than outside it — but it is also helping people fly to their deaths on the strength of a maybe-lie. Grounding them keeps them alive at the cost of the only thing they live for — a safety that is also a robbery. Telling the truth respects them as adults capable of facts but may shatter the one structure holding a community of the discarded together, and buys nothing but honesty. And the game, characteristically, does not tell the player whether the Far Beyond is real — Jason's faith is total, the physics is grim, and the transcendence is unfalsifiable. The player decides where a whole congregation's faith goes with no confirmation of what waits there, which is exactly the position every real question of belief puts a person in: you cannot check the heaven before you decide whether to fly toward it.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** faith is aimed at a place you cannot verify before you commit to it, and deciding whether to fuel someone's beautiful doomed hope, ground it to keep them safe, or confront it with a truth you also can't fully prove is a choice between kinds of love and kinds of cruelty — there is no option that is purely kind, because you never get to see the Far Beyond before you send someone toward it.

## 1 CAST + WHAT EACH ONE WANTS

### JASON BRIGHT — the preacher of the Far Beyond
- **What he wants (stated):** to lead his flock to paradise among the stars; to launch.
- **What he wants (real):** to give the discarded a destination — a heaven for those the world threw away.
- **What he'll trade:** the safety of staying, for the transcendence of going.
- **What he cannot confirm:** that the Far Beyond is anything but vacuum.
- **Function:** THE FAITH AIMED AT THE UNVERIFIABLE: the preacher whose paradise cannot be checked before the flight.

### THE BRIGHT BROTHERHOOD — the congregation of the discarded
- Ghouls who organized their whole existence around the coming exodus; a community held by the dream.
- **Function:** THE BELIEVERS: the ones whose lives and coherence ride on a launch that may be a death.

### THE HOSTILE GHOULS — the threat at the site
- Feral-tending ghouls infesting the rockets, endangering the launch and the Brotherhood.
- **Function:** THE OBSTACLE: the danger that draws the player in and hands them the dream's throttle.

### THE PLAYER (the Courier) — the one holding the throttle
- **What they want:** to help; and to decide, with no confirmation, where a faith should go.
- **Function:** THE THROTTLE-HOLDER: the one who fuels, grounds, or exposes the doomed dream.

### THE FAR BEYOND — the heaven that cannot be checked
- The promised paradise; transcendence or vacuum, unfalsifiable either way.
- **Function:** THE UNVERIFIABLE DESTINATION: the thing the whole choice aims at and no one can confirm.

## 4 THE BRANCH MAP

**COUNT: 3 terminal outcomes — fuel the launch (the Brotherhood ascends toward the unverifiable heaven, dying inside their faith), ground it (the flock kept alive and robbed of their destination), or expose the truth (the faith confronted with possible vacuum) — with the constant that the Far Beyond can never be confirmed before the choice, so no option is purely kind.**

- **FUEL THE LAUNCH:** the Brotherhood ascends in a glorious fatal flight; agency and faith honored; a death inside the dream. WRITES: believers sent toward a heaven no one confirmed; a beautiful doomed exodus; mercy-as-fatality.
- **GROUND THE LAUNCH:** the flock kept alive and earthbound; the destination stolen; safety without the thing they lived for. WRITES: lives saved, faith robbed; a community intact and purposeless; cruelty-as-protection.
- **TELL THE TRUTH:** Jason and the flock confronted with the possible vacuum; respected as adults; the structure maybe shattered. WRITES: honesty that buys nothing; faith tested against a fact no one can fully prove; the discarded's coherence risked.
- **THE UNVERIFIABLE CONSTANT:** in all branches, no one can check the Far Beyond first — the player decides where a faith goes blind to what waits there, which is the real condition of every belief.
- **THE THROUGH-LINE:** the branch is fuel-ground-or-expose — three kinds of love and cruelty toward a doomed hope, and the quest refuses to reveal the heaven, insisting the choice is always made without seeing the destination.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — THE GAME'S MECHANICAL REWARD NUDGES TOWARD FUELING THE LAUNCH, FLATTENING THE DILEMMA.** Completing the launch is the "main" completion with the cleanest payoff, so the profound three-way (fuel/ground/expose) can collapse into "do the quest as designed" — the grounding and truth paths feel like off-menu deviations rather than equally weighted mercies, tipping a genuine dilemma toward the intended ending.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest offers fuel-ground-or-expose for a doomed faith, all three must be equally SUPPORTED as real endings, not one main path with two deviations — each given its own resolution and dignity — so the dilemma isn't secretly a single intended answer (#181's equal-outcomes law).

**F2 — THE FAR BEYOND'S UNVERIFIABILITY CAN READ AS THE GAME DODGING, NOT AS DESIGNED AMBIGUITY.** Because the game never confirms whether paradise exists, a player can experience the ambiguity as the writers refusing to commit rather than as the point (faith is aimed at the uncheckable) — so the strongest idea risks reading as a narrative cop-out instead of an intentional epistemic mirror.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest's power is that a destination is unverifiable, the ambiguity must be FRAMED as the condition, not a gap — the un-checkability made legibly the point of the choice — so it reads as "you can't know and must choose anyway," not "the writers wouldn't say" (#182's the-ambiguity-must-be-owned law).

**F3 — THE BROTHERHOOD IS UNDERCHARACTERIZED, SO THE STAKES ARE ABSTRACT.** The individual ghouls of the flock are mostly a faceless congregation, so the weight of sending "them" to die or robbing "them" of their dream is diffuse — the choice would cut deeper if the player knew even one believer as a person whose specific hope is on the rocket.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a choice decides the fate of a congregation, at least one believer must be KNOWN as a person — a specific hope on the line — so the stakes aren't an abstract crowd. A faith with no face is a number, not a flock (#180's the-many-need-a-face law).

## 6 WHY IT WORKS (W1-W10)

W1. THE HEAVEN CAN'T BE CHECKED. The Far Beyond is unverifiable — transcendence or vacuum, unfalsifiable — so the player decides where a faith goes blind to what waits, which is the whole engine.
W2. THE PLAYER HOLDS THE THROTTLE. Readying the launch hands the dream's control to the player — the choice is theirs, not the story's.
W3. EVERY OPTION IS LOVE AND CRUELTY. Fuel is mercy-as-death; ground is protection-as-robbery; truth is respect-as-shattering — no purely kind path.
W4. THE FAITH IS A COMMUNITY OF THE DISCARDED. The Brotherhood are the world's thrown-away, held together by the dream — grounding them robs the only thing they have.
W5. THE LAUNCH IS REAL. The rockets can actually fly — the dream is achievable and fatal, not a delusion that fizzles, which makes fueling it a genuine act.
W6. THE DESTINATION STAYS UNSEEN. The removed "check the Far Beyond first" verb is the spine: faith is aimed at what you can't verify before you commit.
W7. AGENCY VERSUS SAFETY. The Brotherhood want to go; honoring that means helping them die — the tension between respecting a choice and preventing a death.
W8. THE PREACHER'S FAITH IS TOTAL. Jason never wavers — the conviction is complete, which makes the truth-path feel like an assault on something load-bearing.
W9. THE GAME WON'T RESOLVE IT. No confirmation of paradise — the ambiguity is the mirror: you're in the same position as any believer, deciding without proof.
W10. IT IS THE PROMISED-AFTERLIFE IN ONE LAUNCH. A heaven for the discarded that can't be confirmed, fueled or grounded or exposed — Bohemia's whole "no one is ever lost" question, aimed at the stars.
