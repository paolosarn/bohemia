**Quest:** Deionarra's ghost / the debt of a past life — the amnesiac immortal protagonist meets the ghost of a woman who loved and was killed by one of his previous incarnations, a version of himself who coldly manipulated her devotion and sent her to her death for his own ends, and the player must reckon with a grief and a betrayal committed by a self he cannot remember but is still responsible for.

## 0 CORE IDEA

**An immortal, amnesiac man who has lived and died countless times, each incarnation forgetting the last, encounters the lingering ghost of a woman who, in a life he cannot remember, loved him utterly — and was coldly, deliberately manipulated by that past version of himself, who cultivated her devotion, used her prophetic gift, and knowingly sent her to her death to serve his own hidden ends; and now she waits, still in love with a man who no longer exists, and the player must reckon with a grief and a monstrous betrayal committed by a self he has no memory of being but is nonetheless morally continuous with, answering to a victim for a crime he did not know he committed and cannot honestly deny is his.**

The Nameless One cannot die and cannot remember. Each of his incarnations lived a life and lost it, and some of those past selves were very different men — kinder, crueler, wiser, monstrous. Deionarra was a woman who loved one of them: a colder, more calculating incarnation who saw in her love and her latent prophetic power a tool. He nurtured her devotion not because he returned it but because he needed it, guided her toward a place and a fate he knew would kill her, and let her die believing in him, all to advance his own secret purposes. Her spirit lingered, bound by that love and that betrayal, waiting. When the amnesiac protagonist finds her, she recognizes him — the face, the presence, the man — and does not at first understand that the person who loved-and-used her is gone, replaced by someone who remembers none of it. The player is caught in an impossible position: he feels no love for her (he doesn't know her), committed no crime he can recall, and yet he wears the face that betrayed her, carries the continuity of the self that did it, and is the only one left for her grief and her accusation to land on. He can lie to her (pretend to be the man she loved, grant a false comfort), tell her the cruel truth (that the man who used her is gone and she died for nothing he'll ever remember), or take responsibility across the gap (accept that the crime is his even though the memory isn't, and try to give her some real release).

The machine is that moral continuity survives memory, so you are answerable for the self you were even when you cannot remember being them — and a victim's grief does not care that you've forgotten. The quest refuses the easy exits. The player cannot say "that wasn't me" and mean it, because it was him — the same continuous being, the same soul dragging its unremembered history. He cannot make it right by feeling the love he doesn't feel. And he cannot escape Deionarra's claim on him, because there is no one else: the man who wronged her exists now only as the amnesiac standing before her, and if anyone is to answer, it must be him. So the reckoning is with the specific horror of inherited guilt without inherited memory: being responsible for atrocities you'd never choose, committed by a version of you that is gone but is still, undeniably, you. The player's choices — comfort her with a lie, wound her with the truth, or shoulder the responsibility honestly and seek her a real peace — are all attempts to answer a debt he did not knowingly incur, and the honesty is that who you were does not stop being you just because you can't remember them, and the people they hurt are still owed, and the bill falls on whoever is wearing the face now.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** you are answerable for who you were even when you cannot remember being them, because moral continuity outlasts memory and the people your past self hurt are still owed — a victim's grief does not care that you've forgotten, and "that wasn't me" is a lie when it was the same continuous self; the crimes of who you were remain yours, and the debt falls on whoever wears the face now, whether or not they ever chose or can recall the wrong.

## 1 CAST + WHAT EACH ONE WANTS

### DEIONARRA — the ghost who loves a man who no longer exists
- **What she wants (stated):** her beloved; understanding; release from the love and betrayal that bind her.
- **What she doesn't know at first:** that the man who used her is gone, replaced by one who remembers nothing.
- **What she is owed:** the truth, or comfort, or release — a debt her killer left for whoever wears his face.
- **Function:** THE VICTIM OF A PAST SELF: the grief that lands on the amnesiac who inherited the crime.

### THE PAST INCARNATION — the self who betrayed her
- A colder version of the protagonist who cultivated Deionarra's love and sent her knowingly to her death.
- **Function:** THE CRIME WITHOUT THE MEMORY: the gone self whose atrocity the present self still carries.

### THE PLAYER (the Nameless One) — the one answerable across the gap
- **What they want:** to answer a debt they can't remember incurring; to face a victim of who they were.
- **What they can't do:** honestly say "that wasn't me," or feel the love they never had.
- **Function:** THE INHERITOR OF THE GUILT: the one on whom the past self's crime and the victim's grief both fall.

### THE FACE — the continuity that makes him answerable
- The shared identity across incarnations; the reason Deionarra's claim lands on the present self.
- **Function:** THE UNBROKEN THREAD: the moral continuity that survives the lost memory.

### THE THREE ANSWERS — lie, truth, or responsibility
- Comfort her as the man she loved, wound her with the truth, or shoulder the debt honestly.
- **Function:** THE ATTEMPTS TO PAY: the ways to answer a crime he did not knowingly commit.

## 4 THE BRANCH MAP

**COUNT: 3 terminal answers — comfort her with a lie (a false peace continuing her killer's deception), wound her with the truth (release through a cruel fact), or shoulder the guilt honestly (responsibility taken across the gap, a real peace sought) — with the constant that moral continuity survives memory, the crime is undeniably the player's, and there is no one else for the debt to fall on.**

- **COMFORT WITH A LIE:** the player pretends to be the man she loved; a false peace granted. WRITES: mercy built on deception; her killer's lie extended; release bought with a comforting untruth.
- **WOUND WITH THE TRUTH:** the player tells her the man is gone and she died for nothing he'll recall; a harsh honesty. WRITES: release through a cruel fact; the deception ended at the cost of comfort; the truth as a hard mercy.
- **SHOULDER THE GUILT:** the player accepts the crime as his across the amnesia and seeks Deionarra a genuine release. WRITES: responsibility taken without the memory; the debt of who he was honored; a real peace attempted.
- **THE CONTINUITY-SURVIVES-MEMORY CONSTANT:** in every answer, the crime is the player's, forgetting doesn't sever it, and the victim's grief falls on whoever wears the face — "that wasn't me" is never honestly available.
- **THE THROUGH-LINE:** the branch is lie-truth-or-responsibility — three ways to answer a debt you don't remember incurring, and the quest insists the crimes of who you were remain yours, and the bill falls on whoever is wearing the face now.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — THE AMNESIA CAN BECOME AN EXCUSE THE QUEST DOESN'T FULLY CLOSE.** The whole power is that "that wasn't me" is a lie, but if the game lets the player disclaim the past self too easily or treats the forgetting as genuinely exculpatory, the profound "moral continuity survives memory" idea leaks — the player needs to be denied the clean out for the reckoning to land.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest's point is that you're answerable for a self you don't remember, the amnesia must be DENIED as an excuse — the continuity made undeniable, "that wasn't me" shown as false — so the reckoning can't be dodged by disclaiming the past self (#26's the-forgotten-self-is-still-you law).

**F2 — DEIONARRA'S LOVE CAN READ AS A PUZZLE TO SOLVE RATHER THAN A GRIEF TO HONOR.** If the encounter is structured as an information/dialogue puzzle (find the right lines to release her ghost), the player can treat her as a mechanism rather than a person owed something — so the emotional weight (a real woman betrayed to death) reduces to a lock to pick.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest is answering a debt to a wronged person, the encounter must be framed as a GRIEF to honor, not a puzzle to solve — her personhood foregrounded over the release mechanic — so the player answers a debt, not a riddle (#202's the-person-is-not-a-puzzle law).

**F3 — THE "SHOULDER THE GUILT" PATH CAN LACK A DISTINCT COST FROM THE OTHER TWO.** Taking responsibility across the gap is the profound answer, but if it plays out with no more difficulty or consequence than lying or truth-telling, it reads as just a third dialogue option rather than the hard, costly choice to own a crime you didn't knowingly commit.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if the deepest answer is shouldering inherited guilt, that path must COST more — the difficulty of owning an un-chosen crime made felt — so responsibility isn't just another menu pick beside the easy comfort and the easy cruelty (#208's the-hard-answer-must-be-hard law).

## 6 WHY IT WORKS (W1-W10)

W1. CONTINUITY SURVIVES MEMORY. The player is answerable for a self he can't remember — moral continuity outlasts forgetting, which is the whole engine.
W2. THE GRIEF DOESN'T CARE YOU FORGOT. Deionarra's love and accusation land regardless of the amnesia — the victim is owed no matter what you recall.
W3. THE DISCLAIMER IS A LIE. Saying "that wasn't me" is false; it was the same continuous self — the removed "make it not have been you" verb is the spine: forgetting transfers nothing.
W4. THERE'S NO ONE ELSE TO ANSWER. The man who wronged her exists now only as the amnesiac before her — the debt falls on whoever wears the face.
W5. HE FEELS NO LOVE HE CAN OFFER. The player can't make it right with a devotion he never had — the gap is unbridgeable by feeling.
W6. THREE ANSWERS, ALL PARTIAL. Lie, truth, or responsibility — three ways to pay a debt he didn't knowingly incur, none fully clean.
W7. THE CRIME WAS COLD AND DELIBERATE. The past self cultivated her love as a tool and killed her knowingly — the wrong is monstrous, not accidental.
W8. THE LIE CONTINUES THE KILLER'S DECEPTION. Comforting her as the man she loved extends the very deception that doomed her — even mercy is complicated.
W9. RESPONSIBILITY CROSSES THE GAP. The profound answer is owning a crime without the memory of it — the hardest and truest kind of accountability.
W10. IT IS ANSWERABLE-FOR-WHO-YOU-WERE IN ONE GHOST. A victim of a self you can't remember, still owed — Bohemia's whole fold question of continuity, guilt, and the unremembered past.
