# BOHEMIA QUESTBOOK #169 — "THAT LUCKY OLD SUN (WHO GETS THE POWER)"
**Game:** Fallout: New Vegas (2010)
**Studio:** Obsidian Entertainment
**Quest:** That Lucky Old Sun — the Helios One solar plant quest. The player restarts a derelict power station and, at the console, must decide who receives the electricity: the whole region, a single fortress, the plant's cult, or a hidden superweapon — an allocation choice with no combat, just a switch and its consequences.

**FORMAT:** v2 — cast + conversation node trees + branch map.
**Filed:** 7/17/26
**Why pulled:** Bohemia IS a survival city-builder where electricity is a currency and allocation is a moral act — and this is the genre's cleanest quest about distributing a scarce resource with a single switch and living with who you starved. That Lucky Old Sun is the specimen: a power plant, a limited output, and a console where you choose who gets the light — the many, the few, the faithful, or a weapon — with the whole region's fate in a menu. It is the Frostpunk decree (#29) and the Pathologic triage (#21) compressed into one lever, and the direct model for our power-grid allocation systems.

## 0 CORE IDEA

**A player restarts a dead solar power plant and, at the control console, decides who receives its electricity — the whole struggling region, a single powerful fortress, the plant's own resident cult, or a buried orbital superweapon — an allocation with no fight, just a switch, and a region's future in the choosing.**

Helios One is a derelict solar power station, long dark. The player is hired to bring it back online — a quest of repairs, terminals, and a resident cult (the Followers of the Apocalypse are nearby; the plant itself has been squatted by worshippers of the sun). Once the plant is working, the real quest is a single decision at the console: where does the power go? The output is finite. The player can route it to the region at large (spreading modest benefit widely — more towns lit, more people helped, the utilitarian good), to a single dominant faction's fortress (concentrating power for one strong ally, a strategic choice), to the plant's cult (rewarding the faithful who kept the flame), or — the hidden option — to reactivate ARCHIMEDES, an orbital energy weapon, turning the plant from an infrastructure into a gun.

The machine is that a scarce resource is allocated by one switch, and every setting is a different theory of who deserves the light. There is no combat in the decision, no villain to kill; there is a console and a menu and a region that will be shaped by which way the current flows. The utilitarian spread helps the most people a little; the fortress route helps one power a lot; the cult route honors loyalty over need; the weapon route abandons welfare for force. Nothing punishes any choice in the moment — the plant hums, the light goes where you sent it — and the consequence is downstream and diffuse: the towns that stayed dark, the faction that grew strong, the weapon that now exists. The quest is the purest demonstration that infrastructure is a moral act: to hold the switch for a whole region's power is to decide, quietly, who matters.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** when you hold the switch for a scarce thing everyone needs, there is no neutral setting — every allocation is a verdict on who deserves it, and the ones you leave dark never see the console you chose at.

## 1 CAST + WHAT EACH ONE WANTS

### THE REGION (the many) — the diffuse need
- **What it wants:** light, modestly, spread wide — more towns powered, more people helped a little.
- **What it'll trade:** nothing; it is the utilitarian option, the greatest good for the greatest number.
- **Function:** THE MANY: the allocation's widest, shallowest beneficiary — help everyone a little.

### THE FORTRESS (the few) — the concentrated power
- **What it wants:** the plant's full output for one dominant faction, made stronger.
- **What it'll trade:** a powerful ally's favor, for the light.
- **Function:** THE FEW: the strategic option — power concentrated, one force empowered over the region's spread.

### THE SUN CULT (the faithful) — loyalty over need
- **What they want:** the power they kept the flame for; the faithful rewarded over the needy.
- **Function:** THE DEVOTED: the option that honors who kept the plant alive over who needs its output — loyalty as a claim.

### ARCHIMEDES (the weapon) — force over welfare
- **What it wants (as an option):** to be reactivated — the plant turned from infrastructure into an orbital gun.
- **Function:** THE HIDDEN LEVER: the choice to make power a weapon instead of a service — welfare abandoned for force.

### THE CONSOLE — the switch that is a verdict
- The control terminal where the allocation is made, no combat, just a menu.
- **Function:** THE MORAL ACT made a lever: infrastructure as a decision about who deserves the light.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — THE CONSEQUENCES ARE TOO DIFFUSE TO FEEL.** The allocation's downstream effects — which towns benefit, how a faction is strengthened — are so diffuse and under-shown that the player rarely FEELS the weight of who they lit or left dark; the choice is morally rich in concept but nearly weightless in play, because the region's response is barely dramatized.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** an allocation choice must have LEGIBLE, felt consequences — the lit town seen thriving, the dark one seen suffering, named people on both sides — so the switch's moral weight lands. A resource decision whose outcomes are invisible is a menu, not a verdict (the #29 Frostpunk faces-not-bars law, applied to allocation).

**F2 — THE OPTIONS AREN'T EQUALLY LEGIBLE.** The hidden weapon option (ARCHIMEDES) and the full range of allocations aren't clearly surfaced; a player can complete the quest without realizing the depth of the choice they made, picking an allocation without understanding the alternatives. The richness is there but not presented as a real weighing.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** the full range of an allocation must be LEGIBLE at the point of choice — the alternatives and their theories of desert clear — so the dynast weighs a real decision, not stumbles into a default. Hidden options aren't depth if the player never knows to consider them (the #26/#162 discoverable-range law).

**F3 — NO ONE IS ACCOUNTABLE FOR THE ALLOCATION.** The player flips the switch and no one — no faction, no town, no companion — ever holds them to account for who they powered; the region doesn't remember the dynast as the one who lit or darkened it, so the moral act has no social consequence. Infrastructure's power is granted without infrastructure's responsibility.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** the one who holds the switch must be HELD to it — factions and people remembering who lit them or left them dark, standing shifting on the allocation — so the power to distribute carries the accountability of distributing. An allocation nobody remembers is a decision with no weight (the #24/#38 the-world-remembers law).

## 6 WHY IT WORKS (W1-W10)

W1. INFRASTRUCTURE IS A MORAL ACT. The quest's whole thesis: to hold the switch for a region's power is to decide who deserves the light — the console is a verdict, and the quest makes distribution the drama, not combat.
W2. THE RESOURCE IS SCARCE. The output is finite, so there is no setting that lights everyone — scarcity forces the choice, and every allocation leaves someone dark. The switch cannot be neutral.
W3. FOUR THEORIES OF DESERT. Region, fortress, cult, weapon — the many, the few, the faithful, or force — each option is a different answer to who the power is for. The menu is a moral philosophy.
W4. NO COMBAT, JUST A SWITCH. The climax is a console, not a fight — the quest proves a decision can be the whole stakes, and that flipping a lever can carry more weight than any battle.
W5. THE CONSOLE JUDGES NOTHING. No option is flagged right; the switch obeys without approving — the moral weight is left entirely on the player, which is the only honest way to present an allocation.
W6. THE FAITHFUL HAVE A CLAIM. The cult who kept the plant alive argue loyalty over need — a real tension between who tended the resource and who requires it, which allocation always faces.
W7. THE WEAPON IS AN OPTION. Power can be made a gun instead of a service — the choice to abandon welfare for force is on the menu, so the quest asks whether infrastructure serves or threatens.
W8. THE CONSEQUENCE IS DOWNSTREAM. The choice shapes the region later, diffusely — the switch's body count is deferred and quiet, which is exactly how real allocation works: the dark towns never see the console.
W9. THE PLAYER IS THE UTILITY. The quest hands one person the power of a whole region's electricity — the god-like weight of the switch, and the loneliness of holding it, is the experience.
W10. IT IS A CITY-BUILDER QUEST IN AN RPG. Restart a plant, allocate its output, shape a region — the quest is Bohemia's exact genre smuggled into a shooter, which is why it ports almost without translation.
