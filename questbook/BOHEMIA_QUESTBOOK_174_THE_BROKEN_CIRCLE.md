**Studio:** BioWare

## 0 CORE IDEA

**A demon of sloth traps the player in a dream that gives them everything they ever wanted — the dead alive, the war won, the wound healed — so they will stop fighting and sleep forever, and escaping means recognizing the perfect life as a prison and choosing the hard, real, unfinished world over the easy, false, complete one.**

The mage tower has fallen to demons; inside, the player and companions are pulled into the Fade, the dream-realm, by a Sloth demon. And the Sloth demon does not attack — it SEDUCES. It builds each captive a perfect dream: for the player, a personalized paradise where their deepest loss is undone, their hardest fight is already won, their grief is simply gone; for each companion, the same — a dwarf given the surface and a family, a templar given peace, each wrapped in the one thing they most want. The dream feels real, feels earned, feels like rest. And escaping requires the hardest recognition: that the perfect life is a cage, that the comfort is a leash, that everything you wanted has been given to you precisely so you will stop. The player must move through the dream, wake each companion by helping them recognize their own paradise as a prison, and choose to leave heaven for a world that is worse and true.

The machine is a heaven you must reject. The Sloth demon's power is not force but fulfillment — it wins by giving, and its prison has no walls, only satisfaction. The player's task is to feel the perfect dream and refuse it, and to help others do the same: each companion is trapped in their own personalized comfort and must be shown, gently, that the thing they most wanted is the thing keeping them asleep. It is a devastating inversion of temptation — usually the tempter offers a bad thing dressed as good; here it offers the genuinely good thing, the real wish, the thing you'd give anything for, and the trap is that it's real enough to keep you. Waking means grieving all over again — leaving the dream where the dead live, choosing the world where they don't. The Fade is the quest that asks whether the player can refuse the one offer they most want to accept.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** the most complete prison is the one built from everything you ever wanted, and freedom sometimes means walking out of the dream where your dead are alive, back into the world where they are not, because the true unfinished thing is worth more than the perfect false one.

## 1 CAST + WHAT EACH ONE WANTS

### THE SLOTH DEMON — the tempter who gives
- **What it wants:** the captives asleep forever, feeding on their contentment; it wins by fulfillment, not force.
- **What it'll trade:** everything — the perfect dream, the dead alive, the wound healed — for the player to stop fighting.
- **What it will never say out loud:** that the paradise is the leash; the comfort is the cage.
- **Function:** THE SEDUCER WHO OFFERS THE REAL WISH: temptation inverted — the genuinely good thing, given to keep you.

### THE PLAYER (in the dream) — the one who must refuse heaven
- **What they want:** their deepest loss undone, which the dream provides — and, under it, the truth.
- **What they'll trade:** the perfect false life, for the hard real one.
- **Function:** THE REFUSAL: the one who must feel the paradise and walk out of it.

### THE COMPANIONS (each in their own paradise) — the trapped, personalized
- **What they want:** each their deepest wish, which the dream grants — the surface, a family, peace.
- **Function:** THE PERSONALIZED PRISONS: each captive asleep in their own comfort, to be woken by recognizing it.

### THE PERFECT DREAM — the cage with no walls
- The personalized heaven the demon builds from each captive's wish.
- **Function:** THE TRAP made of fulfillment: a prison escaped not by breaking out but by refusing to stay.

### THE HARD REAL WORLD — the thing worth waking for
- The unfinished, grieving, true world outside the dream.
- **Function:** THE CHOICE: worse than the dream, and true, and worth leaving heaven for.

## 4 THE BRANCH MAP

**COUNT: 2 core postures — wake and leave (refuse the dream) or stay asleep (accept it) — times each companion's separate wake-or-sleep, so the branch is how many the player pulls out of heaven.**

- **WAKE AND LEAVE:** the player recognizes the paradise as a cage, wakes the companions, refuses the Sloth demon, and walks out into the hard real world. WRITES: the dream refused; the war resumed; the dead grieved again but the truth chosen.
- **STAY ASLEEP:** the player (or a companion) accepts the perfect dream and stays — the comfort wins, the captive lost to their own paradise. WRITES: heaven accepted; the fight abandoned; a life spent in a fulfilling lie.
- **THE PER-COMPANION AXIS:** each companion is a separate wake-or-sleep — the player can save all, some, or none from their personalized prisons. WRITES: how many chose truth over comfort.
- **THE THROUGH-LINE:** every path answers "will you refuse the thing you most want" — the branches are heaven rejected or heaven accepted, and the quest makes refusing the hardest possible choice by making the offer genuinely good.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — THE DREAM ISN'T PERSONALIZED ENOUGH TO THE PLAYER.** The Sloth demon's paradise is powerful in concept, but the player character's version is often generic (a vague "everything is fine") rather than built from THEIR specific loss — so the "everything you ever wanted" trap lands harder for the companions (who have defined wishes) than for the player, whose paradise is under-tailored.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** the perfect-dream prison must be built from the dynast's SPECIFIC deepest wish — the exact dead brought back, the exact loss undone — so the temptation is genuinely theirs. A generic paradise can't tempt; the cage must be tailored to the one it holds (the #34 make-the-lie-specific law).

**F2 — WAKING THE COMPANIONS IS A PUZZLE, NOT A GRIEF.** Recognizing each companion's paradise as a cage is largely a gameplay puzzle (find the trigger, break the illusion) rather than an emotional act — the devastating idea (helping someone leave the thing they love most) is mechanized into a fetch, so the grief of waking lands lighter than the concept.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** waking someone from their perfect dream must be an EMOTIONAL act, not a puzzle — the dynast helping them grieve the granted wish, the leaving felt as a loss — so the mercy of truth carries its cost. Breaking the illusion should hurt, not just solve (the #155/#173 grief-not-mechanic law).

**F3 — THE REAL WORLD ISN'T MADE WORTH THE SACRIFICE ON SCREEN.** The player leaves heaven for the "hard real world," but the quest doesn't dramatize WHY the broken truth is worth more than the perfect lie — the choice is presented as obviously correct (dreams are traps) rather than as a genuine weighing, so the profound "why choose grief over paradise" question is answered before it's asked.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** the choice to leave the perfect dream must make the REAL WORLD'S worth felt — what the truth offers that the lie can't (agency, meaning, the dignity of an unfinished life) — so refusing paradise is a genuine weighing, not a reflex. If the dream is obviously bad, it isn't a temptation (the #156 make-the-alternative-real law).

## 6 WHY IT WORKS (W1-W10)

W1. THE TEMPTER GIVES, NOT TAKES. The Sloth demon wins by fulfillment — it offers everything you want, and the trap is that it's real enough to keep you. Temptation inverted: the genuinely good thing as the leash.
W2. THE PRISON HAS NO WALLS. The cage is made of contentment; there is nothing to break out of, only a comfort to refuse. Escape is a choice, not a fight, which is far harder.
W3. THE DREAM IS PERSONALIZED. Each captive gets their deepest wish — the dead alive, the surface, peace — so the trap is tailored to exactly what each person can't refuse.
W4. THE WISH IS THE LEASH. The thing you most want, given, is the thing keeping you asleep — the recognition that fulfillment is the trap is the hardest and truest thing the quest asks.
W5. WAKING IS GRIEVING AGAIN. Leaving the dream where the dead live means grieving them a second time, choosing the world where they don't — the escape costs the wish all over.
W6. THE COMPANIONS ARE SEPARATE PRISONS. Each is asleep in their own paradise, woken only by recognizing it — the player must pull each out of the thing they love most.
W7. THE OFFER IS GENUINELY GOOD. Not a bad thing dressed as good, but the real wish — which is why refusing it is devastating: you're rejecting the one offer you most want to accept.
W8. FREEDOM IS CHOOSING THE WORSE TRUE THING. The real world is harder, sadder, unfinished — and worth more than the perfect lie. The quest's answer to the Amalgamation: truth over comfort, even when comfort is heaven.
W9. THE DEMON DOESN'T LIE. It gives you exactly what you wanted, honestly — the horror is that the fulfillment is real, and the trap is in the staying, not in a deception.
W10. IT IS THE AMALGAMATION AS A DUNGEON. "No one is ever lost, the grief is gone, the dead are back" — the Amalgamation's whole promise, made a level the player walks out of by refusing it. The perfect lie you choose to leave.
