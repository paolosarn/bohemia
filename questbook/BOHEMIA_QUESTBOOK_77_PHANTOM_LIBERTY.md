# BOHEMIA QUESTBOOK — DEEP DIVE 77: EVERYONE IS LYING, PICK ONE / THE CURE THAT COSTS YOU EVERYTHING YOU ARE (Cyberpunk 2077 — Phantom Liberty)
Full teardown, the whole enchilada: the everyone-is-lying spy-thriller structure, the forced betrayal at
Firestarter, the twist that your ally was lying about your cure the whole time, the four endings, the
"Face in the Crowd" ending where the cure costs you everything you are, the both-sides-are-tools-of-a-
worse-system theme, the no-right-answer design, the honest flaws, and Bohemia ports. Widely called the
best expansion since Blood and Wine. DIRECTLY relevant to our Megaton law, our no-clean-answer ethos, +
our Amalgamation's bargain. CD Projekt Red. Reference only; Paolo does not read it. No Bohemia quest here.

Arc: Phantom Liberty (CDPR, 2023). V, dying of a biochip in their skull, is contacted by SONGBIRD (Song So
Mi) — a legendary netrunner and the NUSA President's personal weapon — who promises a CURE in exchange for
help. It's a spy thriller in DOGTOWN, a walled-off warzone: rescue the President, work with burnt-out
sleeper agent SOLOMON REED, and eventually choose between Reed and Songbird. Every party is lying. The four
endings include one that gives V what they wanted at a price they never imagined.

===============================================================================
## 0. THE CORE IDEA (why it's in the canon)
===============================================================================
- EVERY SINGLE PARTY IS LYING, INCLUDING THE ONE SAVING YOUR LIFE (the structure — the spy-thriller engine):
  the President wants Songbird back as a WEAPON ("she says she wants to save her, but it's clear that it's
  so she can continue to USE her"); Reed is "little more than a tool of a jealous, power-hungry world
  government" who genuinely loves Songbird and will still cage her; Songbird has been lying about V's cure
  from the start. There is no honest broker. You are asked to pick a side among people who are ALL using
  you (cf. our faction web + Network; the world with no honest party).
- THE FORCED BETRAYAL: YOU CANNOT NOT BETRAY SOMEONE (the hinge — Firestarter): mid-arc, in the middle of a
  heist, you must either use Reed's ICEbreaker to knock Songbird out, or let her run. There's no third
  option, no talk-it-out, no save-everyone. And it costs IMMEDIATELY + asymmetrically: betray Songbird and
  she warns Hansen, who murders your ally ALEX in the observation room; side with Songbird and she loses
  control of the Blackwall + wrecks the stadium, and Reed + Alex clock your treachery. Someone you like
  dies or hates you either way, in the next five minutes (cf. our Megaton law; the choice with no clean
  exit).
- THE TWIST: SHE WAS NEVER GOING TO CURE YOU (the reveal that re-reads the whole arc): late, on a shuttle,
  Johnny lays it out — "Songbird has been LYING about the cure and always intended to leave us behind." The
  person you may have betrayed a friend for was using you exactly like everyone else. And it lands "at a
  point where I didn't think there could be any more surprises." The arc's emotional question flips from
  "who do I trust?" to "who do I want to be, knowing nobody deserved it?" (cf. our Amalgamation's offer;
  the reveal that recontextualizes the betrayal).

## 1. THE ARCHITECTURE (how a no-win choice is built)
===============================================================================

### BOTH SIDES ARE GIVEN A REAL, HONEST CASE (the balance — key craft)
- SONGBIRD: "she has spent her ENTIRE ADULT LIFE being moulded into a weapon of mass destruction AGAINST
  HER WILL, forced to hurt friends and betray comrades to meet the whims of her masters." She's dying of a
  Blackwall infection. Her only crime, as one critic puts it, "was CURIOSITY." She wants "treatment on her
  own terms and a future of her own choice."
- REED: honest, decent, genuinely loved her, mentored her — and is a lifelong instrument of a government
  that will use her until she breaks. He believes capturing her is for "her own safety and the safety of
  others," and he is not entirely wrong (she IS a walking Blackwall breach). He "may not deserve to die,
  but he puts his own life on the line to prevent Songbird from going free."
- The result: GamesRadar, after playing everything — "I wouldn't say that one of the above choices is
  clearly better than the other." A reviewer: "I wasn't able to decide who to help or who to betray until
  I was FORCED to. At no point in the game was I able to definitively make up my mind, and that's a
  testament to the strength of the writing" (cf. our Megaton law; both cases honest).
### YOU CAN CHANGE YOUR MIND AS YOU LEARN (the arc rewards re-evaluation)
- The choice isn't one-and-done: "both options let players explore more about the characters' past and
  motives and FORCE THEM TO QUESTION THEIR ACTIONS through to the end of the DLC, with there being FURTHER
  CHOICES allowing them to CHANGE THEIR MINDS depending on the new information they're uncovering (for
  example, learning Songbird's backstory through flashbacks if they chose to side with Reed)." Siding
  against someone is how you LEARN about them — and then you get to reconsider (cf. our faction web +
  standing; the branch that teaches you why you were wrong).
### THE CHORUS: JOHNNY FRAMES THE THEME BEFORE YOU FEEL IT (the commentator as craft)
- Johnny Silverhand isn't decoration — he "has a lot of interesting input throughout the story about
  working for military and government establishments. He explains that once the establishments have their
  hooks in you, THEY'LL NEVER LET YOU GO FREE. Through this dialogue, Johnny essentially FRAMES THE REALITY
  OF SONGBIRD'S SITUATION before we can even come to grips with it ourselves." He also draws a parallel
  from Reed + Alex's relationship to his own past, foreshadowing the ending. A companion who reads the
  theme aloud without preaching it (cf. our companions + no-preaching rule; the chorus).
### THE ENDINGS (four, and the "best" one is the cruellest)
- SIDE WITH SONGBIRD (most players did — a LowSodiumCyberpunk poll found the majority betrayed Reed): you
  can send her to the MOON for treatment on her own terms — after gunning down Reed, who dies trying to
  stop her. Or you can hand her over even after helping her. She lied to you, and you free her anyway.
- SIDE WITH REED: Songbird lives, in custody, to be used again — and this unlocks the base game's new
  ending, "FACE IN THE CROWD."
- AND THAT ENDING IS THE KNIFE: Reed gets V the surgery. It works. Johnny is WIPED for good. V wakes from
  a COMA — TWO YEARS LATER. And the body can no longer take combat cyberware: "the merc life they once
  knew and loved was gone." The ending literally shows V unable to defend themselves from two low-life
  thugs. Misty helps V "come to grips with being an AVERAGE PERSON." V "resigns themselves to a normal,
  boring life off the grid and blends into the Night City crowds as the camera fades to black."
  - You get the CURE. You lose your friend, your body, your legend, two years, and everyone who moved on.
    The reviewer calls it "the TRUE ending because of the way it connects everything to the base game" —
    and ScreenRant calls it "one of the WORST endings possible in Cyberpunk 2077." Both are right (cf. our
    Amalgamation's bargain; the wish granted exactly, cf. Hearts of Stone Q70).
### "THE ONLY RIGHT CHOICE IS THE ONE YOU CAN LIVE WITH" (the thesis, stated by a critic)
- TheGamer's verdict: "I went through every single option, RELOADING my save again and again because I
  didn't know what to do, who to betray, or how I wanted this story to end... The only right choice is the
  one that makes you feel the most satisfaction and least regret, and EVEN THEN you'll be left wondering
  what could have been... Every ending has you consider all the decisions you had the power to influence
  WITHOUT KNOWING WHAT THE REPERCUSSIONS WOULD BE days, months, weeks, or years down the line. That's why
  it's so powerful, and why I decided to stick to a decision that sat best with WHO I AM" (cf. our Megaton
  law + no-clean-answer ethos; the choice that reveals the player).

## 2. THE HONEST FLAWS (banked)
===============================================================================
- THE FANDOM STILL SORTED IT INTO GOOD/BAD (the Shadowheart problem again): despite immaculate balance,
  guides declare "players SHOULD side with Songbird during Firestarter," and the Reed path's reward is
  called "one of the worst endings possible." A majority poll went one way. LESSON (our Megaton law):
  even perfectly-balanced WRITING loses to reward-framing + community consensus — if one branch's payoff
  is a coma and a broken body, players will call it the bad end regardless of theme (balance the FRAMING).
- THE "BEST" ENDING REQUIRES BETRAYING SOMEONE WHO BETRAYED YOU FIRST (a moral muddle some found cheap):
  the most-recommended path is to free Songbird AFTER learning she lied about your cure and always meant
  to abandon you — which some read as profound (mercy without reward) and others as the writers wanting a
  noble gesture regardless of what was earned. LESSON: a redemption gesture must be EARNED by the recipient
  or at least explicitly framed as being for the GIVER's sake, not the receiver's (our Pacifist Law's
  framing — mercy as self-definition).
- THE CHOICE IS FORCED, NOT DESIGNED-AROUND: there is no clever third path — no talk-down, no build, no
  amount of preparation gets everyone out. Intentional (that's the theme), but it means player AGENCY is
  narrower than the game's reputation implies: you pick from a menu at a scripted moment. LESSON: "no
  third option" is powerful ONCE; if every big beat is a forced binary, players notice the rails (our
  Megaton law + Pacifist Law: our refusals must have real routes — contrast Dark Brotherhood's stub Q75).
- IT'S GATED BEHIND A SEPARATE PURCHASE + A SPECIFIC SAVE STATE: the entire arc (and the base game's new
  ending) only exists in paid content, entered at a specific point. Six people in a poll skipped it
  entirely and let the President die. LESSON: n/a commercially for us, but structurally — content that
  changes the MAIN game's ending shouldn't be invisible or optional to find (our finale-accessibility).
- V IS A CIPHER IN THEIR OWN STORY: the arc's best characters (Songbird, Reed, Alex, Johnny) are so strong
  that V mostly REACTS. Fine for a player-avatar, but the emotional weight lives in NPCs. LESSON (for our
  fold): if our DYNASTS are ciphers and only the NPCs are people, the fold has no center (the PSIII lesson
  Q67 — our dynasts must be characters, not cameras).

## 3. WHY IT WORKS (craft, itemized for stealing)
===============================================================================
W1. EVERY PARTY IS LYING, INCLUDING YOUR LIFELINE: a president who wants a weapon, an honest man who's a
    government tool, a victim who's using you — no honest broker exists (cf. our faction web + Network).
W2. YOU CANNOT NOT BETRAY SOMEONE: no third option, no talk-out — and the cost lands within five minutes
    (a friend murdered, or your allies clocking your treachery) (cf. our Megaton law).
W3. THE TWIST RE-READS YOUR BETRAYAL: she was never going to cure you — the question flips from "who do I
    trust?" to "who am I, knowing nobody deserved it?" (cf. our Amalgamation's offer).
W4. BOTH CASES ARE HONEST + IRRECONCILABLE: a woman made into a weapon against her will vs a man who loves
    her and is right that she's dangerous — critics who played everything couldn't rank them (cf. our
    Megaton law).
W5. THE OPPOSING BRANCH IS HOW YOU LEARN THE TRUTH: side against someone and you get their backstory in
    flashback — then you're allowed to CHANGE YOUR MIND (cf. our faction web; the branch that teaches you).
W6. A CHORUS WHO FRAMES THE THEME WITHOUT PREACHING: Johnny names the system's logic ("once they've got
    their hooks in you, they'll never let you go") before the player can articulate it (cf. our companions
    + no-preaching rule).
W7. THE CURE COSTS EVERYTHING YOU ARE: you win — and wake from a two-year coma with your friend erased,
    your body unable to hold your trade, and a normal, boring life (cf. Hearts of Stone Q70; the wish
    granted exactly).
W8. THE "TRUE" ENDING IS ALSO THE "WORST" ENDING: both readings are defensible — it's the most connected +
    the most devastating (cf. our finales; ambiguity that survives the wiki).
W9. THE CHOICE IS MADE WITHOUT KNOWING THE REPERCUSSIONS: "every ending has you consider all the decisions
    you had the power to influence WITHOUT KNOWING what the repercussions would be" (cf. our fold's
    generational blindness — the deepest port).
W10. MERCY AFTER BETRAYAL AS SELF-DEFINITION: you can free the person who lied to you, gaining nothing —
     "the only right choice is the one that makes you feel the least regret" (cf. our Pacifist Law).

## 4. BOHEMIA PORTS (directions, not built) — no honest brokers + the cure that costs everything
===============================================================================
Phantom Liberty is the modern benchmark for a MEGATON choice where every option is a betrayal and nobody
deserves your loyalty — which is our FACTION WEB's endgame shape, and its "cure that costs everything you
are" is our Amalgamation's bargain rendered as an ending.
- W1/W4 (no honest brokers + both cases honest — our faction web's endgame): bank the structure. Our 13
  factions + the NETWORK should converge on an endgame where NO party is honest: the ones who'd free the
  dead want a weapon, the ones who'd contain them love them and will cage them anyway, and the victim is
  using the dynasty too. Both cases must be REAL enough that a critic who played every branch can't rank
  them (ties our faction web + Network + Megaton law + Liberate/Respect/Become; the honest-broker-free world).
- W2/W9 (you cannot not betray + you choose without knowing the repercussions — our FOLD's deepest port):
  the sharpest port here. W9 especially: "every ending has you consider all the decisions you had the power
  to influence WITHOUT KNOWING what the repercussions would be days, months, WEEKS, OR YEARS down the
  line." That is LITERALLY our fold — a dynast's choices detonate in a GRANDCHILD's lifetime, and they
  make them blind. Bank it as our fold's emotional thesis + the argument for our persistent-consequence
  system: the generational delay makes Phantom Liberty's best idea structural for us (ties our fold +
  generational persistence + persistent-consequence + Megaton law; our structure does this natively).
- W3/W7/W8 (the twist re-reads the betrayal + the cure costs everything + true=worst — our AMALGAMATION's
  bargain): STEAL the ending shape. The Amalgamation offers what the dynasty wants most (the dead back,
  the district safe, the memory whole) — and DELIVERS, exactly, at a cost that guts what they were: the
  ally erased, years gone, the family unable to do the thing that defined them, a quiet ordinary life among
  people who moved on. And bank W3: the entity/faction they trusted was USING them the whole time, which
  re-reads every sacrifice made to get there. This is Hearts of Stone's fine-print (Q70) rendered as a
  playable finale (ties our Amalgamation + Network + Liberate/Respect/Become + fold; a top-tier finale port).
- W5 (the opposing branch is how you learn the truth): STEAL this directly — in Bohemia, siding AGAINST a
  faction should be how you learn their real story (flashbacks, records, [READ] fragments you only get from
  the inside of the opposing camp) — and then let the dynasty CHANGE ITS MIND with the new information.
  That makes replay + faction-switching narratively productive instead of punitive (ties our faction web +
  standing + [READ]; the branch that teaches you why you were wrong).
- W6 (a chorus who frames the theme without preaching): bank the device — a Bohemia companion/voice (a
  ghost in the record, an older relative, an Amalgamation fragment) who names the SYSTEM's logic ("once
  they've got their hooks in you, they never let go") before the player can articulate it — theme delivered
  as character, not sermon. Fits our no-preaching rule exactly (ties our companions + Amalgamation +
  no-preaching rule + writing pillar).
- W10 (mercy after betrayal as self-definition — our Pacifist Law): bank it — the dynasty can extend mercy
  to someone who lied to them, gaining NOTHING, because the choice is about who THEY are, not what the
  other deserved. That's our Pacifist Law's philosophical spine (ties our Pacifist Law + Megaton law +
  conscience system; mercy as self-definition, not reward).
- FLAWS (bank HARD): (a) balanced WRITING loses to reward-FRAMING — if one branch ends in a coma + a broken
  body, the wiki calls it the bad end no matter what the text says (our Megaton law: balance framing +
  rewards, the Shadowheart lesson Q76 repeated); (b) a mercy gesture must be EARNED or explicitly framed as
  for the GIVER's sake (our Pacifist Law's framing); (c) "no third option" is powerful ONCE — if every big
  beat is a forced binary, players see the rails; our refusals need REAL routes (our Pacifist Law + Megaton,
  contra Dark Brotherhood's stub Q75); (d) content that changes the MAIN ending shouldn't be invisible (our
  finale-accessibility); and (e) if the DYNAST is a cipher and only the NPCs are people, our FOLD has no
  center — our dynasts must be characters, not cameras (the PSIII lesson Q67).
