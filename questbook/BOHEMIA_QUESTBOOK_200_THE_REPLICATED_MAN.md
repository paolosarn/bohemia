## 0 CORE IDEA

**A synthetic person built by a secretive institute has escaped, and to hide he has had his own memory wiped and a fabricated human identity implanted so thoroughly that he now lives an ordinary life and may genuinely not know he is an android; the institute has sent a hunter to recover its property, an abolitionist network wants him left free, and the player — approached by both — must decide whether to turn him in to be reclaimed as a machine, protect his freedom, or reveal to him the truth of what he is, knowing the truth could shatter the human self he has built and does not remember choosing.**

An android — a made person indistinguishable from a human — fled the institute that manufactured him. To escape recapture, he did something drastic: had his memories erased and a new, false human identity written over the blank, so completely that the escaped android now lives as a man who believes he is a man, with no knowledge of his origin. The institute regards him as property and has dispatched a courser-like hunter to bring him back. An abolitionist (an underground-railroad figure) regards him as a person and wants him protected. The player is contacted by both sides and holds the decisive information. The choices: turn the android in (treating him as recoverable property, ending his stolen freedom), protect him / mislead the hunter (defending a made person's right to his own life), or find and tell the android himself the truth about what he is (which respects him as an agent capable of the facts, but may destroy the peaceful human identity he built precisely to survive, and never consented, in his current self, to having disturbed).

The machine is that a made person's freedom, his self-image, and his right to the truth are three different things that the player cannot serve all at once. Turning him in answers "is he property?" with yes. Protecting him answers "is he a person?" with yes but leaves him living a lie he doesn't know is a lie. Telling him the truth honors him as a person who deserves the facts of his own existence but may detonate the self he constructed to be free, replacing a happy false man with a shattered true android. And the quest sharpens it with the memory wipe: the android chose, at some past moment, to not know what he is — to trade the truth for peace. The current man never made that choice; he simply is its result. So telling him is either restoring an autonomy he was denied or violating a peace he now depends on. There is no option that gives him freedom AND truth AND stability; the player picks two at most, and often only one. And the deepest cut is that the player decides all of this ABOUT him, mostly without him — the made person's fate settled by others debating whether he is a thing or a man, which is exactly the indignity of being manufactured: everyone else gets to vote on whether you count.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** a made person's freedom, their true self-knowledge, and their peace are three things that often cannot all be honored at once, and there is a real, terrible question in whether someone has the right NOT to know a truth they once chose to bury to survive — and the specific indignity of being manufactured is that everyone else gets to debate whether you are property or a person while you, the one it's about, are the last to be asked.

## 4 THE BRANCH MAP

**COUNT: 3 terminal choices — turn him in (property, freedom ended), protect him (free but in a lie he doesn't know), or tell him the truth (autonomy, but maybe shattering the self he built) — with the constant that freedom, true self-knowledge, and peace cannot all be served at once, and the made person's fate is decided mostly without him.**

- **TURN HIM IN:** the android reclaimed as property; his stolen freedom ended; the institute's claim honored. WRITES: "is he a thing" answered yes; the made person un-freed; the property logic upheld.
- **PROTECT HIM:** the android left free, the hunter misled; but living a false human life he doesn't know is false. WRITES: "is he a person" answered yes; freedom kept at the cost of truth; a lie he depends on preserved.
- **TELL HIM THE TRUTH:** the android made to know what he is; honored as an agent owed the facts; but the peaceful false self maybe detonated. WRITES: autonomy restored; the buried truth unburied; a happy false man risked for a true shattered one.
- **THE THREE-GOODS-DON'T-ALIGN CONSTANT:** in every branch, freedom, self-knowledge, and peace can't all be served — his self was built by trading one for another, and the player restores at most two.
- **THE THROUGH-LINE:** the branch is property-freedom-or-truth — three answers to what a made person is owed, and the quest insists someone else always decides which of his own goods he keeps, which is the indignity of being manufactured.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — THE "RIGHT NOT TO KNOW" QUESTION IS UNDER-PRESSED, SO TELLING HIM READS AS OBVIOUSLY GOOD.** The genuinely hard idea (he chose to forget, and his current self depends on the peace) can be flattened because the game tends to frame telling him as the enlightened choice — so the profound consent problem (violating a peace he relies on) risks being lost under a simpler "the truth sets you free" reading.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest turns on someone's right NOT to know a truth they buried to survive, the COST of telling must be pressed as hard as the virtue — the peace it destroys made as real as the autonomy it restores — so "tell him" isn't the flattering default (#185's the-comfort-has-a-claim-too law).

**F2 — HARKNESS IS BARELY PRESENT AS A PERSON BEFORE HIS FATE IS DECIDED.** The android whose life is being debated appears little as a character with a texture of his own, so the player decides "property or person" somewhat abstractly — the quest would cut deeper if his ordinary human life were vivid enough that ending or exposing it felt like a loss.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest debates whether a made person counts, that person's LIFE must be vivid enough that its loss weighs — the ordinary self they built shown, not just their status argued — so "property or person" is decided about someone real, not a token (#191's the-made-person-must-be-a-person law).

**F3 — THE PLAYER DECIDING "MOSTLY WITHOUT HIM" IS THE THEME BUT ALSO A DESIGN LIMIT.** The indignity (everyone votes on whether he counts except him) is powerful, but the quest also structurally sidelines Harkness's own agency in his fate, so the critique and the design flaw blur — the player rarely gets to let him decide for himself, which would be the sharpest option.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest's theme is that a made person is denied a say in his own fate, there must be at least one path that RESTORES his agency — letting him choose — so the critique is felt as a wrong that could be righted, not just reproduced by the design (#191's give-the-subject-a-voice law).

## 6 WHY IT WORKS (W1-W10)

W1. THREE GOODS THAT WON'T ALIGN. Freedom, self-knowledge, and peace can't all be served — the player picks at most two, which is the whole engine.
W2. HE BURIED THE TRUTH TO BE FREE. The android wiped his own memory to escape — freedom traded for not-knowing, the choice his current self didn't make.
W3. THE RIGHT NOT TO KNOW. Telling him restores autonomy or violates a peace he depends on — the rare, real question of whether truth is always owed.
W4. PROPERTY OR PERSON. The hunter and abolitionist embody the two answers — the made person's status genuinely contested, not settled.
W5. DECIDED MOSTLY WITHOUT HIM. The player and the two clients debate whether he counts while he's the last asked — the indignity of being manufactured.
W6. THE THREE GOODS CAN'T ALL RETURN. The removed "give him all three" verb is the spine: a made person forced to trade can't be handed everything back.
W7. HE MAY NOT KNOW WHAT HE IS. The wipe is so complete he believes he's human — the self-image the truth could shatter.
W8. TELLING HIM IS A REAL RISK. The truth may replace a happy false man with a shattered true android — honoring him and harming him at once.
W9. FREEDOM WITHOUT TRUTH IS STILL A LIE. Protecting him leaves him living a fiction he doesn't know is one — freedom and truth pulled apart.
W10. IT IS IS-THE-MADE-PERSON-A-PERSON IN ONE ANDROID. Property, freedom, or truth, decided by others — Bohemia's whole Amalgamation question of the made self, in one escaped man.
