## 0 CORE IDEA

**In a slave-labor hell where workers die of a mutagenic plague, a rebellion asks you to steal the only cure so everyone can be freed and healed — and the cure turns out to be a living, immune infant, the tyrant-boss's adopted daughter, so freeing the slaves means taking a child from the one place she is loved, and both the tyrant and the rebels are simultaneously right and monstrous.**

The Pitt is the irradiated ruin of Pittsburgh, run as an industrial slave camp by a raider boss named Ashur. The workers — slaves — labor in toxic conditions and die of a mutation-plague called the Trog Degeneration that turns victims into feral monsters. A slave rebellion recruits the player: steal the "cure" Ashur is hoarding, and the slaves can be freed and saved. The player fights up through the mill to Ashur's stronghold and learns the cure is not a serum. It is Marie, an infant born immune to the plague, whose blood is the key to a cure that does not yet exist and must be developed from her. Ashur is her adopted father; he found her, he loves her, and he is protecting her from exactly what the rebels intend — years of being a lab-subject, bled for a cure. The rebels are not lying about the slaves' suffering, which is real and horrific. Ashur is not lying about loving Marie, which is real. The cure that frees hundreds is a specific baby, and taking her is both a liberation and a kidnapping.

The machine is that the greater good has a face and a name, and the face is a child. Every abstraction the rebels offer — "the cure," "the slaves' freedom," "the future of the Pitt" — resolves, at the top of the mill, into an infant in a crib. The player must decide whether hundreds of suffering slaves are worth one child's stolen childhood, and the quest refuses to make either side clean: Ashur is a slaver who loves his daughter, the rebel leader is a liberator who would put a baby on a table. There is no faction whose hands are clean, no choice that saves everyone, and the "cure" the player has spent the whole DLC fighting toward turns out to be a living person who cannot consent. The quest lets the player take Marie for the rebellion (the many freed, the child taken), leave her with Ashur (the slaves left in hell, the child loved), and the aftermath sours either way — a freed Pitt that descends into its own chaos, or a slave camp that grinds on.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** the greater good always has a specific face when you finally reach it, and "the cure for everyone" turning out to be one child who can't consent is what every abstraction does at the end of the road — the number was always a person, and someone has to decide whether the many are worth the one, knowing neither side's hands are clean.

## 1 CAST + WHAT EACH ONE WANTS

### ASHUR — the slaver who loves his daughter
- **What he wants (stated):** to keep Marie safe; to build a working Pitt on the backs of the slaves.
- **What he wants (real):** to protect the one thing he loves from being turned into a lab-subject for the cure.
- **What he'll trade:** the slaves' freedom, forever, to keep the child un-bled.
- **What he will never say out loud:** that his love for one child is worth, to him, hundreds of others' suffering.
- **Function:** THE TYRANT WHO LOVES: the monster whose protected innocent is the cure the many need.

### WERNHER / THE REBELS — the liberators who would bleed the baby
- **What they want:** the cure; the slaves freed; the plague ended.
- **What they'll trade:** the child's childhood, her body, her consent — a baby on a table for the many.
- **Function:** THE LIBERATORS WHO WOULD DISSECT: the just cause whose means is a stolen, un-consenting infant.

### MARIE — the cure with a name
- An immune infant whose blood is the key to a cure that must be developed from her over years.
- **Function:** THE FACE OF THE GREATER GOOD: the abstraction ("the cure") resolved into a specific child who cannot consent.

### THE SLAVES — the many whose suffering is real
- Workers dying of the Trog plague in toxic labor; the hundreds the cure would free.
- **Function:** THE NUMBER: the real, horrific many, whose freedom is weighed against the one child.

### THE PLAYER — the one who decides which unclean side wins
- **What they want:** to end the horror; to find the choice that saves everyone (there isn't one).
- **Function:** THE DECIDER: the one who puts the many against the one and picks a side with dirty hands.

## 4 THE BRANCH MAP

**COUNT: 2 terminal choices at the crib — take Marie for the rebellion (the many freed, the child taken) or leave her with Ashur (the child loved, the slaves left in hell) — with the constant that both factions are simultaneously right and monstrous, and the cure has no form but the child.**

- **TAKE MARIE:** the child removed to the rebellion; the cure developed from her over years; the slaves freed. WRITES: hundreds saved; one infant taken from love into a lab-life; a freed Pitt that curdles into its own chaos.
- **LEAVE MARIE:** the child left with the father who loves her; the slaves left dying in the plague-hell. WRITES: one childhood spared; hundreds ground on; the slave camp intact under a tyrant who loves exactly one person.
- **THE NO-CLEAN-FACTION CONSTANT:** in both branches, Ashur is a slaver who genuinely loves his daughter and the rebels are liberators who would genuinely bleed a baby — neither side's hands are clean, and the player chooses which monstrousness wins.
- **THE THROUGH-LINE:** the branch is the-many-versus-the-one-with-a-face — two answers to "is the greater good worth the specific child it turns out to be," and the DLC refuses a serum, a third path, or a clean side.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — ASHUR IS TOO ARTICULATE, WHICH TILTS THE SCALE TOWARD KEEPING THE SLAVES.** Ashur gets a long, persuasive speech about order and Marie, while the rebels' case is delivered mostly through squalor and a leader who reads as unhinged — so the "both sides unclean" balance tips, and many players feel the game wants them to side with the eloquent slaver, undercutting the genuine dilemma.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a choice depends on both factions being equally right and equally monstrous, both must be given equal ARTICULATION — the liberator's case as well-argued as the tyrant's — so the scale isn't tilted by who got the better speech. A dilemma where one side is eloquent and the other is squalor is a recommendation in disguise (#169's both-sides-fully-built law).

**F2 — TAKING MARIE HAS THIN CONSEQUENCES, SO THE KIDNAPPING DOESN'T WEIGH.** Once Marie is taken, the game shows little of what her lab-life actually costs her — the profound "you took a child to save the many" recedes into a faction-outcome slide, so the weight of the one is stated and then dropped, letting the player off the hook the crib put them on.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if the greater good is bought with a specific child, the child's COST must return — what the taken life became, glimpsed after — so the one isn't a price the player pays once and never sees again. A sacrificed innocent who vanishes from the aftermath was never really weighed (#170/#178's the-cost-must-return law).

**F3 — THE "NO SERUM, ONLY THE CHILD" REVEAL CAN BE SPOILED INTO A GAME PROBLEM.** Because the cure is a fetch-object the whole DLC points at, some players approach Marie as loot — "get the cure" — and the horror of the reveal (it's a baby) lands as a mechanical twist rather than a moral gut-punch, blunting the face-of-the-greater-good idea.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** when an abstraction is about to resolve into a person, the reveal must be STAGED as a person, not delivered as a loot-pickup — the child seen, held, humanized before the choice — so the greater-good-has-a-face lands as horror, not inventory. A person revealed as an objective reads as an item (#161's the-victim-must-be-a-person-first law).

## 6 WHY IT WORKS (W1-W10)

W1. THE CURE IS A CHILD. The abstraction the whole DLC chases — "the cure" — resolves into a specific immune infant, which is the greater good getting a face at the end of the road.
W2. LIBERATION AND KIDNAPPING ARE THE SAME ACT. Freeing the slaves requires taking Marie — the two are one action, which is the dilemma's cruel spine.
W3. NO FACTION IS CLEAN. Ashur is a slaver who loves his daughter; the rebels are liberators who would bleed a baby — the player chooses which monstrousness wins, not good versus evil.
W4. THE MANY ARE REAL. The slaves' suffering is horrific and true — the number isn't a strawman, which is what makes taking the child a genuine temptation.
W5. THE ONE CAN'T CONSENT. Marie is an infant — the cure is developed from a person who cannot agree, which is the sharpest form of the-one-versus-the-many.
W6. THERE IS NO SERUM. The removed "free the slaves without the child" verb is the spine: no third option, no clean path, the greater good inseparable from the specific person it became.
W7. THE TYRANT'S LOVE IS REAL. Ashur genuinely loves Marie — the monster is humanized, which is what makes leaving her a real temptation too.
W8. THE AFTERMATH SOURS BOTH WAYS. A freed Pitt curdles; a slave camp grinds on — neither choice buys a clean future, which is the honesty of an unclean dilemma.
W9. IT IS A HELL WORTH FREEING. The Pitt's horror is vivid enough that the slaves' freedom feels genuinely urgent — the stakes on the many's side are real, not abstract.
W10. IT IS THE ENDING-MATH IN ONE CRIB. The greater good with a specific child's face — Bohemia's whole Liberate/Respect/Become question of what the many are worth against the one, staged over a baby.
