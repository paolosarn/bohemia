# BOHEMIA QUESTBOOK #206 — "THE BULL AND THE BACHELOR (TWO HEALERS SAVE THE TOWN BY OPPOSITE CURES AND ONLY ONE PLAGUE-LOGIC CAN BE TRUE)"
**Game:** Pathologic 2 (2019)
**Studio:** Ice-Pick Lodge
**Quest:** the three-healers structure / the Bull-vs-Bachelor split — a plague ravages a town and two outsiders each believe they can save it by incompatible methods rooted in incompatible understandings of what the plague even is, so the player, embodying one healer, must commit to a cure that damns whatever the other healer would have saved, in a town where every day spent deciding is a day people die.
**Type:** INDIVIDUAL-QUEST DEEP DIVE (mined fresh 7/18 from the Pathologic catalogue; corpus anchor Q21-class for the game's larger machines)
**FORMAT:** v2 — cast + conversation node trees + branch map.
**Filed:** 7/18/26
**Why pulled:** Bohemia needs the quest where two rescuers hold incompatible theories of the disaster itself, so their cures cannot both be pursued, and committing to one means the other's people die — where the player cannot hedge, cannot combine, and every hour of indecision has a body count. The Bull-vs-Bachelor split is the specimen: a plague, two healers with mutually exclusive worldviews (mystical/organic vs rational/scientific), a town that can only be saved one way, and a clock that kills. It is the fold's incompatible-cures machine (#25) fused with the time-as-body-count engine (#21), and the "you must pick a worldview to save anyone and the other's people die" law.

## 0 CORE IDEA

**A lethal plague is devouring a remote town, and two outsider healers have each arrived convinced they can save it — but their methods are not merely different, they are rooted in fundamentally incompatible understandings of what the plague even is (one sees it as a living, almost mystical organism bound to the town's soil and body; the other as a disease to be rationally isolated and cured), so their cures cannot both be pursued, and the player, embodying one of these healers, must commit fully to a single worldview's remedy, knowing that doing so forecloses the other's, damns whatever the other would have saved, and that every day spent deliberating is a day the plague takes more of the town.**

Pathologic's town is dying. Three healers — most sharply, the Bachelor (a rationalist scientist who sees the plague as a disease with a material cause to be isolated and cured through reason and institutions) and the Haruspex/Bull (a native-trained healer who understands the plague as something organic, living, bound to the town's flesh and earth and myth) — each hold a theory of the catastrophe. These are not two techniques for the same problem; they are two different diagnoses of what the problem IS, and each implies a cure that makes the other's impossible. To save the town the Bachelor's way requires treating it as a containable outbreak; to save it the Haruspex's way requires treating it as a wounded living thing. You cannot do both, because they contradict at the root. The player inhabits one healer and must commit — and committing means the parts of the town, the people, the institutions that only the OTHER healer's worldview could have saved are lost, because your cure is not built to save them. And over all of it runs the clock: the plague spreads daily, resources dwindle, people you know die on a schedule, and every hour spent agonizing over which worldview is right is an hour the disease uses to take more.

The machine is that the disaster's very nature is contested, so saving anyone requires committing to one account of reality that damns whatever the other account would have preserved, under a clock that punishes hesitation with corpses. Most rescue fantasies let the player find the one true solution. Pathologic denies there is a shared reality to be right about: the Bachelor and the Haruspex do not just disagree on the cure, they disagree on what the plague is, and the game refuses to referee — both worldviews are internally coherent, partially validated, and mutually exclusive. So the player cannot hedge (hedging saves no one), cannot synthesize (the theories contradict), and cannot wait (the clock kills). They must plant their feet in one understanding of the catastrophe, pursue its cure, and accept that the other healer's people — the ones only the other diagnosis could have reached — will die because the player chose a different reality to be true. And the deepest cut is that the player never gets certainty they chose the right worldview; they only get the town they saved and the town they let die, and the knowledge that a different committed person, choosing the other account, would have saved and lost different people, equally sure.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** sometimes the people trying to fix a disaster do not even agree on what the disaster IS, and their cures contradict at the root, so saving anyone requires committing to one account of reality that damns whatever the other account would have saved — and you never get certainty you chose right, only the ones you saved and the ones you let die, under a clock that turns every hour of hedging into a body, because refusing to commit to a worldview is itself a choice that kills.

## 1 CAST + WHAT EACH ONE WANTS

### THE BACHELOR — the rationalist who sees a disease
- **What he wants:** to save the town by reason — isolate, contain, cure the plague as a material outbreak.
- **What his worldview forecloses:** whatever only the organic/mystical understanding could have reached.
- **Function:** THE RATIONAL CURE: one coherent diagnosis of the catastrophe, exclusive of the other.

### THE HARUSPEX / THE BULL — the native healer who sees a living thing
- **What he wants:** to save the town by treating the plague as a wounded living organism bound to its flesh and earth.
- **What his worldview forecloses:** whatever only the rational/institutional understanding could have saved.
- **Function:** THE ORGANIC CURE: the other coherent diagnosis, mutually exclusive with the first.

### THE TOWN — the dying thing only saveable one way
- The plague-ridden settlement; different parts of it reachable only by different worldviews.
- **Function:** THE PARTIALLY-SAVEABLE: the town where committing to one cure damns what the other would have saved.

### THE PLAYER (the embodied healer) — the one who must commit
- **What they want:** to save everyone (impossible); to know which worldview is right (never granted).
- **Function:** THE COMMITTER: the one who plants their feet in one account of reality and accepts the cost.

### THE CLOCK (THE PLAGUE'S SPREAD) — the body count of hesitation
- The daily advance of the disease; the dwindling resources; the scheduled deaths.
- **Function:** THE TIME-AS-CORPSES: the mechanism that makes indecision itself a lethal choice.

## 4 THE BRANCH MAP

**COUNT: 2 worldview commitments (the rational cure or the organic cure) each saving different parts of the town and damning what the other would have saved — with the constant that the diagnoses contradict at the root (no synthesis), the clock turns hesitation into corpses (no delay), and the player never gets certainty they chose the right account of reality.**

- **THE RATIONAL CURE:** the plague treated as a containable disease; the parts of the town reachable by reason and institutions saved; what only the organic understanding could reach, lost. WRITES: one reality committed to; the Bachelor's people saved, the Haruspex's lost; no certainty it was right.
- **THE ORGANIC CURE:** the plague treated as a living thing bound to the town's flesh and earth; what only that understanding could reach, saved; the rational side's reachable, lost. WRITES: the other reality committed to; the Haruspex's people saved, the Bachelor's lost; equally uncertain.
- **THE NO-SYNTHESIS, NO-DELAY CONSTANT:** the cures can't be merged (they contradict) and can't be postponed (the clock kills) — the player must plant their feet in one account and pay for it.
- **THE THROUGH-LINE:** the branch is which-reality-do-you-commit-to — two incompatible diagnoses of the same catastrophe, and the quest insists saving anyone means damning what the other worldview would have saved, with no certainty and no time to be sure.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — THE INCOMPATIBILITY CAN READ AS ARBITRARY IF BOTH WORLDVIEWS AREN'T MADE COHERENT.** The whole power depends on both diagnoses being internally valid and partially confirmed; if one reads as obviously right or obviously mystical-nonsense, the choice collapses into "pick the correct one" and the profound "you must commit to a contested reality" idea is lost.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest forces a choice between incompatible accounts of a disaster, BOTH accounts must be made internally coherent and partially validated — neither obviously right — so the commitment is to a contested reality, not a hunt for the correct answer (#180's both-worldviews-must-hold law).

**F2 — THE CLOCK CAN PUNISH SO HARSHLY IT PRODUCES DESPAIR RATHER THAN COMMITMENT.** Pathologic's time pressure is brutal, and if it's purely punishing it can push players into paralysis or quitting rather than the intended forced commitment — the body-count clock needs to compel a choice, not just crush the player into helplessness.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest uses a body-count clock to force commitment, the pressure must COMPEL a choice, not induce paralysis — the clock making a decision urgent, not making the player give up — so time-as-corpses drives commitment rather than despair (#21's the-clock-must-compel-not-crush law).

**F3 — "YOU NEVER KNOW YOU WERE RIGHT" CAN FEEL LIKE WITHHELD INFORMATION RATHER THAN THE POINT.** The refusal to referee which worldview was correct is the thesis, but it can read as the game hiding the answer rather than as the truth that there was no shared answer to have — the uncertainty needs to be legibly the condition, not a puzzle the game won't solve.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest's point is that no worldview can be confirmed right, the un-referee-ability must be OWNED as the condition — the absence of a correct answer made the lesson, not a withheld solution — so it reads as "there was no shared reality to be right about," not "the game won't tell you" (#195's the-ambiguity-must-be-owned law).

## 6 WHY IT WORKS (W1-W10)

W1. THE DISASTER'S NATURE IS CONTESTED. The healers disagree on what the plague IS, not just how to cure it — the root incompatibility that is the whole engine.
W2. THE CURES CONTRADICT. Committing to one worldview forecloses the other — you cannot do both because they disagree about reality.
W3. SAVING ANYONE DAMNS THE OTHER'S PEOPLE. Your cure reaches some and cannot reach what the other diagnosis would have saved — the cost of committing to one reality.
W4. THE CLOCK KILLS HESITATION. The plague spreads daily; every hour of deciding is a body — refusing to commit is itself a lethal choice.
W5. THERE IS NO SYNTHESIS. The removed "combine both cures" verb is the spine: contradicting diagnoses cannot be merged, and no one is saved by trying.
W6. YOU NEVER KNOW YOU WERE RIGHT. The game refuses to referee — only the ledger of who lived, never certainty the worldview was correct.
W7. BOTH ACCOUNTS ARE COHERENT. Rational and organic are each internally valid and partly confirmed — neither obviously right, which makes the choice real.
W8. HEDGING SAVES NO ONE. Splitting the difference saves nobody, because the cures require full commitment to opposite realities.
W9. A DIFFERENT COMMITTER SAVES DIFFERENT PEOPLE. Someone choosing the other worldview, equally sure, saves and loses different people — the contingency of the ledger.
W10. IT IS INCOMPATIBLE-CURES UNDER A CLOCK IN ONE PLAGUE. Committing to a contested reality to save anyone, timed in corpses — Bohemia's whole question of irreconcilable worldviews and time-as-body-count, in a dying town.
