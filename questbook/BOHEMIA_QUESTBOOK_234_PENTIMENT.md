## 0 CORE IDEA

**An ordinary artisan investigating a murder in a small, close-knit town is forced, again and again across years and deaths, to name a culprit before a hard deadline, on evidence that is always incomplete, contradictory, and never quite conclusive — knowing that an innocent person may be executed on the strength of the accusation, that the accused's family and the whole community will carry the consequences of the verdict for decades, and that the accusation, once made, cannot be unmade — and the game never, at any point, confirms whether the person named was actually guilty, so the investigator must author a verdict they can never verify and live, along with the town, in the permanent uncertainty of whether they condemned the right person or destroyed an innocent.**

Pentiment is a game about deciding truth without the luxury of certainty. Its protagonist, Andreas, is drawn into a series of murders in the town of Tassing over many years. Each time, the structure is the same and merciless: there is pressure, a deadline, an authority demanding a name, and evidence that never fully resolves. Andreas can investigate — gather clues, hear testimony, weigh motives — but the evidence is always partial, the witnesses contradict, and no path leads to genuine proof. And yet a name must be given. When Andreas accuses, someone is taken and, often, executed. The accused may be guilty; they may be innocent; the game does not say, then or ever. The consequences ripple outward and forward: the executed person's family is marked, the town's fabric is altered, grudges and griefs are seeded that play out across generations the game lets the player live through. Years later, the town still carries what was decided in a room with incomplete evidence and a ticking clock. And the truth — whether Andreas named the killer or killed the innocent — is never confirmed, not in an epilogue, not in a reveal, not ever. The player authored a verdict on a guess and must live inside the not-knowing, exactly as the town does.

The machine is that you are forced to decide the truth without ever being able to verify it, an innocent may pay, the community carries your verdict for generations, and the game withholds confirmation permanently — so the weight is not a wrong answer but the unbearable authorship of a verdict you can never check. Pentiment refuses every comfort the mystery genre offers. There is no moment where the true killer is revealed and the player learns whether they were right; there is no undo; there is no certainty. The accusation is real, its consequences are real and generational, and its correctness is permanently unknowable. The player is placed in the exact position of anyone who has ever had to judge with incomplete information and lasting stakes: a decision must be made, it cannot be deferred, it may destroy an innocent, and you will never know. And the game's cruelest and truest stroke is time — it lets the player return to Tassing years and decades later and see the accusation still shaping lives, the family of the accused still marked, the town still living with a verdict the player authored on a guess and can still not confirm. The player carries the not-knowing as a permanent condition, which is the honest weight of every real judgment made under uncertainty. And the honesty is that most consequential decisions are made without the truth, that an accusation can be both necessary and unverifiable, that the community lives longest with the verdicts its judges were least sure of, and that the heaviest thing is not being wrong but never being allowed to know.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** most consequential judgments are made without the truth and can never be verified afterward, and the heaviest weight is not being wrong but never being allowed to know — a decision must be made, it cannot be deferred, an innocent may pay, and the community carries the verdict for generations while its correctness stays permanently unknowable; you author the truth on a guess and live in the not-knowing, exactly as everyone does who has ever had to judge with incomplete evidence and lasting stakes, and the record convicts whom you name, not whom the deed was done by.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — THE PERMANENT WITHHOLDING CAN READ AS THE GAME NOT HAVING AN ANSWER RATHER THAN AS THE POINT.** The refusal to ever confirm the killer is the thesis, but a player can experience it as the writers not bothering to resolve the mystery rather than as the deliberate condition (you judge without certainty) — so the profound "you'll never know" weight risks reading as an unsatisfying non-ending.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest's power is that the truth is permanently withheld, the un-knowability must be OWNED as the condition — the impossibility of certainty made legibly the point, the weight of judging-without-proof foregrounded — so it reads as "you must decide and never know," not "the game had no answer" (#206's the-ambiguity-must-be-owned law).

**F2 — THE FORCED DEADLINE CAN FEEL ARBITRARY IF THE PRESSURE ISN'T GROUNDED.** The "you must accuse now, on incomplete evidence" bind only lands if the pressure to name someone is structurally real (the authority, the town's demand, the danger of no verdict); if it's an arbitrary game timer, the forced guess reads as contrived rather than as the genuine impossibility of deferring a real judgment.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest forces a verdict on incomplete evidence, the PRESSURE to decide must be grounded — the real reason a name can't be deferred made legible — so the forced guess reads as a genuine bind, not an arbitrary clock (#226's the-no-defer-must-be-earned law).

**F3 — THE GENERATIONAL CONSEQUENCE CAN BE UNDERPLAYED, DRAINING THE VERDICT'S WEIGHT.** The unique power is seeing the accusation shape the town for decades; if the long-term consequences are thin or easy to miss, the "the community carries your guess for generations" idea loses force and the accusation feels like a one-time event rather than a lasting authorship.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest's weight is a verdict carried for generations, the LONG-TERM consequence must be shown and felt — the accusation's decades-long ripple made present — so the player experiences the verdict outliving them, not just a momentary choice (#187's the-consequence-must-outlive-the-choice law).
