# BOHEMIA QUESTBOOK #189 — "HEART OF STONE (GRANT A DEAD MAN'S THREE WISHES AND WATCH LOVE ROT INTO A CURSE)"
**Game:** The Witcher 3: Hearts of Stone (2015 expansion)
**Studio:** CD Projekt Red
**Quest:** Olgierd von Everec's contract — a devil buys your service to fulfill three impossible wishes for a man who long ago sold his own heart for immortality-in-life, and the final wish forces you into the wreckage of a marriage his bargain destroyed, so the player becomes the instrument of a soul-broker collecting a debt that turns out to be a love story rotted from the inside.
**Type:** INDIVIDUAL-QUEST DEEP DIVE (mined fresh 7/18 from the Witcher catalogue; corpus anchor Q09 for the game's larger machines)
**FORMAT:** v2 — cast + conversation node trees + branch map.
**Filed:** 7/18/26
**Why pulled:** Bohemia needs the quest where a soul-broker's bargain is revealed, wish by wish, to be a love story it destroyed — where the immortality a man traded his heart for made him unable to feel the wife who loved him, and the player, hired by the devil, becomes the tool that collects the human wreckage. Heart of Stone is the specimen: a Mephistopheles figure buying the player's service, three wishes that each excavate the cost of a heartless bargain, and a final choice between serving the broker or defying him for a doomed man. It is the Amalgamation's soul-bargain (#34/#175) fused with the fold's what-a-deal-costs-the-living, and the "the devil's contract is somebody's ruined marriage" machine.

## 0 CORE IDEA

**A devil — a broker of souls with courtly manners and total power — compels the player into his service and orders the fulfillment of three impossible-seeming wishes for a nobleman who, years ago, traded his own heart to this same broker in exchange for a life that cannot be ended; and as the player grants each wish, the bargain's true cost surfaces: the heartless man has become physically and emotionally stone, unable to love the wife who still loves him, so the contract the player is executing turns out to be the slow-motion collection on a love story the deal already destroyed.**

Olgierd von Everec made a bargain with Gaunter O'Dimm — a mysterious, near-omnipotent devil-figure — for a life that could not be taken from him, a heart of stone that made him invulnerable and eternal. The price, as such prices are, was disguised: his literal heart, his capacity to feel, and eventually his soul. Now O'Dimm compels the player (Geralt), through a mark of servitude, to grant Olgierd three wishes as the final movement of that old contract. The wishes seem whimsical or absurd — but each one, in the doing, opens a chamber of the ruin the bargain caused. Chief among them: the ghost of Olgierd's wife, Iris, whose love could not reach a man made of stone, whose marriage rotted not from cruelty but from a husband who literally could no longer feel her, and whose grief has calcified the house they shared into a haunted nightmare-painting of what love becomes when one person has traded away the ability to receive it. The player walks through the wreckage as the devil's instrument, granting wishes that are really exhumations, until the last movement: a choice to help O'Dimm collect Olgierd's soul, or to defy the devil and try to save a man who spent his own heart.

The machine is that the soul-bargain is not an abstraction of damnation but a specific ruined marriage, and the player is made the broker's hand in collecting it. O'Dimm is the most dangerous kind of devil — he does not force anyone to sign; he grants exactly what they ask, and lets the true cost reveal itself as the years pass, always technically fair, always catastrophic. Olgierd wanted to not be able to be hurt, and got it, and the getting cost him the only thing that made a life worth being unkillable for. The player, hired to finish the deal, sees the human debris up close: a wife who loved a statue, a house turned to grief, a man who cannot even mourn what he lost because he traded away the machinery of feeling. And the final choice is whether to serve the letter of the contract (the devil always keeps his word, and the word is damning) or to gamble on beating the broker at his own game to spare a man who is, technically, getting exactly what he agreed to. The quest's honesty is that the flashiest wish and the deepest wound are the same object, and that a bargain for invulnerability is a bargain to stop being able to love.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** the price of never being able to be hurt is never being able to be reached, and the bargains people make to stop feeling pain quietly cost them the feeling that made anything worth protecting — the devil doesn't have to cheat, he just grants exactly what you ask and lets you discover, years later and too late, that you traded away the heart along with the wound.

## 1 CAST + WHAT EACH ONE WANTS

### GAUNTER O'DIMM — the devil who only grants what you ask
- **What he wants (stated):** the three wishes fulfilled; the contract's final movement completed.
- **What he wants (real):** Olgierd's soul, collected in perfect technical fairness.
- **What he'll trade:** exactly what is asked, and no more — the terror is that he never has to cheat.
- **What he will never do:** force a signature; he grants, and lets the cost reveal itself.
- **Function:** THE FAIR-AND-DAMNING BROKER: the soul-buyer whose contracts are always kept and always ruinous.

### OLGIERD VON EVEREC — the man who traded his heart
- **What he wanted:** to never be hurt; a life that could not be taken — invulnerability, eternity.
- **What he lost:** the capacity to feel, to love, to receive the wife who loved him — the heart itself.
- **Function:** THE HEARTLESS BY BARGAIN: the man whose deal for invulnerability cost him the ability to be reached.

### IRIS VON EVEREC — the wife who loved a statue
- Olgierd's wife, whose love could not reach a man made of stone; her grief calcified into a haunted house.
- **Function:** THE RUINED LOVE: the specific marriage the abstract soul-bargain actually destroyed.

### THE PLAYER (Geralt) — the devil's collecting hand
- **What they want:** to survive the mark, to grant the wishes, and — by the end — maybe to cheat the devil for Olgierd.
- **Function:** THE INSTRUMENT: the one made to collect the human wreckage of a bargain they didn't make.

### THE THREE WISHES — the exhumations disguised as whims
- Absurd-seeming tasks that each open a chamber of the bargain's true cost.
- **Function:** THE FLASHY-WISH-IS-THE-DEEP-WOUND: the spectacle that is really an excavation of the ruin.

## 4 THE BRANCH MAP

**COUNT: 2 terminal choices at the collection — serve O'Dimm (the contract's letter kept, Olgierd's soul taken in perfect fairness) or defy the devil (beat the broker at his own game to spare a doomed man) — with the constant that the soul-bargain is a specific ruined marriage, and the heart traded for invulnerability can never be returned.**

- **SERVE THE LETTER:** O'Dimm collects Olgierd's soul; the devil's word kept; the bargain completed in technical fairness. WRITES: a man damned exactly as agreed; the player the broker's hand; the ruin finished.
- **DEFY THE DEVIL:** the player gambles against O'Dimm and spares Olgierd; the broker cheated by his own rules. WRITES: a heartless man freed from the collection; the devil beaten this once; the marriage still rotted, the heart still gone.
- **THE FAIR-AND-RUINOUS CONSTANT:** in both branches, O'Dimm never cheated — he granted exactly what Olgierd asked, and the catastrophe was the cost revealing itself; the bargain was always technically fair.
- **THE THROUGH-LINE:** the branch is letter-versus-gamble — serve the ruinous fair deal or beat it at its own game, and the quest insists the price of not being hurt was not being reached, and that no choice returns the traded heart.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — THE WISHES' WHIMSY CAN SWAMP THE EXHUMATION, SO THE COST READS AS SPECTACLE.** Two of the three wishes are large, playful set-pieces (a wild wedding, a heist-like caper), and their spectacle can so dominate that players enjoy them as fun beats and miss that each is excavating the bargain's ruin — so the "flashy wish IS the deep wound" design lands only for players who look past the fun.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest hides its deepest wound inside a spectacle, the spectacle must POINT at the wound, not just entertain — a beat inside the fun that turns the player's face to the cost — so the excavation isn't optional. A set-piece that's only fun buried the wound it was supposed to open (#185's the-comedy-must-crack law).

**F2 — O'DIMM'S "ALWAYS FAIR" TERROR DEPENDS ON THE PLAYER PARSING THE RULES.** The chilling idea (the devil never cheats, only grants and waits) requires the player to understand the contract's mechanics, and the game sometimes lets the horror ride on lore the player skimmed — so O'Dimm can read as an arbitrary powerful villain rather than the specific horror of a broker who wins by keeping his word.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a soul-broker's terror is that he's always technically fair, the FAIRNESS must be made legible in play — the player seeing that every ruin was exactly what was asked for — so the horror is "he kept his word," not "he's strong." A devil whose fairness the player can't trace is just a strong enemy (#175's the-price-must-be-legible law).

**F3 — OLGIERD IS HARD TO PITY BECAUSE HIS HEARTLESSNESS IS SHOWN AS COLDNESS, NOT LOSS.** Because a man who traded his heart is, by design, cold and off-putting, the player can struggle to feel the tragedy of Iris's marriage from his side — the "save him" choice can feel unearned, since the game shows his effect (a rotted marriage) more than his loss (a man who can't mourn what he traded).
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a character's tragedy is that they traded away feeling, the LOSS must be glimpsed under the coldness — a moment where the inability to mourn is itself mournful — so pity is possible and the save-choice is earned. A traded-away heart shown only as coldness gives the player no reason to save it (#177's the-numbness-must-ache law).

## 6 WHY IT WORKS (W1-W10)

W1. THE DEVIL NEVER CHEATS. O'Dimm grants exactly what's asked and lets the cost surface — the fair-and-damning broker, whose terror is that he keeps his word, which is the whole engine.
W2. THE BARGAIN IS A RUINED MARRIAGE. The abstract soul-deal is really a specific destroyed love — Iris, the house, the husband of stone — the human debris made vivid.
W3. INVULNERABILITY COST THE HEART. Olgierd traded the ability to be hurt and lost the ability to be reached — the sharpest thing the quest says about not-feeling.
W4. THE PLAYER IS THE COLLECTING HAND. Geralt is made the devil's instrument, walking through wreckage he didn't cause — complicity as gameplay.
W5. THE WISHES ARE EXHUMATIONS. Each absurd task opens a chamber of the bargain's ruin — the flashy surface hiding the deep wound.
W6. THE HEART DOESN'T COME BACK. The removed "give Olgierd his heart" verb is the spine: what's traded for invulnerability is gone; you only decide whether the collection completes.
W7. THE DEAL CAN BE BEATEN BY ITS RULES. Defying O'Dimm means out-riddling him, not overpowering him — the devil cheated only by his own logic.
W8. THE MARRIAGE STAYS ROTTED. Even saving Olgierd doesn't restore Iris or the love — the ruin precedes and outlasts the choice.
W9. THE COST IS DELAYED, NOT HIDDEN. Olgierd got exactly what he asked and discovered the price over years — the honest horror of a bargain that's fair and slow.
W10. IT IS THE AMALGAMATION'S DEAL IN ONE HEART. A bargain to escape pain that costs the capacity to love — Bohemia's whole soul-broker question, in one man's traded heart and one haunted house.
