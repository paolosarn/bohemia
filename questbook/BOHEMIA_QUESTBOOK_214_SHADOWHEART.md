## 0 CORE IDEA

**A woman raised from childhood by a cult devoted to the goddess of loss and secrets has been trained to prove her faith by surrendering her own memories, her mercy, and her past — taught that giving up pieces of herself is the highest devotion — and over the course of her journey she discovers the horror beneath her piety: the painful history she can't remember, and even her own parents, were not lost by accident but deliberately taken from her by the cult that claims to have saved her, her whole self surgically and spiritually removed to make her a perfect instrument, and the cult's final loyalty test is to have her murder the captive parents she does not remember, so she must choose between completing the erasure by killing them or reclaiming a self her entire life was spent deleting.**

Shadowheart is a devotee of Shar, goddess of loss, darkness, and forgetting. Her cult teaches that pain and memory are burdens to be surrendered, that true devotion is the willing erasure of the self — and Shadowheart has been giving herself away for as long as she can remember, which is not very long, because the cult has been taking her memories as the price of her faith. She carries wounds she can't explain, a fear of the sun, a tenderness she was trained to suppress. As her arc unfolds, the truth surfaces: her lost past was stolen, not misplaced; the cult engineered her forgetting; the very devotion she is proud of is the mechanism of her own deletion. And the cult delivers its ultimate test: her parents, whom she does not remember, are held captive, and to complete her ascension as Shar's chosen she must kill them — a final surrender of the last piece of the self she never got to keep. The player, as her companion, is present at the choice: help her plunge the knife and complete the erasure (becoming the perfect instrument, empty and devoted), or help her stay her hand, refuse the goddess, and begin the agonizing work of reclaiming a self that a lifetime of engineered forgetting has nearly dissolved.

The machine is that the devotion was the erasure, and reclaiming yourself means reversing an institution that spent your whole life deleting you — with the family you don't remember as the final test. Shadowheart's tragedy is that she cannot even mourn what she lost, because the losing was done to her memory itself; the cult did not just take her past, it took her ability to know it was taken, and dressed the theft as holiness. The genius of the loyalty test is its cruelty: killing the parents is framed not as a sin but as the completion of a virtue, the last and highest act of a devotion she has been practicing her entire life. To refuse is not simply to spare two strangers; it is to reject the entire meaning she was raised to have, to declare that the self the cult deleted was worth keeping, to choose the agony of remembering over the peace of erasure. The player helps her see that her devotion was her own dismantling, that the "loss" she worships is the loss of herself, and that her parents are not a test of her faith but proof of what her faith cost her. And the honesty is that an institution can take your very self, piece by piece, and make you grateful for the taking — and that reclaiming who you were is not a moment of revelation but a long, painful reversal against a lifetime of engineered forgetting, begun by refusing the final erasure you were trained to complete.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** an institution can delete your self piece by piece and teach you to call the deletion devotion, taking not just your past but your ability to know it was taken — and the cruelest loyalty test is the one that frames the murder of what you love as the completion of a virtue you're proud of; reclaiming who you were is never a single revelation but a long, painful reversal against a lifetime of engineered forgetting, and it begins the moment you refuse the last erasure they trained you to perform.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — THE RECLAMATION CAN RESOLVE TOO FAST, MAKING "A LIFETIME OF ERASURE" FEEL UNDONE IN A SCENE.** The power is that reclaiming the self is a long, painful reversal, but if refusing the goddess instantly restores Shadowheart to a whole, healed person, the profound "engineered forgetting can't be undone in a moment" idea collapses into a one-scene cure.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a character's self was deleted over a lifetime, reclaiming it must be shown as SLOW and partial — the reversal ongoing, the wounds still present after the refusal — so "you can't undo a lifetime in a moment" holds (#186's the-reclamation-is-gradual law).

**F2 — THE MURDER-AS-VIRTUE FRAMING CAN BE UNDERSOLD, MAKING THE TEST READ AS OBVIOUS EVIL.** The test's genius is that killing the parents is framed as the completion of a virtue Shadowheart is proud of, not an obvious sin; if the game presents it as plainly evil, the horror (she was trained to want this) is lost and refusing becomes a trivial good-choice.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a loyalty test frames an atrocity as the completion of a devotion, that FRAMING must be made convincing — the act genuinely feeling like the highest virtue from inside the faith — so refusing costs the whole meaning of her life, not just a moral point (#179's the-atrocity-must-feel-holy law).

**F3 — THE PARENTS CAN BE PLOT DEVICES RATHER THAN PEOPLE, WEAKENING THE PROOF-OF-COST.** The parents work as "proof of what her faith cost her" only if they read as real, specific people with a real relationship stolen; if they're generic captives, sparing them is abstract and the "evidence of the erasure's price" weight is lost.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if captives are the proof of what an erasure cost, they must be VIVID enough that their reality indicts the theft — specific people, a stolen bond glimpsed — so sparing them is reclaiming something real, not freeing tokens (#204's the-loved-ones-must-be-real law).

## 6 WHY IT WORKS (W1-W10)

W1. THE DEVOTION WAS THE ERASURE. Shadowheart's faith was the mechanism of her own deletion — giving herself away was the theft, which is the whole engine.
W2. THEY TOOK HER ABILITY TO MOURN. The cult stole not just her past but her knowledge it was taken — she can't grieve what she can't remember losing.
W3. THE MURDER IS FRAMED AS VIRTUE. Killing the parents is the completion of a devotion she's proud of — the loyalty test's cruelest design.
W4. THE PARENTS ARE PROOF, NOT A TEST. They are evidence of what her faith cost her — the erasure's price made flesh.
W5. RECLAMATION IS SLOW. The removed "give back her self in a moment" verb is the spine: a lifetime of forgetting can't be undone in an instant.
W6. REFUSING REJECTS HER WHOLE MEANING. To stay her hand is to reject everything she was raised to be — not a small choice but a total one.
W7. THE ERASURE WAS DRESSED AS GRACE. The cult made her grateful for her own deletion — the horror of an institution that thanks you for disappearing.
W8. THE CRACKS WERE ALWAYS THERE. Her fear of the sun, her suppressed tenderness — the self resisting its own erasure, findable if looked for.
W9. THE PLAYER HELPS HER SEE. The companion's role is to reveal the devotion as dismantling — the witness to a reclamation.
W10. IT IS ENGINEERED-FORGETTING IN ONE DEVOTEE. A self deleted piece by piece and called faith — Bohemia's whole Amalgamation question of the taken self, in a cult of loss.
