**Game:** Cyberpunk 2077 (2020)

**Quest:** River Ward's "I Fought the Law" — a suspended NCPD detective pulls you into an off-the-books murder investigation of a councilman, and the deeper you dig the clearer it gets that the department, the family, and the corporation all already know who did it and have decided the truth will not be allowed to matter, so solving it perfectly changes nothing the power structure didn't pre-approve.

**FORMAT:** v2 — cast + conversation node trees + branch map.

**Why pulled:** Bohemia needs the quest where the investigation is real and the verdict is pre-decided — where doing the detective work perfectly runs you straight into a wall the powerful built before you started, and the truth you find is true and inert because the system already chose what the answer would be allowed to be. "I Fought the Law" is the specimen: a genuine murder, a genuine solve, and a machine of family, department, and corp that has already agreed the truth will not change the outcome. It is the unrecorded-ledger (#26) as a police procedural, and the "you can be completely right and completely powerless" machine our factions can carry.

## 0 CORE IDEA

**A suspended cop drags you into an unofficial investigation of a murdered city councilman, and every clue you uncover is real and every deduction is correct — and it slowly becomes clear that the department, the dead man's family, and the corporation behind it all already know the truth and have decided, before you ever started, that it will not be allowed to matter, so you solve the case perfectly and the solution changes nothing the power structure didn't pre-approve.**

River Ward is an NCPD detective, suspended, working a case the department has effectively closed: a city councilman found dead. He needs an outsider — you — to run the investigation the badge won't let him run officially. The detective work is genuine: crime scenes, braindance forensics, a trail through corporate security footage, a real chain of evidence leading to a real culprit and a real motive tangled in city politics and corporate money. You are good at it. You find the truth. And as you close on it, the shape of the thing you're actually inside becomes visible: the police brass, the councilman's own family, and the corporation whose interests the murder touched have all already reached the same conclusion you have — and have all already agreed that the conclusion will be buried, spun, or pinned on a disposable party, because the truth is inconvenient to everyone with the power to act on it. Your perfect solve is delivered into a system that decided its verdict before the body was cold.

The machine is that being right is not the same as having power, and an investigation can be completely honest and completely inert. The quest gives the player the full satisfaction of the procedural — the clues, the deductions, the moment it all clicks — and then removes the payoff every procedural promises: that the truth, once found, does something. Here the truth does nothing the powerful didn't pre-authorize. River gets no vindication, no reinstatement, no justice for the dead; he gets confirmation that the system he still half-believes in was never going to let his correctness count. The player leaves having solved it and changed nothing, holding a true answer the world has already agreed to ignore. The quest lets the player help River anyway (a private solidarity between two people the system outranks) or recognize the futility and disengage, and it refuses to grant the fantasy that a good enough investigation beats a decided-enough system.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** the truth and the power to make the truth matter are two different things, and you can do the work perfectly, find the real answer, and watch it change nothing — because a system that decided the verdict before you started was never going to be beaten by your being right, only by having the power it already made sure you don't.

- The clues, forensics, and deductions that click perfectly and then do nothing.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — THE FUTILITY CAN READ AS THE WRITERS GIVING UP, NOT THE SYSTEM WINNING.** Because the quest deliberately denies the procedural payoff, a player can experience the ending as an anticlimax or a writing failure ("nothing happened") rather than as the intended point (the truth is inert against power) — so the profound idea risks landing as a dropped thread instead of a designed wall.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** when a quest denies the expected payoff on purpose, the DENIAL must be legibly the system's doing, not the story's absence — the wall shown as a wall, the powers' pre-decision made concrete — so "the truth doesn't matter here" reads as the point, not a plot that fizzled. An intentional anticlimax that looks like a mistake taught nothing (#169's the-refusal-must-be-legible law).

**F2 — RIVER'S ARC ABSORBS THE THEME INTO A PERSONAL STORY, SOFTENING THE SYSTEMIC POINT.** The quest is also the start of River's romance/friendship arc, so the systemic horror (power decides the truth) can get folded into a warm character beat (you and River against the world), which comforts the edge off the idea that being right is powerless.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest's point is systemic powerlessness, the personal solidarity must not ANESTHETIZE it — the human bond can coexist with the horror but must not resolve it, the system staying unbeaten even as the two people connect — so the comfort doesn't cancel the critique. A systemic wound healed by a hug is a critique the story took back (#173's the-bond-doesn't-fix-the-system law).

**F3 — THE PRE-DECISION IS TOLD MORE THAN DEMONSTRATED.** That the department, family, and corp all already decided is largely conveyed through implication and River's conclusions rather than shown as concrete, felt collusion — so the wall is more asserted than experienced, and the "verdict predates the investigation" idea depends on the player accepting a summary rather than hitting the machine themselves.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest's spine is a pre-decided verdict, the collusion must be DEMONSTRATED — the player hitting the closed doors, the spiked report, the pre-written outcome — so the wall is experienced, not summarized. A pre-decision the player is told about is a wall painted on paper (#181's the-consequence-must-be-shown law).

## 6 WHY IT WORKS (W1-W10)

W1. THE INVESTIGATION IS GENUINE. Real clues, real forensics, a real solve — the procedural is honest, which is what makes its inertness land.
W2. THE VERDICT PREDATES THE WORK. Department, family, and corp decided before the player started — the pre-decided verdict, which is the quest's whole engine.
W3. BEING RIGHT ISN'T POWER. The player solves it perfectly and changes nothing — the sharpest form of "correctness and power are different things."
W4. THE PAYOFF IS DELIBERATELY REMOVED. Every procedural promises the truth will matter; this one denies it on purpose, which is the quest's honest cruelty.
W5. RIVER STILL HALF-BELIEVES. The honest cop who wants the truth to count — his disillusion is the human face of the systemic point.
W6. THE TRUTH IS TRUE AND INERT. The correct answer the world has already agreed to ignore — the unrecorded-ledger (#26) as a police case.
W7. THE SYSTEM ISN'T BEATEN. The removed "make the truth matter" verb is the spine: a perfect solve does not overturn a decided-enough machine.
W8. THE SOLIDARITY IS PRIVATE, NOT VICTORIOUS. Standing with River changes nothing systemic — the bond is real and the wall is intact, which is the honest version of "at least we have each other."
W9. THE POWERS ARE PLURAL. Not one villain but department, family, and corp all agreeing — the wall is a structure, not a person, which is what makes it unbeatable by being right.
W10. IT IS POWERLESSNESS IN ONE CASE. You can be completely right and completely unable to make it count — Bohemia's whole question of who the ledger serves, staged as a murder nobody with power will let you solve out loud.
