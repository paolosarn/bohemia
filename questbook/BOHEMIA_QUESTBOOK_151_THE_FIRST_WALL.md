**Game:** Kenshi (2018)
**Studio:** Lo-Fi Games (Chris Hunt, near-solo)
**Quest:** the game's second great unauthored story: planting a base in an indifferent world — and discovering that the moment you own a wall, the world's politics acquire your address. Tax collectors from the local power, raids scaled to your visible wealth, neighbors with opinions, and the first siege your own people stand or break on. #146 mined the arc of losing everything; this file mines the arc of finally having something to lose.

## 0 CORE IDEA

**The world ignores you perfectly until you build a wall. Then it sends the bill.**

Kenshi's world is famously indifferent: factions war, patrol, and trade without the player mattering at all (#146, Q51's extreme cousin). A wandering squad is weather — the world moves around it. The outpost story begins the moment the player converts wealth into GEOGRAPHY: a house, a wall, a well, a farm, in the open wasteland. And the indifference ENDS — not as a script, but as arithmetic: a base is visible, taxable, raidable, and permanent. The wandering nobody was nobody; the landowner is a LINE ITEM in every nearby power's books.

What arrives, in reliable order, is the world's full political stack, previously invisible: the local power's collectors (pay tribute or be "visited"); the hungry (raiders scaled to visible prosperity); the ideological (faction patrols who now have a fixed place to disapprove of); and the desperate (starving wanderers at the gate — the settlement as magnet, threat and promise both). None of it is quest content. All of it is the same faction simulation that was always running, finally given a fixed address to interact with. The player who was learning the world's rotations from outside (#146's stage 3) is now the institution whose rotations others learn.

And the arc's emotional payload is the pool's investment law at settlement scale: every wall was hauled, every recruit leveled from nothing, every crop is hours — so the FIRST SIEGE, when it comes, is not a tower-defense wave. It is everything the player built, standing in one place, unable to run for the first time in the whole game. Kenshi's cruelest and truest design decision: mobility was the player's real armor all along, and building is the voluntary surrender of it. The wall that keeps the world out is the same wall that keeps you findable, and the story of every outpost is the story of that trade.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** you can stay free or you can build something, and the price of the building is that the world finally knows where you live.**

## 1 CAST + WHAT EACH ONE WANTS

**Roles, not names — the simulation casts them (the .bq model, per #146).**

### THE FOUNDER (the player-squad) — the new address
- **What they want:** to stop hauling everything on their backs. Autonomy, food security, a forge of their own — the whole survival economy internalized.
- **What they will never say out loud (the design says it for them):** that the base is also a flag. Building is a SPEECH ACT in a simulated polity: it announces wealth, permanence, and defiability, and the world reads all three.
- **Function:** THE INSTITUTION, newborn. The player crosses from #146's student-of-institutions to proprietor-of-one, and every lesson runs in reverse.

### THE LANDOWNERS (the local power's collectors) — the bill
- **What they want:** tribute. A cut of existing, delivered on schedule, backed by a garrison.
- **What they'll trade:** being left alone — protection in its oldest sense.
- **What they will never say out loud:** that the tax IS the world's recognition. The collector at the gate is the first NPC in the whole game who came looking for YOU — insult and milestone in one visit.
- **Function:** THE FIRST POLITICS. Ownership's welcome committee.

### THE RAIDERS — the appetite
- **What they want:** the visible surplus. Raid scale tracks prosperity; the better the base, the better the target — success as its own antagonist-generator.
- **Function:** THE PRESSURE that converts a building project into a story: walls become choices (what's inside them), gates become theses (who they open for).

### THE RECRUITS — the manufactured attachment
- **What they want:** food, wages, safety; each a nobody leveled by the player's hours into someone load-bearing.
- **What they will never say out loud:** nothing — Kenshi barely lets them speak, and the attachment forms anyway, from pure invested labor (the pool's investment law). The siege's stakes are these unwritten people.
- **Function:** THE STAKES, headcount edition. The settlement's health bar is made of names the player trained.

### THE GATE — cast as a character, per corpus convention
- The base's one hole; the object every arriving party addresses first.
- **What it wants:** a policy. Open for traders and the starving? Shut against collectors? The gate is a standing question the player answers daily.
- **Function:** THE THESIS OBJECT. A settlement's ethics compress into its door rules — Bohemia's LIGHT=TERRITORY law has a sibling here: THE GATE IS THE POSTURE.

## 2 FULL EVENT FLOW (STAGE BY STAGE)

**Emergent and statistically stable (the #146 methodology): the stages are system states in the order the arithmetic reliably produces them.**

### STAGE 1 — THE PURCHASE OF PERMANENCE [player-initiated]
Entry: enough wealth and materials; a site chosen in the open world (resources, water, and — unknowingly, the first time — WHOSE territory it sits in).
What changes: the squad acquires an address. The game's deepest state-flag flips silently: FINDABLE.

### STAGE 2 — THE FIRST VISITS [systemic, reliable]
The collectors arrive (tribute demand, deadline, garrison behind it); prospectors, traders, and the starving follow. The world's map of itself now includes the player, and every nearby ledger opens an account.
MISSABLE: nothing — the visits are arithmetic. What varies is the ORDER, which teaches whose territory the site really was.

### STAGE 3 — THE POLICY ERA [the long middle]
Pay or defy the tax; open or shut the gate; feed or turn away the desperate; arm the walls or hide the wealth. Every policy writes faction state (pay → protected-and-subordinate; defy → the raid calendar acquires a new entry). The base's LAYOUT becomes strategy (kill-boxes, inner walls, food stores) — the player's values, expressed in architecture, readable by anyone who breaches the outer wall.

### STAGE 4 — THE FIRST SIEGE [the arc's crown, systemically guaranteed by any defiance]
The raid that is actually a bill: the defied power, or the appetite the prosperity fed. What makes it a STORY: nothing can run. The squad that survived everything by mobility stands on its investment, and the fight happens exactly where the player's hours live — the forge they built, the wall they placed, the recruits they trained, all in the same frame as the consequence.
Exit: stood or broken. Both continue (Kenshi's #146 law: defeat continues) — a broken base is looted, burned, its people scattered, enslaved, or dead, and the world moves on, indifferent again.

### STAGE 5 — THE STANDING, OR THE ASH [both real, both continuations]
Stood: the base's reputation hardens; raids re-price; the settlement enters the region's arithmetic as a POWER, however small — patrols route past it, traders schedule it. Ash: the survivors' arc re-enters #146's territory (capture, scatter, the crawl), carrying the lesson every Kenshi veteran repeats: build later than you think, walls buy nothing you can't defend, and the world was never going to applaud.

## 3 THE CONVERSATIONS (THE ACTUAL MACHINE)

### NODE O-1 — the collector at the gate. Speaker: the local power. Entry: stage 2.
The demand, flat and administrative: this land is theirs, the number is this, the schedule is this.
> pay [gate: the money] -> protected-and-subordinate: raids from THIS power stop; the tribute line recurs forever; the settlement is now a client
> refuse [gate: none] -> the visit's polite version ends; the raid calendar begins. Defiance is priced openly and honestly — the collector explains the consequence like a utility company
> stall / negotiate [gate: none, thin] -> Kenshi's dialogue is minimal by design; the stall buys days, not terms. (See F2 — the thinness is the flaw AND the finding)
> (shut the gate and say nothing) [gate: none] SILENCE -> read as refusal, minus the dignity. The world's politics accept silence as an answer and bill accordingly
WHAT THIS NODE COSTS: the illusion of neutrality. There is no rate at which the settlement stays nobody's business; the first visit forecloses unaffiliation forever.

### NODE O-2 — the gate, daily. Speaker: whoever arrives. Entry: stage 3, standing.
> open for traders [gate: none] -> the economy's oxygen, and intelligence leaking both ways (a trader who walked your streets can describe them)
> open for the starving [gate: food surplus] -> recruits, gratitude, mouths — the settlement as promise. The wanderers the player once was, at the gate the player now owns
> shut against all [gate: none] -> safety's costume: no economy, no recruits, and the walls read as wealth-worth-hiding to every scout who passes
WHAT THIS NODE COSTS: each policy is legible from outside. The gate's pattern IS the settlement's reputation — posture broadcast by architecture (the corpus's cheapest reputation system: no meter, just what people saw the door do).

### NODE O-3 — the siege, as conversation. Speaker: the world, at the wall. Entry: stage 4.
> stand on the walls [gate: everything built] -> the investment defends itself or doesn't; every casualty is a recruit the player leveled by hand
> abandon the base mid-siege [gate: none — always available, and the arc's TRAP-shaped mercy] -> the squad survives, mobile again; the hours die in place. The option's existence is the design's honesty: freedom was always purchasable, at the price of everything static
> (no surrender node exists) — NOVERB "we yield, take a cut": Kenshi's raiders and garrisons accept no mid-siege terms; the negotiation verb died when the deadline did. THE REMOVED VERB teaches the arc's timing lesson: terms exist BEFORE sieges, never during (O-1 was the negotiation; this is the collections department)
WHAT THIS NODE COSTS: stood or fled, the settlement's people learn what the player values — and so does the player. The siege is the founder's conduct book (#143), written in one night.

## 4 THE BRANCH MAP

**COUNT: no authored endings; 4 stable attractor states, multiplied by the tribute posture and the gate policy — and the real branch surface is WHERE the wall went up (whose ledger the address landed in).**

- **THE CLIENT SETTLEMENT:** pay the tribute; the local power's raids never come; its enemies' still might. Safety as subordination, priced monthly. A real ending many veterans choose on purpose.
- **THE DEFIANT FREEHOLD:** refuse; stand the sieges; re-price the raids by winning. The arc's heroic attractor, and its costliest — the freehold's graveyard is its founding document.
- **THE ASH:** broken by the siege; survivors scattered into #146's arcs. The world's default verdict on premature walls, and the community's most-told cautionary testimony.
- **THE HIDDEN HOLE:** build small, dark, and poor-looking; evade the arithmetic by being a bad line item. The stealth answer at settlement scale — real, viable, and the quietest confession that visibility was always the price.
- **CROSS-CUTTING — THE SITE:** the same base in different territories produces different stories (whose collectors, whose enemies, what routes pass). The founding choice IS the campaign; everything after is consequence.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — THE SIEGE AI COLLAPSES INTO PATTERNS.** Raids path predictably, funnel into kill-boxes, and veteran bases become farms that harvest their own attackers — the world's terrifying bill matures into a turret-fed income stream. The pool banked the general seams; at outpost scale the seam is fatal: the arc's crown event becomes a routine the player designed once.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** siege-class threats must read the defense and vary the approach (composition, timing, target selection — burn the crops, not the kill-box), or cap their own frequency below routine-formation. A siege the player can schedule is a subscription, not a story. (Bohemia's beat-world and faction sim can vary by LAW: attackers who learned the last siege's shape — the #146 curriculum property, pointed back at the player.)

**F2 — THE POLITICS HAVE ONE VERB.** The collectors demand, the raiders raid — but there is no envoy TO them: no tribute renegotiation, no alliance offer, no buying a raid's redirection onto a rival. The settlement is addressable by everyone and can address no one; the world's politics remain read-only, and the arc's midgame thins into pay-or-fight.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** ownership must open VERBS, not just liabilities. The moment the settlement enters the world's books, the player gets the ledger's other side: envoys, terms, redirections, joint defenses — the faction graph is already typed for it. A settlement that can be billed but cannot negotiate is scenery with a health bar.

**F3 — THE INTERIOR LIFE IS SILENT.** The recruits the siege spends have no reaction to any of it — no one resents the defied tax, mourns the burned forge, or credits the held wall. The attachment is real (the investment law) but entirely player-side; the settlement never speaks about itself, and post-siege mornings are emotionally load-bearing moments the game leaves mute.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** the settlement testifies. Bohemia's citizen web (#149) plus bark-scale reactions give the interior a voice: the fold logs the siege, the residents reference it, the gate's history is spoken of. One night of consequence should echo in the settlement's text for a generation — that echo is what Kenshi's silence proves is missing.

## 6 WHY IT WORKS (W1-W10)

W1. OWNERSHIP IS AN ADDRESS. Building flips one flag — findable — and the entire dormant political simulation acquires a place to happen. The cheapest possible trigger for the deepest possible consequence-class.
W2. THE TAX IS THE RECOGNITION. The collector's visit is insult and milestone in one: the world's first acknowledgment that the player EXISTS arrives as a bill. No fanfare could land harder than an invoice.
W3. RAIDS SCALE WITH VISIBLE PROSPERITY. Success generates its own antagonists by arithmetic — the difficulty curve is the player's balance sheet, and modesty becomes a viable defensive strategy. Wealth as aggro is post-collapse truth, mechanized.
W4. BUILDING IS THE SURRENDER OF MOBILITY. The game's real armor was always the ability to leave; the base trades it away voluntarily. The arc's dread flows from one honest exchange rate, never from a script.
W5. THE GATE IS THE POSTURE. Door policy broadcasts the settlement's ethics to everyone who approaches — reputation without a meter, values as architecture. (LIGHT=TERRITORY's sibling law, found in the wild.)
W6. THE SIEGE HAPPENS WHERE THE HOURS LIVE. The fight's arena is the player's own labor, so every breach costs biography. No authored setpiece can buy the stakes that hauling your own walls builds for free.
W7. THE FLEE OPTION NEVER CLOSES. Abandoning everything mid-siege stays on the table, always — and its standing availability is what makes staying a CHOICE instead of a checkpoint. (The Q140 off-ramp law, at settlement scale.)
W8. THE SCALE-SHIFT IS SEAMLESS. Lone survivor to squad to settlement in one unbroken system-set — no mode switch, no genre seam. The proof that the fold's arc (dynast → settlement → city) can be ONE game, not three bolted together.
W9. ATTACHMENT IS MANUFACTURED BY LABOR. Unwritten recruits become devastating losses purely through invested hours — the pool's investment law, and the siege's ammunition. Authored backstory is optional; leveled-by-hand is not.
W10. THE WORLD NEVER APPLAUDS. Stand the siege and nothing congratulates you; the region just quietly re-prices. The indifference that made #146's captivity honest makes the freehold's survival honest too — and hands Bohemia the same lesson twice: the LEDGER must witness what the world won't (#146 F1, confirmed at scale).

## 7 BOHEMIA PORTS

### PORT 1 — THE FINDABLE FLAG [W1, W2 — the core port]
**System:** city-builder core / the 13 factions / CLUSTERED POWER / territory
Bohemia's settlement mechanics adopt the address law: the moment the player's plot crosses visibility thresholds (walls, light, smoke, traffic — LIGHT=TERRITORY already makes light a broadcast), faction arithmetic acquires the address: collectors, scouts, the desperate, on schedules the faction sim prices from visible prosperity. The world's first acknowledgment arrives as a demand. Mechanism mine; which factions collect, and at what rates, is canon.
```
@TALK collector_first_visit speaker=collector entry=settlement:findable
  @SAY Nice wall. New wall. New walls mean somebody finally has something worth counting.
  @SAY So we counted. The number's on the paper. The schedule's under the number.
  @OPT "We'll pay."                    [gate: the number] -> tribute_client
  @OPT "This ground isn't yours to bill." [gate: none] -> tribute_defied  @DO set_flag defied_collector
  @OPT "Who exactly is 'we'?"           [gate: none] -> collector_named   @DO set_flag read_the_power
  @OPT (shut the gate)                  [gate: none] SILENCE -> tribute_defied @DO set_flag silent_refusal
  @NOVERB "Make us nobody again"
@END
```
**THE NOVERB IS THE PORT** (O-1's cost): findability never reverses. The gate can shut; the address stays on the books.

### PORT 2 — THE LEDGER'S OTHER SIDE [F2's law]
**System:** faction graph / envoy verbs / the .bq format
Ownership opens verbs: once billed, the settlement can SEND — envoys, terms, tribute renegotiation, raid redirection onto rivals, joint-defense pacts — along the faction graph's typed edges. Compile rule: every threat-class relationship a settlement can RECEIVE must have at least one player-initiable verb pointing back. Read-only politics don't ship.

### PORT 3 — SIEGES THAT LEARN [F1's law]
**System:** the dial / faction memory / the fold
Siege-class attackers read the previous siege's fold entry (the breach point, the kill-box, the casualty ledger) and vary: composition, timing, target — burn the granary the defense wasn't built around. The #146 curriculum property pointed back at the player: the world's institutions also train on failure. Frequency-capped so sieges stay events, never subscriptions.
