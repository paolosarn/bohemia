# BOHEMIA QUESTBOOK #193 — "SINNERMAN (A MURDERER PAYS TO BE CRUCIFIED AND ASKS YOU TO HELP HIM MEAN IT)"
**Game:** Cyberpunk 2077 (2020)
**Studio:** CD Projekt Red
**Quest:** Sinnerman / They Won't Go When I Go — a condemned murderer, forgiven by his victim's widow, has sold his own execution to a media company that will crucify him on camera for a livestreamed martyrdom, and the player is drawn into escorting him to his death and then witnessing a filmed crucifixion, forced to decide whether a man has the right to seek his own agonizing atonement and whether watching it is reverence or complicity in spectacle.
**Type:** INDIVIDUAL-QUEST DEEP DIVE (mined fresh 7/18 from the Cyberpunk 2077 catalogue; corpus anchor Q10 for the game's larger machines)
**FORMAT:** v2 — cast + conversation node trees + branch map.
**Filed:** 7/18/26
**Why pulled:** Bohemia needs the quest where a guilty man seeks the most extreme atonement imaginable and asks the player to help him reach it — where the question is not whether he's guilty but whether a person has the right to choose their own suffering as penance, and whether the media turning his death into content makes it holy, obscene, or both. Sinnerman is the specimen: a murderer forgiven by the widow, a real crucifixion sold as a stream, and a player positioned as escort, witness, and possibly participant in a filmed death. It is the conduct-book's atonement question (#143) fused with the spectacle-of-suffering machine, and the "does a person own the right to their own punishment" law.

## 0 CORE IDEA

**A man on death row who murdered someone in a botched robbery has found faith in prison and wants to atone in the most literal and extreme way possible — by being crucified — and has sold the rights to his own execution to a corporation that will stage the crucifixion as a livestreamed religious spectacle; the victim's widow has, astonishingly, forgiven him and even participates; and the player is hired into the machinery of this death, escorting the condemned man toward it, drawn into conversation with him about guilt and grace, and finally positioned to witness or even assist the filmed crucifixion, forced to decide whether a person has the right to choose their own agony as penance and whether being present is reverence or complicity in obscene spectacle.**

Joshua Stephenson killed a man. He does not dispute it. In prison he converted, and his conversion took an extreme form: he believes his sin requires the ultimate expiation, and he has arranged, through a media corporation, to be literally crucified — nailed to a cross — on camera, as a livestreamed martyrdom that the company will monetize as transgressive religious content. The victim's widow has forgiven Joshua, genuinely, and is involved in the production. The player is pulled into the logistics: escorting Joshua, protecting the shoot, talking with him. And Joshua is not raving. He is calm, articulate, and completely sincere. He wants this. He believes it is the only proportionate answer to what he did, and he asks the player, in various ways, to help him do it and to be a real witness rather than a squeamish bystander. The corporation wants the footage. The widow wants, somehow, peace. And the player is left holding a question with no comfortable answer: is Joshua exercising a profound right to atone, or being exploited by a company that has commodified his guilt; is watching his crucifixion an act of respect for his choice or participation in a snuff-spectacle dressed as faith; and does a person own the right to their own punishment even when it is this?

The machine is that a man's sincere atonement and a corporation's obscene content are the same event, and the player cannot separate them. Every frame of Joshua's crucifixion is simultaneously his genuine, chosen expiation and the media company's monetized transgression. The quest refuses to resolve which it "really" is, because it is really both: Joshua means it completely, and the corporation is filming it completely, and the widow's forgiveness is real, and the stream's viewership is real. The player can go along — escort, protect, witness, even (in the extreme) participate in the act — treating Joshua as a man exercising his right to his own atonement; or recoil, refuse, try to stop it, treating him as a victim of a spectacle that should not be allowed regardless of his consent. And the quest's genius is that it makes the player feel the pull of both: Joshua's calm sincerity makes stopping him feel like denying a man his one path to peace; the corporation's cameras make helping him feel like crewing a snuff film. There is no reading of the crucifixion that is only holy or only obscene, because it is a real atonement and a real product at once, and the player's presence is both reverence and complicity, inseparably.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** a person's sincere reckoning with their own guilt and someone else's exploitation of it can be the exact same event, and being present at another's chosen suffering is reverence and complicity at once — the right to atone, the right to forgive, and the wrong of turning agony into content all occupy the same frame, and you cannot stand in it cleanly on either side.

## 1 CAST + WHAT EACH ONE WANTS

### JOSHUA STEPHENSON — the murderer who wants to be crucified
- **What he wants (stated):** to atone for his murder in the most literal, extreme way — to be crucified.
- **What he wants (real):** proportion — an expiation as total as his sin; peace through ultimate penance.
- **What he'll trade:** his life, his agony, on camera, believing it is the only right answer.
- **What he insists:** that this is his choice, sincerely made, and that the player be a real witness.
- **Function:** THE SELF-CONDEMNED SEEKING PROPORTION: the guilty man exercising a right to his own extreme atonement.

### THE MEDIA CORPORATION — the buyer of the death
- The company staging the crucifixion as monetized transgressive content; the exploiter of the guilt.
- **Function:** THE COMMODIFIER: the power turning sincere atonement into spectacle and product.

### THE WIDOW — the forgiveness that complicates everything
- The victim's wife, who has genuinely forgiven Joshua and participates in the production.
- **Function:** THE REAL GRACE: the forgiveness that makes the event neither simple exploitation nor simple justice.

### THE PLAYER (V) — the witness who is also complicit
- **What they want:** to know whether to help, stop, or watch; to stand cleanly somewhere (there is no clean spot).
- **Function:** THE REVERENT-COMPLICIT: the one whose presence is respect and participation at once.

### THE CRUCIFIXION / THE STREAM — the atonement that is also content
- The filmed death; genuine expiation and monetized obscenity in the same frame.
- **Function:** THE INSEPARABLE EVENT: the act that is holy and obscene simultaneously, unresolvable.

## 4 THE BRANCH MAP

**COUNT: 2 terminal postures — be present at / assist the crucifixion (honor Joshua's chosen atonement, and crew a monetized death) or refuse / try to stop it (reject the spectacle, and deny a man his sincere penance) — with the constant that the atonement and the exploitation are the same inseparable event, and no stance is clean.**

- **REVERENCE / PRESENCE:** the player honors Joshua's right to his extreme atonement, is a real witness, perhaps assists. WRITES: a man's chosen penance respected; the player complicit in a filmed, sold death; reverence and participation inseparable.
- **REFUSAL / DISRUPTION:** the player rejects the spectacle, refuses or tries to stop it. WRITES: the snuff-stream denied; a sincere man refused his one path to peace; protection that is also paternalism.
- **THE INSEPARABLE-EVENT CONSTANT:** in both branches, the crucifixion is genuine atonement and monetized obscenity at once, and the widow's forgiveness is real — no reading makes it only holy or only exploitative.
- **THE THROUGH-LINE:** the branch is honor-the-atonement versus reject-the-spectacle — two ways to stand in a frame that is reverence and complicity at once, and the quest refuses to let the player extract the sincere penance from the sold content.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — THE CORPORATION'S OBVIOUS EVIL CAN TIP THE PLAYER TOWARD "STOP IT," FLATTENING THE DILEMMA.** The media company is cartoonishly exploitative, which makes "refuse the spectacle" the easy read and can drown out Joshua's genuinely difficult claim to his own atonement — so the profound question (does he own the right to his penance) risks collapsing into "obviously the corp is bad," losing the harder half.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest weighs a person's sincere choice against its exploitation, the exploiter must not be so cartoonish that it decides the question — the sincere claim kept as strong as the exploitation is ugly — so the dilemma stays balanced rather than tipping on villain-obviousness (#180's both-sides-articulated law).

**F2 — THE PLAYER'S DEGREE OF PARTICIPATION IS UNDER-SPECIFIED, SO COMPLICITY BLURS.** How much the player actually does — witness, protect, or physically assist — shifts the moral weight enormously, but the quest can leave the player's exact role fuzzy, so the "reverence and complicity" idea loses its edge when the player isn't sure how implicated they are.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest's weight is the player's complicity in another's suffering, the DEGREE of participation must be legible — the player knowing exactly what their presence does — so the reverence-complicity tension is sharp rather than blurred. Complicity the player can't measure is a weight that doesn't land (#181's the-participation-must-be-legible law).

**F3 — JOSHUA'S SINCERITY IS SO TOTAL IT CAN READ AS DELUSION, LETTING THE PLAYER DISMISS HIS RIGHT.** Because Joshua is unwavering, a player can file him as a brainwashed fanatic and thereby dodge the hard question (a competent person's right to choose their own atonement) — the quest doesn't fully establish his soundness of mind, so "he's not in his right mind" becomes an escape hatch from the dilemma.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest turns on a person's right to choose their own extreme suffering, that person's SOUNDNESS must be established — sincere and competent, not dismissibly deluded — so the player can't dodge the right-to-atone question by declaring him incapable (#185's the-chooser-must-be-competent law).

## 6 WHY IT WORKS (W1-W10)

W1. ATONEMENT AND EXPLOITATION ARE ONE EVENT. Joshua's sincere penance and the corporation's monetized content are the same crucifixion — inseparable, which is the whole engine.
W2. HE MEANS IT COMPLETELY. Joshua is calm, articulate, sincere — not raving — so his right to his own atonement is a real claim, not a symptom.
W3. THE WIDOW FORGAVE HIM. Real grace complicates everything — the event is neither simple justice nor simple exploitation because forgiveness is genuine.
W4. THE PLAYER IS REVERENT AND COMPLICIT. Presence is respect for his choice and participation in a snuff-spectacle at once — no clean stance.
W5. THE QUESTION IS OWNERSHIP OF PUNISHMENT. Does a person own the right to their own agony as penance — the conduct-book's atonement question at its most extreme.
W6. THE EVENT CAN'T BE PURIFIED. The removed "atonement without spectacle" verb is the spine: the penance is sold and filmed; you can't extract one from the other.
W7. STOPPING HIM IS ALSO A WRONG. Refusing denies a sincere man his one path to peace — protection as paternalism, the other horn of the dilemma.
W8. IT LITERALIZES THE CRUCIFIXION. Not metaphor — actual nails, actual cross, actual stream — the extremity forces the question rather than gesturing at it.
W9. THE PULL GOES BOTH WAYS. His sincerity pulls toward helping; the cameras pull toward stopping — the quest makes the player feel both, not choose an obvious side.
W10. IT IS THE CONDUCT-BOOK AT THE CROSS. Whether a person owns their atonement, and whether witnessing suffering is reverence or complicity — Bohemia's whole penance question, on a livestreamed cross.
