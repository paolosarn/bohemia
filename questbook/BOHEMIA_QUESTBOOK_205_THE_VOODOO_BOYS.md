## 0 CORE IDEA

**A marginalized community of netrunners, protecting the one neighborhood the world lets them have, agrees to help the player reach something they need — but only by using the player as an expendable test subject to probe a lethal AI space no one has survived, extracting the data they want from the attempt and then betraying the player the moment the transaction is complete, because from their perspective the player is an outsider with powerful, dangerous connections whom they cannot afford to trust, and their willingness to use and discard the player is not villainy but the rational, earned product of living in a world that has only ever exploited them.**

The Voodoo Boys are a Haitian netrunner gang in the walled-off, over-policed district of Pacifica — a community the rest of the city abandoned, exploited, and left to fend for itself. They are the best netrunners in the city and they use that power to protect their own. The player comes to them needing access to something only they can provide, expecting, perhaps, a sympathetic alliance between two marginal parties. What they get is a transaction: the Voodoo Boys will help, but the "help" is to send the player into a deadly AI space beyond the Blackwall as a probe, to gather intelligence from whether and how the player survives, treating the player's body and life as an instrument. And when the player comes back with what they went in for, the Voodoo Boys betray them anyway — because the player is an outsider, connected to powers (a construct in the player's head, corporate interests) that could destroy the community, and a people who have been used their whole existence do not extend trust to a powerful stranger just because that stranger feels like an ally. Their betrayal is cold, calculated, and, from inside their history, entirely reasonable.

The machine is that the oppressed owe the player nothing, and the player's expectation of solidarity is itself a naivety the marginalized cannot afford. The player arrives with an implicit assumption: we are both outsiders, so we are on the same side. The Voodoo Boys puncture it. Solidarity is a luxury of people who can afford to be wrong about who to trust; a community that has been exploited by every outsider who ever smiled at them has learned that trusting a powerful stranger is how you get destroyed. So they use the player the way the world has always used them — instrumentally, extractively, disposably — not out of cruelty but out of the exact logic their survival taught them. And the player's sense of betrayal, their hurt at being treated as a tool, is a kind of privilege: it assumes a baseline of trust the Voodoo Boys have never once been given. The quest refuses to let the player be the sympathetic ally who deserves the marginalized community's faith; it makes them, from the other side, just another powerful outsider who wanted something, and it insists that the community's paranoia is not a flaw to be overcome by the player's good intentions but a rational scar the player is in no position to judge. There is no clean alliance available, because the conditions for trust were destroyed long before the player arrived.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** the people a cruel world has only ever exploited do not owe you their trust, and your hurt at being used by them is often a privilege — it assumes a baseline of good faith they were never once granted; solidarity is a luxury of those who can afford to be wrong about whom to trust, and expecting the marginalized to extend it to a powerful stranger, just because you feel like an ally, is asking them to take a risk the world has taught them is fatal.

## 1 CAST + WHAT EACH ONE WANTS

### THE VOODOO BOYS — the exploited who use you back
- **What they want (stated):** the data from the AI-space probe; to protect their neighborhood.
- **What they want (real):** survival, on terms that never require trusting a powerful outsider.
- **What they'll trade:** the player's help, extracted, then discarded — instrumentally, as they've been treated.
- **What they will not risk:** trust in a connected stranger, because that trust has only ever destroyed them.
- **Function:** THE EARNED PARANOIA: the marginalized who use and betray the player rationally, not cruelly.

### THE PLAYER (V) — the outsider who expected solidarity
- **What they want:** access, and, implicitly, an alliance between fellow marginal parties.
- **What they carry:** dangerous connections that make them, to the Voodoo Boys, a genuine threat.
- **Function:** THE PRESUMED ALLY: the one whose expectation of trust is a privilege the community can't afford.

### THE PROBE (THE AI SPACE / THE BLACKWALL) — the disposable use
- The lethal net-space the player is sent into as an expendable test subject.
- **Function:** THE INSTRUMENTAL USE: the player's body and life treated as a tool, as the world treats the Voodoo Boys.

### THE BETRAYAL — the cold, reasonable discard
- The turn on the player once the transaction is complete; calculated, not gratuitous.
- **Function:** THE RATIONAL DISCARD: the betrayal that is the earned logic of the exploited, not villainy.

### THE PLAYER'S HURT — the privilege of expecting good faith
- The sense of betrayal at being used; the assumption of a baseline of trust never extended to them.
- **Function:** THE PRIVILEGE EXPOSED: the player's wound revealed as an expectation the marginalized never got.

## 4 THE BRANCH MAP

**COUNT: 1 structural terminal — the Voodoo Boys use and betray the player regardless — with the meaning-fork in whether the player recognizes the betrayal as earned paranoia (their distrust a rational scar, the player's hurt a privilege) or resents it as villainy; the quest refuses the clean alliance because the conditions for trust were destroyed before the player arrived.**

- **THE RECOGNITION READING:** the player grasps that the Voodoo Boys' distrust is the rational product of a world that only exploited them, and their own hurt assumes a good faith the community never got. WRITES: the oppressed-owe-you-nothing truth received; solidarity seen as a luxury; the player's privilege exposed.
- **THE RESENTMENT READING:** the player holds the betrayal as a wrong done to them. WRITES: the hurt kept; and, underneath, the unexamined expectation that the marginalized should have trusted a powerful stranger.
- **THE EARNED-PARANOIA CONSTANT:** in every reading, the Voodoo Boys use and betray the player rationally, not cruelly — their paranoia is a scar, and no good intention could have overridden it.
- **THE THROUGH-LINE:** the branch is recognition-versus-resentment — two ways to hold being used by the people you expected to be allies, and the quest insists the exploited owe you nothing and your hurt at being used is a privilege.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — THE VOODOO BOYS CAN READ AS SIMPLE VILLAINS, LOSING THE "EARNED PARANOIA" TRUTH.** Because they use and betray the player, a player can file them as just another treacherous gang and miss that their distrust is a rational scar — so the profound idea (the oppressed owe you nothing) collapses into "the Haitian gang betrayed me," which is exactly the reading the quest most needs to avoid and risks reinforcing.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest's point is that a marginalized faction's distrust is earned, their HISTORY must be made present enough that the betrayal reads as a scar, not villainy — the exploitation that taught the paranoia shown — so the player can't file them as simple traitors (#192's the-reframe-must-be-grounded law).

**F2 — THE PLAYER'S HURT-AS-PRIVILEGE IS NEVER NAMED, SO THE SHARPEST IDEA STAYS IMPLICIT.** The quest lets the player feel betrayed but rarely surfaces that their expectation of solidarity was itself a privilege the Voodoo Boys never had — so the most cutting truth (your hurt assumes a good faith they were never granted) is left for the player to find, and most won't.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest's point is that the player's hurt at being used is a privilege, something must NAME it — a line, a perspective that turns the player's wound back on them — so the sharpest idea isn't left entirely implicit (#182's the-mirror-must-be-shown law).

**F3 — THE FORCED PROBE REMOVES THE PLAYER'S AGENCY, WHICH CAN READ AS RAILROADING.** Being used as an expendable test subject and then betrayed regardless can feel like the quest taking the wheel, so the powerful theme (you don't control whether the marginalized trust you) can blur with a design flaw (you don't control the quest) — the player's lack of agency needs to read as the point, not as a rail.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest deliberately strips the player's agency to make a point about power, the powerlessness must be FRAMED as the theme — the player's lack of control legibly the lesson — so it reads as "you were never in a position to be trusted," not "the quest wouldn't let you choose" (#191's the-powerlessness-must-be-owned law).

## 6 WHY IT WORKS (W1-W10)

W1. THE OPPRESSED OWE YOU NOTHING. The Voodoo Boys use and betray the player rationally — the marginalized are not obligated to a powerful stranger's expectation of solidarity, which is the whole engine.
W2. THEIR PARANOIA IS EARNED. Their distrust is the scar of a world that only exploited them — not a flaw to overcome but a rational survival logic.
W3. THE PLAYER'S HURT IS A PRIVILEGE. Being wounded by the betrayal assumes a good faith the community was never granted — the sharpest, most uncomfortable truth.
W4. THEY USE YOU AS YOU'VE BEEN USED. The player is treated instrumentally, disposably — exactly how the world treats the Voodoo Boys, turned outward.
W5. SOLIDARITY IS A LUXURY. Trusting a stranger is a risk only the safe can afford — the marginalized cannot, and the quest refuses to pretend otherwise.
W6. GOOD INTENTIONS CAN'T OVERRIDE IT. The removed "earn their trust" verb is the spine: the conditions for trust were destroyed before the player arrived.
W7. THE BETRAYAL IS COLD, NOT CRUEL. Calculated survival, not gratuitous evil — which is what makes it un-dismissable as mere villainy.
W8. THE PLAYER IS THE OUTSIDER. From the community's side, the player is just another connected stranger who wanted something — the perspective flip is the quest's power.
W9. THERE IS NO CLEAN ALLIANCE. The quest refuses the sympathetic-ally fantasy — trust was foreclosed by history, not by the player's failure.
W10. IT IS THE-OPPRESSED-OWE-YOU-NOTHING IN ONE GANG. A marginalized community using a powerful stranger back — Bohemia's whole faction question of trust, exploitation, and earned paranoia, in Pacifica.
