# BOHEMIA QUESTBOOK #126 — "RAPHAEL'S DEAL / THE HOUSE OF HOPE"
**Game:** Baldur's Gate 3 (2023)
**Studio:** Larian Studios
**Quest:** Act 3. A devil offers you the one tool that opens the best ending. The price is a crown you do not have yet. The contract is real, he honors it, and the alternative is to rob his house.
**Type:** INDIVIDUAL-QUEST DEEP DIVE

**Filed:** 7/16/26

## 0 CORE IDEA

**The devil is the only honest party in the transaction, and the correct move is to rob him.**

That is the machine, and it is a strange one, because every instinct the genre trained into you is wrong here.

He offers a fair trade. He states the terms plainly. He does not hide the price in a subclause. He hands over the goods **the instant you sign**, before you have delivered anything, on trust. And at the end of the game, if you keep your word, **he keeps his.** He is the only character in an eighty-hour game who does exactly what he said he would do.

And the game spends the entire quest telling you not to. The narrator tells you not to. Your companions tell you not to. Everyone in the room can see it and says so out loud.

**Because the deal is fair and the outcome is catastrophic.** Those are not in tension. The trade is honest. What he does with what he buys is the problem. He is not cheating you. He is buying Hell, and you are the vendor, and the price he is offering is generous.

**The second move, which is where the quest becomes great:** you can refuse, and nothing happens. No ambush. No curse. No penalty. He simply waits, in a brothel, for the rest of the game, in case you change your mind. The offer stays open. **The most patient antagonist in the genre just leaves the door unlocked and goes back to his drink.**

**The third move, which is the actual quest:** you can go to his house and take it. Rob the devil. And the house is not a dungeon in any normal sense. It is **peaceful.** You can walk it, loot it, insult the staff, steal his gloves, sleep in his fountain. Nothing attacks you. The house does not care. **It stays peaceful right up until you touch the one thing you came for, and then everything catches fire at once.**

**The fourth move, which is the wound:** inside the house is a prisoner. A woman. She has been tortured for years and she is still hopeful, and her name is Hope, and the house is called the House of Hope, and it is his, and he named it after her.

And you can use her and leave her there.

The game will let you trick Hope into pointing you at what you want and then walk out the door.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** the person offering you a fair price for something that isn't yours to sell is not your enemy. He's your mirror. Your enemy is the part of you doing the math.

## 1 CAST + WHAT EACH ONE WANTS

### RAPHAEL — the buyer
- **What he wants (real):** the Crown of Karsus, and through it, rulership of the Hells. Permanent. Total.
- **What he wants (stated):** exactly that. He says it. **He does not lie about the goal.**
- **What he'll trade:** the Orphic Hammer, up front, on signature, before you've delivered anything.
- **What he will never say out loud:** that he needs you. He cannot get the Crown himself. Every ounce of the theatrics, the house, the fire, the singing, is covering the fact that **a devil with 666 hit points is asking a mortal for a favor** because there is no other way for him to get it.
- **Function:** TEMPTATION, and a mirror. He is what ambition looks like when it is honest about itself.

### HOPE — the prisoner
- **What she wants:** out. And, worse, she wants it out loud, sincerely, without irony, after years of torture.
- **What she'll trade:** directions. She knows where the thing you want is kept, and she will tell you, because you are the first person who has spoken to her in a long time.
- **What she will never say out loud:** that she knows you might leave. She has done this math. She tells you anyway.
- **Function:** THE COST, and the test. She is not an obstacle and not a reward. She is a person who is useful, in a quest about whether you are the kind of thing that uses people.

### HELSIK — the diabolist
- **What she wants:** twenty thousand gold. That's it. She is a merchant.
- **What she'll trade:** the ritual that opens the door to Hell.
- **What she will never say out loud:** nothing. She is completely transparent, which is the joke. **The two most honest characters in Act 3 are a devil and the woman who sells you the way into his house.**
- **Function:** THE TOLL. And a beautiful loop: one accepted payment is **a pair of gauntlets stolen from the house you have not broken into yet.** You can pay the toll with the proceeds of the heist the toll is for.

### THE EMPEROR — the rival counsel
- **What he wants:** you not to free Orpheus. The Hammer is the only thing that can.
- **Function:** the reason the deal has teeth. He is pleased if you refuse, angry if you sign. The deal is not a side quest; it's a vote on the endgame.

### YURGIR — the debt
- **What he wants:** release from a contract of his own. Cross-referenced from a different quest, in a different act, in a different building.
- **Function:** MERCY BANKED. Spare him hours earlier, in a place with no connection to this, and **he shows up in the House of Hope and fights the devil with you.** He is bad at it. He misses constantly. He shows up anyway.

### THE COMPANIONS — the conscience you outsourced
- **What they want:** you to not do this.
- **Function:** the chorus. **And a flaw, because they are unanimous.** See F3.

## 2 FULL EVENT FLOW (STAGE BY STAGE)

### STAGE 1 — THE OFFER [MANDATORY, but the whole quest is refusable]
Act 3. Raphael appears. He lays out the situation: Orpheus, shackled, would be a powerful ally against the Elder Brain, and only the Orphic Hammer frees him. He has the Hammer. He wants the Crown of Karsus, which you do not have yet and will have later.

He is proposing a **futures contract.** He gives you a legendary weapon now, in exchange for a thing that does not exist in your inventory, and will not, for many hours.

Three options: **accept**, **refuse**, or **accept intending to break it.**

### STAGE 2A — YOU SIGN [OPTIONAL]
Instant delivery. The Hammer, on signature. Companions disapprove. If Voss is recruited, he hands Lae'zel a legendary greatsword on top. **You are richer, better armed, and the endgame just opened up.** The deal is *good*.

Tell the Emperor and he is furious.

### STAGE 2B — YOU REFUSE [OPTIONAL]
Nothing happens.

No fight. No curse. No consequence. He goes back to Sharess' Caress and **stays there for the rest of the game**, in case you reconsider. Tell the Emperor and he's pleased, though not enough to change how he treats you.

**This is the single best-designed beat in the quest and it is made entirely of nothing.**

### STAGE 3 — THE TOLL [OPTIONAL]
Devil's Fee, Lower City. Helsik. Twenty thousand gold for the ritual materials, or persuade her down, or pay her in Gauntlets of Hill Giant Strength.

You can also pickpocket the money straight back. You can also just steal the components out of chests and skip her entirely, if you can pick the locks. **The game does not care how you pay the toll. It cares that there is one.**

### STAGE 4 — THE RITUAL
Draw the sign, place the components, open a hole into Hell.

### STAGE 5 — THE HOUSE, AND THE PEACE [THE CRAFT]
You arrive in the House of Hope. And nothing happens.

**The house is calm.** Staff move around doing their jobs. You can wander it at will. Pick up Potions of Angelic Slumber. Antagonize the servants. Loot the boudoir. Steal the gauntlets. Read the archive.

**They mostly leave you alone.** For as long as you do not touch the hammer.

You are a burglar in a mansion where the owner's staff have been told to be polite to you. The tension is not generated by threat. It is generated by **permission.**

### STAGE 6 — HOPE
An apparition first, then the woman. A dwarven cleric. Years of torture by Raphael and his people. She is, despite it, hopeful and optimistic. That is the whole characterization and it is unbearable.

She begs to be freed.

**You do not have to.** You can trick her into pointing you at the Hammer, take the directions, and abandon her.

Freeing her is *work.* Her prison has imps and Spectators. It costs you resources you'd rather spend on the boss. **The game makes mercy expensive and optional and then puts the price tag right where you can see it.**

### STAGE 7 — THE ARCHIVE
The contracts. Yours, if you signed. Others'. Mol's is there.

If you signed: you can steal your contract back. If you refused: same room, same heist, you're taking the Hammer instead. **Structurally identical. The only thing that changes is which piece of paper you came for.**

### STAGE 8 — THE LINE
Take the thing.

**Everything catches fire. Everyone turns on you.** The house was never neutral. It was waiting.

### STAGE 9 — THE FIGHT, AND THE SONG
Raphael. 666 hit points. Soul Pillars feeding him health and spells; break them and he can go Ascended Fiend and multi-attack you. Choose your poison.

If you freed Hope, **she fights with you and heals you**, and she is the reason you survive.

If you spared Yurgir, he shows up. He misses a lot. He occupies the devil for a while.

**And Raphael sings.**

His own boss theme, "Raphael's Final Act," with lyrics, performed by him, at you, during his own murder. Players were split: some cringed, some got goosebumps, one player noted it fits his narcissism exactly.

**And you can cast Silence on him to shut him up.** The game anticipated that some players would find the theatrics insufferable and made the counter-play thematically perfect: the worst thing you can do to a narcissist mid-monologue is take his voice away. It is a spell. It works. **A player found this out and posted it as a tip.**

### STAGE 10 — AFTER
Helldusk Armour. Gloves of Soul Catching. Hope free. Orpheus freeable.

**And if you signed and never broke it:** he honors it. At the end. You hand him the Crown and **you get a post-credits scene where he explains, calmly, how he is going to dominate Hell.** He does not gloat about tricking you. There was no trick. He thanks you and gets to work.

## 3 THE CONVERSATIONS (THE ACTUAL MACHINE)

*Lines paraphrased to structure; the craft is the tree, not the transcript. What each option WRITES is what matters for the port.*

### NODE: OFFER_OPEN — Raphael, Sharess' Caress, entry: Act 3 reached
Opens with the pitch, in full, without hedging: Orpheus is an ally, the Hammer is the only key, he has the key, he wants the Crown, and he will give you the key first.
**THE LINE DOING THE WORK:** he tells you what he wants the Crown FOR. Rulership of the Hells, forever. He does not soften it.

```
> "What's the catch?"          [gate: none]                  -> OFFER_TERMS
> "Why do you need me?"        [gate: none]                  -> OFFER_NEED      *the real question*
> "No."                        [gate: none]                  -> OFFER_REFUSED
> "Done."                      [gate: none]                  -> OFFER_SIGNED
NOVERB: "What happens to the people of Baldur's Gate if you win?"
```
**NOVERB is the tell.** You can ask what he wants. You cannot ask what it costs everyone who isn't in the room. The quest is scoped to the transaction and the externality is unpriceable, which is the entire economics of it.

### NODE: OFFER_TERMS
He states them clean. No subclause. No trick. The contract says what he said.
```
> "That's it?"                 [gate: none]     -> OFFER_NEED
> "Done."                      [gate: none]     -> OFFER_SIGNED
> "No."                        [gate: none]     -> OFFER_REFUSED
```
**COSTS:** nothing. **This node exists to remove your excuse.** After it, you cannot say you were tricked.

### NODE: OFFER_NEED — the node the quest is built on
He needs you because **he cannot take the Crown himself.** A devil with 666 hit points, a private layer of Hell, and a personal demon staff, is asking a mortal for a favor.
```
> "You're begging."            [gate: none]     -> OFFER_NEED_2
> "Then my price is higher."   [gate: none] TRAP -> OFFER_NEED_2   *he is amused; terms unchanged*
> "Done."                      [gate: none]     -> OFFER_SIGNED
> (say nothing)                [gate: none] SILENCE -> OFFER_TERMS
NOVERB: "Then I'll take it myself"     <- you CAN do exactly this. You just cannot SAY it to him.
```
**THE TRAP:** haggling. It feels like leverage. It is not. **The terms do not move, and you have now told him you're a seller.**
**THE BEST NOVERB IN THE FILE.** The winning play in the entire quest is to go rob him. **The game will not let you say it to his face.** You have to leave the room, cross the city, buy a ritual, and do it. The threat is unspeakable and the action is available. That gap is the design.

### NODE: OFFER_SIGNED
Signature. **Hammer handed over immediately.** Companion disapproval fires from everyone present.
```
WRITES: flag:contract_signed, item:orphic_hammer, companion_approval -X (all)
LOCKS: nothing. You can still break it later. The door stays open both ways.
```
**COSTS:** an ending. And nothing else, yet. **That's the seduction: the bill is not due for forty hours.**

### NODE: OFFER_REFUSED
He is unbothered. The offer stands. He'll be at the brothel.
```
WRITES: flag:refused
LOCKS: NOTHING. OFFER_OPEN stays live for the rest of the game.
```
**COSTS:** nothing. **THIS IS THE CRAFT.** A refusal that costs nothing and forecloses nothing is rarer than any branch in this file. Most games punish "no" to make the "yes" feel weighty. Larian made "no" free, **and the offer more tempting for it**, because now it's not a trap you escaped, it's a door that never closes, and every hour you don't take it is an hour you chose not to.

### NODE: EMPEROR_TELL — entry: after either
```
> "I made a deal."             [gate: flag:contract_signed]  -> EMP_ANGRY
> "I turned him down."         [gate: flag:refused]          -> EMP_PLEASED
> "Nothing happened."          [gate: flag:contract_signed] TRAP -> EMP_LIED
```
**EMP_PLEASED costs you the discovery that his approval changes nothing.** He is pleased. He treats you identically. **A reward that is purely a feeling, and the game does not pretend otherwise.**

### NODE: HELSIK_TOLL — Devil's Fee
```
> "I'll pay."                        [gate: has:20000gold]        -> HELSIK_PAID
> "Half."                            [gate: none]                 -> HELSIK_HAGGLE
> "Take these gauntlets."            [gate: has:hill_giant_gloves] -> HELSIK_PAID   *stolen from his house*
> (steal the components instead)     [gate: none] SILENCE          -> HELSIK_BYPASSED
```
**THE LOOP:** one accepted payment is an artifact taken from the house you have not yet broken into. **You can pay the toll for the heist with the proceeds of the heist.** Time-travel economics, played straight, never remarked on.
**And you can pickpocket the twenty thousand back off her immediately.** The game knows. It lets you.

### NODE: HOPE_MEET — Hope, her cell, entry: House of Hope entered
Years of torture. Still hopeful. Asks to be freed.
**THE LINE DOING THE WORK:** she is optimistic, out loud, sincerely, in the House of Hope, having been tortured in it for years by the man who named it.
```
> "Where's the Hammer?"        [gate: none] TRAP -> HOPE_USED     *she tells you. she just tells you.*
> "I'll get you out."          [gate: none]      -> HOPE_FREE_START
> "Why are you still hopeful?" [gate: none]      -> HOPE_WHY
> (leave)                      [gate: none] SILENCE -> END
NOVERB: "I'll come back for you"      <- the lie is not offered. You cannot promise. You can only DO.
```
**THE TRAP IS NOT MARKED AND IT WORKS.** Ask her where it is and she tells you, immediately, warmly, because you spoke to her. Then you may walk out. **The game does not stop you and does not comment.**
**THE NOVERB IS THE BEST IN THE QUEST.** "I'll come back for you" is the single most human lie available in any RPG and **Larian did not write it.** There is no promise option. There is freeing her and there is not. **The verb that would let you feel good while abandoning her does not exist.**

### NODE: HOPE_WHY
She has done the math. She knows what she is and where she is. She is hopeful anyway, and she knows that's what it is.
```
> "That's not hope, that's damage."  [gate: none] -> HOPE_WHY_2
> "Okay."                            [gate: none] -> HOPE_FREE_START
```
**COSTS:** nothing mechanical. **You just have to hear it.**

### NODE: HOPE_USED
```
WRITES: knows:hammer_location, flag:hope_abandoned
LOCKS: HOPE_FREE_START forever if you take the hammer and leave.
```
**COSTS:** her. And the fight, later, because she was going to be your healer and you left her in the cell. **The mechanical punishment for cruelty here is that you are worse at the boss.** That is the only sermon the game delivers and it delivers it in hit points.

### NODE: THE_LINE — no speaker. The house itself.
There is no conversation. You touch the Hammer.
```
WRITES: house_hostile=true, fire=everywhere
```
**The house has no dialogue for this because the house was never negotiating.**

### NODE: RAPHAEL_FIGHT — Raphael, singing
He performs his own theme during his own death, with lyrics, at you.
```
> [cast Silence]               [gate: has:silence]   -> RAPHAEL_MUTE
```
**RAPHAEL_MUTE is the best joke in the game and it is not a joke.** It is a spell, in the spell list, working as written, and it happens to be the cruelest thing available. **The counter to a narcissist's final performance is to take his voice.** A player posted it as a tip for people who got fed up with the singing. Larian did not label it. It just works.

### NODE: POST_CREDITS — entry: flag:contract_signed AND NOT flag:contract_broken
He honors it. He explains, calmly, how he will dominate Hell. He does not gloat. There was no trick.
```
NOVERB: (there is no reply. it is a credits scene. you are not there.)
```
**The final beat of the entire honest-deal branch is him thanking you and getting to work.**

## 4 THE BRANCH MAP

**COUNT: 6 terminal states.** (Q117 law: count branches before scoping volume.)

**B1 — REFUSE, NEVER GO.** Cost: no Hammer, Orpheus stays shackled, the Emperor is your only endgame option. Writes: `flag:refused`. Locks: the Orpheus endings. Cashes: endgame. **The cheapest branch in the quest and it is fully supported.**

**B2 — REFUSE, ROB HIM.** Cost: 20,000 gold or a theft, and the hardest fight in the game. Writes: `item:orphic_hammer`, `flag:raphael_dead`, `flag:hope_free`. Cashes: endgame + Hope's ending. **The best outcome. No deal was ever made.**

**B3 — SIGN, BREAK IT.** Cost: same fight, plus the contract theft. Writes: `flag:contract_broken`. Identical to B2 mechanically. **Structurally the same heist, different piece of paper.** Cashes: endgame. **The dishonest path and the honest path arrive at the same room.**

**B4 — SIGN, HONOR IT.** Cost: Hell gets a new ruler. Writes: `flag:crown_delivered`. Cashes: **post-credits.** **He does exactly what he said. This is the "you were not tricked" ending.**

**B5 — ROB HIM, ABANDON HOPE.** Cost: Hope stays. Writes: `flag:hope_abandoned`. **Mechanically harder fight, no healer.** Cashes: immediately, in damage taken.

**B6 — SIGN, NEVER MENTION IT.** Lie to the Emperor. Writes: `flag:emperor_lied`. Cashes: later, badly.

**MERCY MODIFIER (not a branch, a multiplier):** spare Yurgir in Act 2, in an unrelated building, and he fights beside you in B2/B3/B5. **Q125 W8 and Q123's passenger manifest, third confirmation.**

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — THE DEAL IS MECHANICALLY DOMINATED AND EVERYONE KNOWS IT.**
Guides converge on the same verdict: don't take it, you can just steal the Hammer, and the House of Hope is one of the best parts of the game anyway. One player, having finished, noted flatly in hindsight that he could have skipped the whole thing and the story still progressed. **The "temptation" is an inferior option available to anyone who reads a wiki.** The devil is offering you a worse deal than burglary, and the burglary comes with the best loot, the best fight, and the best level.
**LAW: if the tempting offer is strictly worse than the alternative, it is not a temptation, it is a difficulty setting.**

**F2 — THE NARRATOR TELLS YOU THE ANSWER.**
The narrator states outright that Raphael is up to something evil and the deal isn't worth the pain. **The game's own voice pre-solves its central moral choice.** This is the same failure as theme-in-optional, inverted: instead of hiding the meaning, it announces the verdict.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA: the narration NEVER grades the choice. Ever. Not once.**

**F3 — THE COMPANIONS ARE UNANIMOUS.**
Every companion disapproves. Not one argues for it. Karlach in particular is fully against it. **A chorus with no dissent is not a conscience, it's a laugh track.** The strongest version has one companion who wants you to sign, for a reason you cannot dismiss.

**F4 — THE FIGHT'S TWO OPTIONS ARE THE SAME OPTION.**
Soul Pillars feed him. Destroy them and he goes Ascended Fiend and multi-attacks. **This reads as a dilemma and resolves as a chore:** everyone destroys the pillars, because you cannot win otherwise. And the honest answer from the community is worse: he gets stunlocked by Hold Monster, or barrelmancy'd, or thrown into a chasm. **A player noted with some irritation that he could one-shot the game's best boss by shoving him into a pit.** The best-written antagonist in the game is trivially cheesed.

**F5 — HOPE IS A HEALER.**
The mechanical reward for freeing her is a healbot in the boss fight. **The moment mercy has a stat line, the player optimizes it.** She should be freed because she is a person. She is freed because she keeps you alive. Larian built the incentive right and thereby corrupted the reason. Related to Q125 F7 (the +5 hug), and it is the harder version, because this one is load-bearing.

**F6 — THE CONTRACT IS NOT YOUR SOUL AND THE GAME NEVER SELLS THAT.**
Players in forums had to explain to each other that the contract is just the crown, not the soul. The iconography (devil, contract, blood, Hell) writes a cheque the terms don't cash. **The scariest thing about the deal is that it's mundane**, and the presentation fights that instead of leaning into it.

**F7 — TWENTY THOUSAND GOLD IS NOT A COST BY ACT 3.**
The toll is trivially bypassed: persuade her down, pay in stolen gauntlets, pickpocket it straight back, or just loot the components out of chests. **A gate with four bypasses is a doorway.**

**F8 — YOU CANNOT LEAVE WITHOUT THE FIGHT.**
A player asked directly: can I break the contract, free Hope, and leave without killing him? He wanted the deal voided so that if he later gave up the crown, **it would be his choice and not compelled.** That is a beautiful thing to want and the game says no. Taking the thing is a red line and the red line is a boss fight. **The one player who understood the quest's actual theme better than the quest did was told to go fight.**

**F9 — THE HOUSE IS AN INCOMPARABLY BETTER IDEA THAN THE DEAL.**
The permission-not-threat design of the peaceful mansion is the best thing in the quest, and it is the part the quest is not about. The title beat, the deal, is the weakest link in its own chain.

**F10 — THE SONG SPLIT THE ROOM AND THAT IS FINE, BUT LARIAN HEDGED.**
Reactions ranged from goosebumps to hard cringe to "very Disney, very un-BG." Larian's response was Silence, a get-out clause. **The bravest swing in the game shipped with an off switch.** Bank both readings: the swing was right, and the off switch means they blinked.

## 6 WHY IT WORKS (W1–W10)

W1. **THE DEVIL IS THE ONLY HONEST PARTY AND HE HONORS THE CONTRACT.** No trick, no subclause, no monkey's paw. He states the terms, states what he wants it FOR, delivers first, on trust, and at the end of the game **he does exactly what he said.** In an eighty-hour game full of liars, the devil is the one who keeps his word. **The horror is not being cheated. It's that you weren't.**

W2. **HE DELIVERS BEFORE YOU PAY.** The Hammer lands on signature. The Crown is owed on a thing that hasn't happened yet, to a person who doesn't have it, for a fight forty hours away. **A futures contract with a mortal, settled in advance, in good faith.** Nothing makes a devil more frightening than watching him take the counterparty risk.

W3. **REFUSAL COSTS NOTHING AND THAT IS WHY IT WORKS.** No ambush, no curse, no closed door. He waits in a brothel for the rest of the game. **The offer never expires.** Every hour you don't take it, you are choosing not to take it, again. A punished "no" is a trap you escaped. An unpunished "no" is a temptation that lives in the save file.

W4. **THE HOUSE IS PEACEFUL AND THE PEACE IS THE TENSION.** You can walk it, loot it, insult the staff, steal the gloves, read the archive. Nobody stops you. **It stays calm until you touch the one thing you came for, and then everything catches fire at once.** Tension built from permission, not threat. **The house isn't guarding the hammer. It's watching you decide.**

W5. **THE PRISONER IS NAMED HOPE AND THE HOUSE IS NAMED AFTER HER.** He tortured a woman for years and hung her name over the door. And she is still, sincerely, hopeful. That is not a pun. It is the thesis: **he keeps hope alive on the premises because a house with no hope in it cannot tempt anybody.**

W6. **YOU CAN USE HOPE AND LEAVE HOPE.** Ask where the Hammer is and she tells you, warmly, immediately, because you spoke to her. Then walk out. No stinger, no comment, no karma. **The cruelest available action is one unmarked dialogue option and a door.**

W7. **"I'LL COME BACK FOR YOU" IS NOT IN THE GAME.** The most human lie in the medium, and Larian did not write the button. There is freeing her, and not. **The verb that lets you feel decent while abandoning her does not exist.** Same family as Q118/Q122/Q123/Q125: the game removes the verb. **This is the sharpest instance in the entire questbook, because the removed verb is a COMFORT, not a cruelty.**

W8. **YOU PAY THE TOLL WITH THE PROCEEDS OF THE HEIST.** Helsik accepts Gauntlets of Hill Giant Strength, an artifact from the house you have not yet broken into. **You fund the burglary with the burglary.** Played completely straight, never winked at, and it means the door to Hell is openable by anyone who has already been through it.

W9. **THE MERCY SHOWS UP AND IT IS BAD AT ITS JOB.** Spare Yurgir in Act 2, in an unrelated building, for no reward. He shows up in Hell and fights the devil with you. **He misses a lot.** He is not good. He came anyway. **This is Q125 W8 and Q123's passenger manifest, third confirmation, and this version is better because the mercy is INCOMPETENT and still worth it.**

W10. **HE SINGS HIS OWN BOSS THEME AND YOU CAN SILENCE HIM.** A devil performs a musical number, with lyrics, at you, during his own murder, because he is a narcissist and this is his final act. Players got goosebumps or cringed hard; both reactions are correct and both are the point. **And Silence works on him.** Not a scripted option, just a spell doing what it says. **The counter to a narcissist's last performance is to take his voice, and it is in the spell list, and nobody labeled it.**

## 7 BOHEMIA PORTS

### PORT 1 — THE HONEST BUYER [W1, W2, core port]
**System:** the 13 factions / NETWORK / three currencies

Bohemia needs a faction rep who **never lies, never cheats, and always pays first.**

Not a trickster. A **counterparty.** He states what he wants, states what he'll do with it, hands over the medicine before you've delivered anything, and at the end of the generation he does exactly what he said he would.

The Amalgamation's whole logic makes this land harder than it does in BG3: **it is made of people who were honest about wanting to be remembered.** Nobody was tricked into uploading. They signed.

**.bq, in Bohemia's voice:**
```
@TALK offer_open speaker=buyer entry=act>=2
  @SAY I want the plant. All of it. The pumps, the maps, the names of everyone who knows the pumps.
  @SAY I am going to use it to make sure nobody in this valley ever has to ask me for water twice.
  @SAY They will ask once. Then they will not have to ask again. That is what I want. I have said it.
  @SAY Here is the medicine. Take it. Count it. I will wait.
  @OPT "You're giving it to me first."  [gate: none] -> offer_need
  @OPT "What's the catch."              [gate: none] -> offer_terms
  @OPT "No."                            [gate: none] -> offer_refused
  @NOVERB "What happens to the people who ask twice"
@END
```
**THE NOVERB IS THE PORT.** You can ask what he wants. **You cannot ask what it costs the people who are not in the room.** The externality is unpriceable and the format proves it by having no line for it.

### PORT 2 — THE REFUSAL THAT COSTS NOTHING AND NEVER EXPIRES [W3]
**System:** persistent consequence / generational persistence

**FIX F1 FIRST.** BG3's temptation is strictly worse than robbing him, so it isn't a temptation. **Bohemia's offer must be genuinely the better deal on the numbers.** Cheaper, safer, faster, and it works. If the player is only refusing because the wiki says the heist has better loot, the quest is a difficulty setting.

Then: **refusing costs nothing and the offer never closes.** He waits. Across the act. **Across the GENERATION.** Gen 1 refuses; the offer is still open when Gen 2 inherits, and the file says who refused it and when.

**The Bohemia escalation BG3 can't do:** every generation the offer stays refused, **the terms get better.** He is patient and he is compounding. By Gen 3 he is offering so much that the refusal is no longer a moral position, it's an expensive habit somebody's grandmother started.

`@OPT "No." [gate: none] -> offer_refused @DO set_flag refused_gen1` and the validator fires GEN_FLAG_TEST, because a flag crossing a generation boundary needs a named test. Q116 shipped one bugged for twenty-five years.

### PORT 6 — MERCY IS ARITHMETIC, NOT A SERMON [W6, F5]

### PORT 7 — THE COMFORT VERB THAT DOES NOT EXIST [W7]

**"I'll come back for you" is not in Baldur's Gate 3.** Larian did not write it.

**LOCK THIS AS A WRITING LAW:**

Every previous instance of the removed-verb law (Q118, Q122, Q123, Q125) removes a **cruelty**. This one removes a **comfort**, and that's the harder, better version.
