## 0 CORE IDEA

**A desert planet's settlers beg the player to deal with the Sand People — raiders they describe as mindless, savage monsters who attack without reason — but by acquiring a droid that can translate the Sand People's language and reach their enclave, the player learns their side of it: they are a people with a culture, a history, and a specific grievance, and their raids are retaliation for a massacre the settlers' own colonizing forebears committed against them, so the "monster problem" resolves into a cycle of atrocity in which the civilized side is the original aggressor, and peace becomes possible only if someone will carry the truth and the "good" people will admit what they did.**

On Tatooine, the settlers of the spaceport frame the Sand People as an existential nuisance: subhuman raiders, killers without language or reason, a threat to be culled. The player can take that framing and hunt them. But there is another road: a translator droid, and the nerve to walk into the Sand People's territory not as an exterminator but as someone willing to hear them. With translation, the "mindless savages" become a people. Their history unfolds: they were here first, or here long; the settlers' arrival brought violence; at some point the colonizers committed an atrocity — a massacre, a betrayal, a broken agreement — and the Sand People's raids are the long retaliation for it, a grief-driven war the settlers have reframed as unprovoked savagery to keep their own hands clean. The player, now holding both sides' truths, faces the choice: exterminate the Sand People (solving the settlers' "problem" by completing the cycle of slaughter), or broker a fragile peace — which requires the storyteller among the Sand People to be heard, and requires the settlers to reckon, at least a little, with the fact that they were the original aggressors.

The machine is that the monster becomes a person the instant you can understand them, and understanding them indicts your own side. As long as the Sand People are untranslatable, they can be monsters, and killing monsters is clean. The translator is the whole quest's hinge: it converts an extermination into a negotiation, a nuisance into a wronged people, a clean cull into a moral reckoning. And what understanding reveals is not just that the Sand People are people but that the settlers — the sympathetic, familiar, human side who asked for help — are the ones who started it. Peace is available, but it is not free: it costs the settlers their story about themselves (that they are innocent victims of savages), and it costs the player the simplicity of the extermination they were hired for. The quest lets the player take the easy, clean-seeming genocide, or do the hard thing: carry the truth, broker the peace, and make the civilized side look at the grave under their prosperity. And its honesty is that the label "savage" is almost always doing work — hiding a history, laundering an atrocity, making a wronged people into monsters so the wrong can stay buried.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** the word "savage" is almost always a lid on a history, and the people a society calls mindless monsters are frequently the ones it wronged first — the moment you can actually understand them, the monster becomes a person and the understanding indicts your own side, which is exactly why the language stays untranslated: because a people you can't understand can be killed clean, and a people you can are a crime you have to answer for.

## 1 CAST + WHAT EACH ONE WANTS

### THE SAND PEOPLE — the "savages" with a history and a grief
- **What they want (stated, once translated):** redress for the massacre; their grievance heard; the raids to have a reason acknowledged.
- **What they want (real):** to not be monsters in the only account that gets recorded; their history spoken.
- **What they'll trade:** the war, for a peace that acknowledges what was done to them.
- **Function:** THE MONSTER WHO BECOMES A PERSON: the untranslated enemy revealed, by language, as a wronged people.

### THE SETTLERS — the "civilized" side who started it
- **What they want:** the Sand People problem gone; their self-image as innocent victims of savages intact.
- **What they buried:** the atrocity their forebears committed that the raids retaliate for.
- **Function:** THE ORIGINAL AGGRESSOR: the sympathetic side whose innocence the truth destroys.

### THE TRANSLATOR (THE DROID / THE STORYTELLER) — the hinge of understanding
- The means of translating the Sand People's language; the storyteller who carries their history.
- **Function:** THE HUMANIZING KEY: the device that converts extermination into negotiation, monster into person.

### THE PLAYER — the one who culls or carries the truth
- **What they want:** to solve the "problem"; and, once they understand, to decide what understanding obliges.
- **Function:** THE DECIDER: the one who chooses the clean genocide or the costly peace.

### THE ATROCITY / THE BURIED HISTORY — the wrong under the framing
- The settlers' original massacre; the reason the raids are retaliation, not savagery.
- **Function:** THE LID-COVERED CRIME: the history the "savage" label exists to keep buried.

## 4 THE BRANCH MAP

**COUNT: 2 terminal outcomes — exterminate the Sand People (the clean-seeming genocide that completes the cycle) or broker a peace (that requires the settlers to admit their original atrocity) — with the constant that translation turns the monster into a person and the understanding indicts the civilized side, so no resolution leaves the settlers' innocence intact.**

- **EXTERMINATE THEM:** the "monster problem" solved by slaughter; the settlers' innocent story preserved by killing the witnesses to their crime. WRITES: the cycle completed; the wronged wiped out; the lid kept on the buried atrocity.
- **BROKER THE PEACE:** the war ended; the Sand People's history heard; the settlers made to reckon with being the original aggressors. WRITES: a fragile peace bought with the settlers' self-image; the buried crime named; the monster restored to a people.
- **THE UNDERSTANDING-INDICTS-YOUR-SIDE CONSTANT:** in both branches, translating the Sand People reveals they are wronged people and the settlers started it — the "savage" label was always laundering an atrocity.
- **THE THROUGH-LINE:** the branch is cull-or-reckon — two answers to "what do you owe an enemy once you can understand them," and the quest insists the word 'savage' hides a history, and peace costs the civilized side the innocence the label protected.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — THE SAND PEOPLE REMAIN HOSTILE ELSEWHERE, UNDERCUTTING THE HUMANIZATION.** Outside this quest the Sand People attack on sight, so the powerful reframe (they're a wronged people, not monsters) collides with the player's other experiences of them as generic enemies — the quest doesn't reconcile the humanized enclave with the hostile raiders everywhere else, weakening the "monster becomes person" reveal.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest humanizes a faction the player fights elsewhere, it must RECONCILE the two — the hostility reframed as the shape of the grievance, not left as a contradiction — so the humanization survives contact with the faction's other appearances (#192's the-reframe-must-hold-against-the-deeds law).

**F2 — THE PEACE PATH CAN BE ACHIEVED WITHOUT THE SETTLERS REALLY RECKONING.** The reconciliation can resolve at the Sand People's end (they stand down) without much shown of the settlers actually admitting their atrocity, so the sharpest idea (peace costs the civilized side its innocence) can go unpaid — the "good" side gets peace without the reckoning the quest says it requires.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest's point is that peace requires the aggressor to admit its crime, the ADMISSION must actually happen on the aggressor's side — the reckoning shown, not just the enemy standing down — so the cost the quest names is really paid (#192's the-lid-must-actually-lift law).

**F3 — THE TRANSLATOR REDUCES A MORAL CHOICE TO AN ITEM GATE.** Because understanding the Sand People requires acquiring a specific droid/translator, the humanization can read as a fetch-quest unlock rather than a choice to listen — so the profound "will you try to understand the enemy" decision becomes "do you have the item," mechanizing what should be a moral posture.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if understanding an enemy is the quest's moral hinge, it must be framed as a CHOICE TO LISTEN, not just an item gate — the willingness to hear made the real requirement, the tool secondary — so humanization is a posture the player takes, not a key they happen to hold (#191's understanding-is-a-choice-not-an-unlock law).
