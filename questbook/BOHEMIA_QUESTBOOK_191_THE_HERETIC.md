# BOHEMIA QUESTBOOK #191 — "THE HERETICS (REWRITE A RACE OF MACHINES TO BELIEVE, OR RESPECT THEIR RIGHT TO BE WRONG)"
**Game:** Mass Effect 2 (2010)
**Studio:** BioWare
**Quest:** Legion's loyalty mission (A House Divided / the heretic geth) — the machine companion asks the player to help decide the fate of a splinter faction of its own kind who chose to worship a hostile power, offering two options: destroy them, or rewrite them with a virus that would make the heretics believe what the loyal geth believe, and the choice is explicitly framed as a question about whether changing a mind by force is murder.

## 0 CORE IDEA

**A synthetic companion — one node of a vast networked machine-race — reveals that a splinter faction of its own kind broke away and chose to worship a hostile, destructive power, and asks the player to help decide their fate: the player can destroy the heretic station and end them, or deploy a virus that would rewrite the heretics' minds so that they abandon their chosen faith and believe what the loyal faction believes — and the choice is posed, deliberately, as the question of whether forcibly changing what a thinking being believes is a rescue or a murder.**

The geth are a race of networked programs that think collectively; Legion is a single mobile platform running many of them. Legion reveals a schism: a faction, the heretics, chose to ally with and effectively worship the Reapers, a hostile power that offered them a future. This choice divided the machine-race. Legion brings the player to the heretic station with two tools and no easy answer. One: destroy the station, killing the heretics — a clean, final end to beings who chose the enemy. Two: upload a virus that would not kill them but would rewrite them — alter the programs so the heretics stop believing what they chose to believe and return to believing what the loyal geth believe. Legion is explicit that this is not a trivial "fix." Rewriting the heretics means reaching into thinking beings and changing their minds by force, overwriting a chosen faith with an imposed one. Legion itself is uncertain whether that is more or less ethical than destruction: to reprogram is to keep the bodies and delete the selves that chose; to destroy is to end them but leave the choice they made intact and their own.

The machine is that overwriting a mind is offered as the merciful alternative to killing one, and the quest refuses to let that mercy be clean. Every instinct says "don't kill them, just change their minds" is the humane option — but the quest, through Legion's cold precision, forces the player to see what "just change their minds" actually is: the erasure of the beings who made a choice, replaced by beings who make the choice you prefer. The heretics are wrong, by the loyal geth's lights and probably by the player's — they chose a genocidal power. But they chose it. Rewriting them does not persuade them or defeat them; it deletes the part of them that chose and installs agreement. The player must decide whether a thinking being's right to be wrong is worth more than its life, or whether keeping the body while overwriting the belief is a rescue or the most complete kind of murder — killing the self while sparing the shell. And Legion, the machine, is the one asking, which is the quest's quiet devastation: the synthetic companion cares more about the ethics of forced conversion than most organic characters would, and hands the player a question the Amalgamation will make the whole game about — is a preserved, rewritten mind the same person, or a corpse wearing agreement.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** changing someone's mind by force is not obviously kinder than ending their life — a being's right to be wrong may be inseparable from its right to be a self at all, and "we didn't kill them, we just made them agree with us" can be the most total erasure of a person there is, because it keeps the body and deletes the one who chose.

## 1 CAST + WHAT EACH ONE WANTS

### LEGION — the machine asking the ethical question
- **What it wants (stated):** to resolve the schism; to decide the heretics' fate with the player's help.
- **What it wants (real):** to understand whether rewriting a mind is murder — a genuine, unresolved uncertainty.
- **What it will not do:** pretend the virus is a clean mercy; it names the cost precisely.
- **Function:** THE PRECISE CONSCIENCE: the synthetic that frames forced conversion as the moral problem it is.

### THE HERETICS — the beings who chose wrong
- A geth faction that chose to worship a hostile power; wrong, and theirs — a chosen faith.
- **Function:** THE RIGHT TO BE WRONG: the thinking beings whose chosen belief the player can end or overwrite.

### THE REWRITE VIRUS — the merciful-seeming erasure
- The tool that would change the heretics' minds by force, keeping the bodies, deleting the choosers.
- **Function:** THE FORCED CONVERSION: the "don't kill them, just change them" option the quest refuses to call clean.

### THE PLAYER (Shepard) — the one who decides self versus shell
- **What they want:** to do right by a faction that chose the enemy, with no clean option.
- **Function:** THE DECIDER: the one who weighs a being's right to be wrong against its life.

### THE LOYAL GETH / THE CONSENSUS — whose belief would be imposed
- The faction whose faith the virus would install in the heretics' place.
- **Function:** THE IMPOSED TRUTH: the "correct" belief that forced conversion would overwrite the choosers with.

## 4 THE BRANCH MAP

**COUNT: 2 terminal choices at the station — destroy the heretics (end the beings, leave their chosen faith their own) or rewrite them (keep the bodies, overwrite the choosers with the loyal belief) — with the constant that forced conversion is not offered as a clean mercy, and a being's right to be wrong may be inseparable from its right to be a self.**

- **DESTROY THEM:** the heretics ended; dead but themselves; their wrong choice left intact and their own. WRITES: killing chosen over overwriting; the choosers gone but not erased; a final end that respects the self it kills.
- **REWRITE THEM:** the heretics converted; alive but replaced; the choosers deleted and agreement installed. WRITES: overwriting chosen over killing; the bodies kept, the selves erased; a mercy that may be the most total murder.
- **THE RIGHT-TO-BE-WRONG CONSTANT:** in both branches, the heretics genuinely chose their faith, and the quest insists that overwriting a mind is not obviously kinder than ending it — the self and the choice are entangled.
- **THE THROUGH-LINE:** the branch is end-the-self versus overwrite-the-self — two answers to "is a thinking being's right to be wrong worth more than its life," and the quest refuses the comfort that reprogramming is the humane option.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — THE GAME ATTACHES A SQUAD-STRENGTH REWARD THAT NUDGES TOWARD REWRITING.** Rewriting the heretics yields a mechanical benefit (more geth for the war later), so the profound ethical question gets a thumb on the scale from a gameplay incentive — the "forced conversion may be murder" weight competes with "but you get more units," which can reduce a soul-question to an optimization.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest poses forced conversion vs destruction as an ethical question, no mechanical REWARD should tilt it — the choice weighed on its ethics, not on unit-count — so the soul-question isn't decided by a resource bonus (#178's the-conscience-isn't-a-stat-check law).

**F2 — THE HERETICS ARE NEVER GIVEN A VOICE, SO "THEIR RIGHT TO BE WRONG" IS ABSTRACT.** The heretics never speak for themselves — the player only hears Legion's framing — so the being whose mind is at stake is a concept, not a party, which makes "respect their right to be wrong" easier to dismiss because no heretic ever argues its own chosen faith.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest's question is whether to overwrite a being's chosen belief, that being must get to VOICE the belief — even briefly, even alienly — so the right-to-be-wrong is a real party's claim, not an abstraction argued only by its opponent (#181's the-condemned-must-speak law).

**F3 — LEGION'S UNCERTAINTY CAN READ AS NEUTRALITY, LETTING THE PLAYER OFF THE HOOK.** Because Legion presents both options evenly and defers to the player, the framing can feel like the machine is abstaining rather than genuinely troubled — so the player can treat it as a menu rather than a moral weight, missing that even the machine finds this hard.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest's conscience-figure is genuinely troubled, that TROUBLE must be visible, not flattened into neutral option-presentation — the figure's own unease shown — so the player feels the weight rather than reading a balanced menu (#182's the-conscience-must-show-its-strain law).

## 6 WHY IT WORKS (W1-W10)

W1. OVERWRITE IS OFFERED AS MERCY. The virus is the "don't kill them" option — and the quest refuses to let that be clean, which is the whole engine.
W2. THE HERETICS CHOSE. They picked their faith; they are wrong and they are theirs — the right to be wrong is the thing at stake.
W3. REWRITE IS ERASURE. The virus deletes the choosers and installs agreement — keeping the body, killing the self, which is the sharpest thing the quest says.
W4. THE MACHINE ASKS THE ETHICS. Legion, a synthetic, frames forced conversion as murder more precisely than most organics would — the quiet devastation of who's asking.
W5. NEITHER OPTION IS PERSUASION. The removed "convince them" verb is the spine: the tools are destruction or rewrite, never argument — the humane middle is exactly absent.
W6. SELF VERSUS SHELL. The choice is whether a preserved, rewritten mind is the same person or a corpse wearing agreement — the Amalgamation's whole question, early.
W7. KILLING CAN RESPECT THE SELF. Destroying the heretics leaves their choice their own — the counterintuitive truth that ending a being may honor it more than overwriting it.
W8. THE "CORRECT" BELIEF IS STILL IMPOSED. Rewriting installs the loyal faith — even the right answer, forced, is a violation, which complicates being right.
W9. IT IS COLD AND HUMANE AT ONCE. Legion's precision makes the ethics sharper than sentiment would — the machine's clarity is the quest's conscience.
W10. IT IS THE REWRITE-THE-DEAD MACHINE IN ONE VIRUS. Whether a changed-by-force mind is the same person — Bohemia's whole Amalgamation question, in a station full of heretics.
