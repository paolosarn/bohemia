## 0 CORE IDEA

**A happy, unremarkable young man — a blacksmith's son with a normal life, a family, a girl, a town — has that entire world annihilated in a single morning when a raiding army sacks his home and cuts down his parents in front of him while he is powerless to stop it; he escapes, comes back to bury his parents with his own hands because he cannot leave them to the dogs, is knocked senseless and wakes days later broken and grieving in a strange town under strangers' care; and the thing that pulls him back into a life is not a thunderous call to vengeance or a chosen-one destiny, but a local man of standing handing him one small, concrete, grounded errand — a task with purpose tied to the loss — that gets him off the grave, onto his feet, and into the first step of a long climb from absolute zero.**

Kingdom Come opens by spending its first hour on the most dangerous thing a game can spend an hour on: nothing happening. Henry wakes to his mother nagging him, is sent to collect a debt, brawls in the street, drinks with friends, and helps his father, the town blacksmith, finish a sword for the local lord. It is deliberately mundane, and that mundanity is the whole investment: the game is buying, in ordinary domestic texture, a life the player will care about losing. Then the raid comes. A mercenary army burns Skalitz, and Henry watches his parents killed and can do nothing but run, fleeing on a stolen horse to warn the neighboring castle. The powerlessness is authored and total; there is no version where the skilled player saves them, because Henry is not skilled, he is a blacksmith's son who has never held a real sword. Later he sneaks back to a ruined, corpse-strewn Skalitz and does the heaviest small thing in the game: he digs his parents' graves himself, because leaving them unburied is unbearable. He is knocked out, and wakes days later in a mill in another town, Rattay, nursed back by a miller's daughter, hollow and adrift. And then the turn: a captain and a lord, local men who now have a use for him, give Henry his first real tasks — training, and then a modest investigation, ride to a nearby farm, find where the raiders came from. Not glory. Not destiny. An errand. And that errand is the exact thread that pulls a grieving nobody off the floor and into the hundred-hour rise that follows.

The machine is that grief is paralysis, and the cure the game chooses is not spectacle or vengeance but a small, grounded, purposeful task handed over by someone who sees the broken person needs one — and the whole arc works only because the game first spent real time making the lost life ordinary and dear, then took it in a way the player was powerless to prevent, then made the grief physical and slow before offering the way forward. Kingdom Come refuses the shortcuts. It does not let you save your family with good reflexes, so the loss cannot be blamed on the player's failure; it belongs entirely to the raiders. It does not skip the burial, so the grief is a thing your own hands perform, not a cutscene you watch. And crucially it does not pull you back into life with a revenge-fantasy montage or a "you are the chosen one" trumpet; it pulls you back with a captain who needs a rider and a lord who needs a name, ordinary people with ordinary uses for a young man with nothing left. That is why the return to action feels earned and human rather than contrived: purpose arrives the way it actually arrives after a catastrophe, as someone small giving you something concrete to do. The rise from zero begins not with a sword raised to the sky but with an errand accepted because the alternative is sitting in the ruin. And the honesty is that after the worst thing, nobody is saved by grandeur; they are saved by the small, real task that gets them upright, and by the people who hand it to them not out of destiny but out of ordinary need.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** grief is a paralysis that grandeur cannot cure, and what actually gets a broken person off the ground is small, grounded, purposeful work handed over by someone who simply needs them — the way back into a life is not a call to vengeance or a promise of destiny but an errand accepted because sitting in the ruin is worse, and the rise from nothing always starts with the first concrete thing you agree to do for someone who sees you still have hands.

## 1 CAST + WHAT EACH ONE WANTS

### HENRY (THE PLAYER) — the grieving nobody who must be pulled back
- **What they want (stated):** at first nothing, then to bury the dead, then vaguely to make the raiders pay.
- **What they're denied:** the power to save their family (a blacksmith's son, no skill, no chance).
- **What they actually need:** not vengeance but a small, concrete task and someone who needs them — the thread back to a life.
- **Function:** THE BROKEN FOUNDER: the one with nothing left, who rises from zero one errand at a time.

### THE ORDINARY LIFE (SKALITZ) — the warm world established so its loss lands
- The mundane, dear, domestic day: the mother, the father's forge, the debt, the friends, the girl.
- **Function:** THE THING WORTH LOSING: the ordinary life the game spends real time making precious before it takes it.

### THE RAID (THE ARMY) — the annihilation the player is powerless to stop
- The mercenary force that burns the town and kills the parents while Henry can only run.
- **Function:** THE AUTHORED LOSS: the catastrophe that belongs wholly to the attackers, never to the player's failure.

### THE PARENTS / THE GRAVE — the loss made physical
- The bodies Henry returns to bury with his own hands, because leaving them is unbearable.
- **Function:** THE GRIEF YOU PERFORM: the loss made a slow physical act, not a cutscene, so it is carried, not watched.

### THE MENTOR (THE CAPTAIN / THE LORD) — the local who hands the first grounded errand
- Ordinary men of standing who now have a use for Henry: training, and a modest task, ride out, find where the raiders came from.
- **Function:** THE THREAD BACK: the small, purposeful errand from someone who needs you, which pulls the grieving nobody upright.

## 4 THE BRANCH MAP

**COUNT: a single authored arc (Unexpected Visit -> Run! -> the burial -> Awakening -> Train Hard, Fight Easy -> The Hunt Begins) with a fixed emotional spine — ordinary life, powerless loss, physical grief, hollow waking, the grounded first errand — and player agency inside it (how you fight/flee, how you handle the burial and the road, which tasks you take first), never over whether the family is lost.**

- **THE ESTABLISHED LIFE (FIXED):** the mundane dear day is played, not skipped. WRITES: a life worth losing, bought in ordinary texture, so the raid lands.
- **THE POWERLESS LOSS (FIXED):** the parents die and Henry can only run; no skill saves them. WRITES: a catastrophe owned by the attackers, never framed as the player's failure.
- **THE BURIAL (FIXED, performed):** Henry buries them by hand. WRITES: grief as a slow physical act the player carries, not a cutscene watched.
- **THE FIRST ERRAND (FIXED as a turn, open in flavor):** a local hands a small grounded task; the player chooses how to take it up. WRITES: the return to life through purpose-from-someone-who-needs-you, not vengeance or destiny.
- **THE THROUGH-LINE:** the branch is fixed-spine-with-agency-inside — the loss is unpreventable and the return is via an errand, and the arc insists that after the worst thing nobody is saved by grandeur, only by the small real task that gets them upright and the ordinary person who hands it over.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — THE LONG MUNDANE OPENING CAN READ AS A SLOG BEFORE THE PLAYER IS INVESTED.** The warm ordinary hour is the entire emotional investment, but a player who does not yet care about Henry can experience the chores-and-errands intro as a slow, boring tutorial that withholds the game, so the very texture meant to make the loss land can instead lose the player before the raid arrives.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if the loss depends on a warm "before," that before must EARN its length — kept short, interactive, and characterful, every mundane beat doing double duty (teaching a system AND drawing the family), so the ordinary life is felt as precious, not endured as a chore (Bohemia's match-cut childhood dinner is the compression: buy the warmth in one scene, not one hour).

**F2 — THE AUTHORED POWERLESSNESS CAN FEEL LIKE THE GAME STEALING AGENCY RATHER THAN AS TRAGEDY.** Forcing the family's death and denying the player any chance to save them is what keeps the loss clean, but a player used to earning outcomes with skill can read the unwinnable raid as the game cheating them rather than as honest tragedy, resenting the loss instead of grieving it.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a loss is authored and unpreventable, it must be framed so it reads as TRAGEDY, not as the game punishing the player — the death belonging visibly to the attackers and to the character's real powerlessness, never to a fight the player was tricked into losing (this is Bohemia's pacifism resolution: the raiders kill the sibling, the player's hands are never the cause, so no one feels cheated of a save that was never theirs to make).

**F3 — THE GRIEF-TO-ACTION TURN COLLAPSES IF THE FIRST ERRAND IS HOLLOW BUSYWORK.** The return to life works only because the first task carries purpose tied to the loss; if the pull-you-back quest were a trivial fetch errand with no connection to what was taken, the turn from paralysis to action would feel arbitrary and the earned weight of the opening would drain into generic questing.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** the first quest that pulls a grieving protagonist back into the world must have real PURPOSE, ideally threaded to the loss or the rebuilding, and be handed over by someone who genuinely needs them — grounded work, not busywork — so the return to action is a believable step out of grief, not a checklist (Bohemia's neighbor gives a task with a stake, and the reward, the backyard wall coming down, is earned, not a day-one gift).

## 6 WHY IT WORKS (W1-W10)

W1. IT BUYS THE LOST LIFE IN ORDINARY TEXTURE. A mundane dear day is played before it is taken — the whole loss lands because the warmth was earned first.
W2. THE LOSS IS POWERLESS AND CLEAN. Henry cannot save his family; the death belongs to the attackers, never to the player's failure — grief without resentment.
W3. THE GRIEF IS PERFORMED, NOT WATCHED. Burying the parents by hand makes the loss a slow physical act the player carries, the opposite of a skipped cutscene.
W4. THE RETURN IS AN ERRAND, NOT A TRUMPET. Purpose arrives as a small grounded task from someone who needs you — the honest way people re-enter life after catastrophe.
W5. IT REFUSES REVENGE-FANTASY SHORTCUTS. No chosen-one call, no vengeance montage; the pull back is ordinary and human, which is why it is believed.
W6. THE FIRST TASK HAS PURPOSE TIED TO THE LOSS. Find where the raiders came from — grounded work threaded to what was taken, so action is earned, not busywork.
W7. THE RISE STARTS FROM ABSOLUTE ZERO. A blacksmith's son with no skill; the climb is long and real because the floor is truly the floor — the fold's rise-from-nothing.
W8. THE MENTOR NEEDS YOU, NOT SAVES YOU. The captain and lord hand tasks out of ordinary use, so the bond is reciprocal from the first beat, not charity.
W9. GRIEF IS WALKED OUT OF, NOT CURED. The removed "bring the dead back" verb is the spine: nothing returns them; you move forward one task at a time.
W10. IT IS THE WHOLE FOUNDING MACHINE IN ONE MORNING. Ordinary life, powerless loss, physical grief, and the grounded first errand — the exact shape of Bohemia's opening and the seed of a hundred-hour rise.

## 7 BOHEMIA PORTS

### PORT 1 — THE NEIGHBOR'S FIRST ERRAND PULLS YOU OFF THE GRAVE [W4, W6, W9 — the core port]
**System:** the fold / companions / the family core
Bohemia's opening IS this machine. After the raid takes a sibling and the family buries them on the ridge, the player sits in grief with nothing. The NEIGHBOR (the house directly behind) comes over not to mourn with you but to hand you a small, grounded errand — a task with real purpose that he genuinely needs done — and that errand is the thread that pulls the grieving founder off the grave and back into a life. It is not vengeance and not destiny; it is ordinary work from an ordinary person who needs you, and it is the first step of the pseudo-faction's rise from zero. A couple of these earned errands, and the backyard walls come down into your first base.
```
@TALK the_first_errand speaker=neighbor entry=after_the_grief
  @SAY I heard it. Last night. All of it. I'm sorry, truly, and I won't stand here and pretend I've got the words, because I don't.
  @SAY But sitting in that house isn't going to feed anyone, and it isn't going to bring them back. Listen. I've got a thing that needs doing and I can't do it alone. Come with me. Just this one. Then we'll see.
  @OPT "I can't. Not yet." [gate: none] -> bohemia_grief_paralysis
  @OPT "What needs doing?" [gate: none] -> bohemia_first_errand
  @OPT "Why me?" [gate: none] -> bohemia_why_me
  @NOVERB "Bring them back"
@END
```
**THE NOVERB IS THE PORT:** nothing brings the dead back, and the game never pretends otherwise (which is exactly the false promise the Amalgamation makes a hundred years later). The way forward is the errand: grief is walked out of, one grounded task at a time. [PENDING, Paolo's call: what the errand actually is.]

### PORT 2 — THE WARM BEFORE EARNS ITS LENGTH [F1's law]
**System:** the family core / the opening
The lost life must be bought in warmth before it is taken, but SHORT and interactive, not an hour of chores. Bohemia's match-cut childhood dinner is the compression: one scene of the family whole and happy, the world intact, then the blink to the ruin — the warmth purchased in a moment, not a slog, so the loss lands without losing the player first (the KCD-length problem solved by the cut).

### PORT 3 — THE AUTHORED LOSS READS AS TRAGEDY, NOT A STOLEN SAVE [F2's law]
**System:** the family core / the pacifist path
The sibling's death is authored and unpreventable, but it must never feel like the game cheating the player of a save. Bohemia's fix: the raiders kill the sibling, never the player's failure or a rigged fight — a pacifist can refuse to kill and still lose them, because the loss was never theirs to prevent. The death belongs to the attackers, so it is grieved, not resented (#197's guard against the loss reading as a punishment).

### PORT 4 — THE FIRST QUEST HAS PURPOSE, NOT BUSYWORK [F3's law]
**System:** companions / the fold / the neighbor bond
The task that pulls a grieving protagonist back must carry real purpose and be handed over by someone who needs them, its reward earned. Bohemia's neighbor errand has a genuine stake and the payoff (the backyard wall coming down into the first base) is earned over a couple of quests, never a day-one gift — so the return to action is a believable step out of grief, not a checklist.
