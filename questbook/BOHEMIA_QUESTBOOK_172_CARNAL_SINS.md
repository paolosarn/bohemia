# BOHEMIA QUESTBOOK #172 — "CARNAL SINS (THE ARTIST WHO KILLS TO FINISH THE MASTERPIECE)"
**Game:** The Witcher 3: Wild Hunt (2015)
**Studio:** CD Projekt Red
**Quest:** Carnal Sins — a Novigrad murder investigation. Bodies are turning up mutilated in ritual poses; the trail runs through a church, a grieving lover, and a fanatic who is killing sinners to compose a religious tableau — a whodunit where the killer believes the murders are holy art.

**FORMAT:** v2 — cast + conversation node trees + branch map.
**Filed:** 7/17/26
**Why pulled:** Bohemia needs a forensic whodunit that reads bodies as a language and where the killer's motive is a corrupted faith — the deduction engine (#19) meets the false-devotion of #166, grounded in a real investigation the player runs clue by clue. Carnal Sins is the specimen: a serial killer staging corpses as a religious composition, a red herring who is a grieving innocent, and a resolution the player reaches (or misreads) by reading the scene. ## 0 CORE IDEA

**Mutilated bodies are turning up posed in ritual tableaux, and the investigation — running through a morgue, a church, and a grieving lover framed by circumstance — reveals a killer who murders sinners not from rage but to COMPOSE them, arranging corpses into a religious masterpiece he believes is holy.**

Novigrad's morgue has bodies, and they are wrong: mutilated, posed, arranged with deliberate care into ritual configurations. The player (Geralt) investigates — reading corpses with witcher-senses, following the forensic trail through the Eternal Fire's church, a mortuary, and a cast of suspects. The obvious lead points to a grieving man whose lover was among the victims, circumstantially damning, and the investigation can wrongly land on him. But the real killer is a religious fanatic who murders those he deems sinners and STAGES their bodies — each corpse a component in a larger composition, a tableau of divine judgment he is assembling with the patience of an artist and the certainty of a zealot. The murders are not crimes of passion; they are brushstrokes, and the killer believes he is making something sacred.

The machine is a whodunit where the bodies are a text and the killer's motive is a corrupted devotion. The player reads the scene — the poses, the mutilations, the ritual grammar — and the deduction is about learning to read the murders as the killer reads them: as a composition, a message, a work in progress. And the horror is aesthetic: the killer is not a beast but an artist, sincere in his faith, arranging his victims with love, and his madness is precisely that he experiences the atrocity as beauty. The resolution can catch him (read the tableau correctly, find the composer) or misfire onto the grieving innocent (follow the circumstantial trail, condemn the wrong man, let the artist keep composing). The quest asks the player to think like the killer to catch him — to see the corpses as he sees them — which is the deduction's power and its unease: to solve it, you have to understand the beauty he finds in the horror.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** the most dangerous devotion is the one that experiences its atrocities as beauty, and to catch the artist who kills you have to be willing, for a moment, to see what he sees.

## 1 CAST + WHAT EACH ONE WANTS

### THE COMPOSER (the killer) — the artist who murders
- **What he wants:** to complete his masterpiece — a religious tableau assembled from the bodies of those he judges sinners.
- **What he'll trade:** nothing; he is a zealot at work, sincere and patient.
- **What he will never say out loud (as a confession):** that it is murder; to him it is composition, judgment, holy art.
- **Function:** THE CORRUPTED DEVOTION: a fanatic who experiences atrocity as beauty, the whodunit's true and terrible answer.

### THE GRIEVING LOVER (the red herring) — the framed innocent
- **What he wants:** justice for his murdered lover, and to not be blamed for the killing.
- **What he'll trade:** his cooperation, if the player reads him right; his freedom, if the player reads the circumstantial trail wrong.
- **Function:** THE MISDIRECTION: the innocent whose grief looks like guilt, whose wrongful condemnation lets the artist keep composing.

### THE BODIES — the text and the artwork
- The mutilated, posed corpses, each a component in the killer's composition.
- **Function:** THE LANGUAGE the player must learn to read — the deduction is understanding the murders as the killer does.

### THE CHURCH (the Eternal Fire) — the faith the killer corrupts
- The religion whose doctrine the killer has twisted into a license to compose with corpses.
- **Function:** THE SOURCE of the corrupted devotion: a real faith aimed at atrocity.

### GERALT / THE PLAYER — the reader who must think like the killer
- **What they want:** to catch the composer by reading the tableau.
- **Function:** THE DEDUCTION: the one who must see what the artist sees to find him — the power and the unease of the investigation.

## 4 THE BRANCH MAP

**COUNT: 2 terminal outcomes — the composer caught (read the tableau) or the wrong man condemned (followed the circumstance) — gated on whether the player reads the bodies as art or as rage.**

- **CATCH THE COMPOSER:** the player reads the tableau correctly, sees the murders as composition, finds the artist, stops the masterpiece. WRITES: the killer taken; the innocent cleared; the horror named as devotion-turned-atrocity.
- **CONDEMN THE INNOCENT:** the player follows the circumstantial trail to the grieving lover, condemns him, and the composer keeps working. WRITES: the wrong man punished; the artist free; the composition continuing.
- **THE READING CONSTANT:** the deduction requires seeing the bodies as the killer sees them — as art, not rage — in both the success and the failure; the frame the player brings decides the outcome.
- **THE THROUGH-LINE:** the branch is deduction-versus-circumstance — two answers to "can you read the murders as the composition they are," and misreading condemns an innocent and frees the artist.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — THE INVESTIGATION CAN'T ACTUALLY FAIL INTO THE WRONG CONVICTION SILENTLY.** Like most witcher-sense investigations, the deduction is largely a guided corridor; the "condemn the innocent" outcome is hard to reach and, when reached, under-consequenced — so the whodunit's stakes (get it wrong, free the killer, punish the innocent) rarely land as a real risk the player feels.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** a forensic whodunit must be genuinely FAILABLE into a wrong conviction with legible consequences — the innocent condemned, the killer free, the world recording it — so reading the scene wrong has weight. A deduction that can't really fail is a tour (the #19/#160 failable-deduction law).

**F2 — THE KILLER'S AESTHETIC HORROR IS UNDERPLAYED.** The idea that the murderer experiences atrocity as beauty — composes corpses as holy art — is the quest's most chilling concept, but it's delivered in a few lines at the end; the player never really inhabits the killer's aesthetic frame, so the "see what he sees" horror is stated more than felt.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a killer experiences atrocity as beauty, the player must be made to briefly INHABIT that frame — the composition's logic shown, the terrible beauty legible for a moment — so the horror of understanding it lands. To catch the artist you must see what he sees, and the game must let you (the #35 town-as-psyche law, applied to a killer's aesthetic).

**F3 — THE CORRUPTED FAITH ISN'T CONNECTED TO THE REAL CHURCH'S DOCTRINE.** The killer twists the Eternal Fire's faith into a license to compose with corpses, but the quest doesn't dramatize HOW the doctrine enables him — the church is backdrop, so the "faith corrupted into atrocity" theme floats free of the actual religion the setting built.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** a corrupted-faith killer's madness must be TRACEABLE to the doctrine he twisted — the line from the real teaching to the atrocity shown — so the corruption indicts the faith, not just the man. Aimed-wrong devotion is only meaningful if the aim is legible (the #154/#166 faith-as-real law).

## 6 WHY IT WORKS (W1-W10)

W1. THE BODIES ARE A TEXT. The murders are posed, arranged, composed — a language the player must learn to read. The deduction is forensic literacy: the corpses mean something, and solving is understanding the grammar.
W2. THE KILLER IS AN ARTIST. The murderer composes rather than rages — corpses as components in a masterpiece. The motive is not passion but aesthetic devotion, which is far more unsettling.
W3. THE ATROCITY IS EXPERIENCED AS BEAUTY. The killer's madness is that he finds his murders sacred and lovely — the horror is not that he's confused but that he's certain, and certain of beauty.
W4. YOU MUST THINK LIKE HIM TO CATCH HIM. The deduction requires seeing the bodies as the killer sees them — as art — which is the investigation's power and its unease: to solve it you glimpse what he glimpses.
W5. THE RED HERRING IS GRIEF. The circumstantial trail points to a grieving innocent whose sorrow looks like guilt — condemning him is the failure, and it lets the artist keep composing.
W6. THE FAITH IS CORRUPTED, NOT ABSENT. The killer twists a real religion into a license — the atrocity wears devotion, which is the false-faith horror (#166) in a serial killer.
W7. THE ZEALOT CANNOT BE REASONED OUT. He is sincere; certainty does not negotiate. The removed "reason him out of his faith" verb is the quest's coldest truth: the corrupted devout are not persuadable.
W8. THE INVESTIGATION IS FORENSIC. Reading corpses, scenes, poses with senses — the quest is detection, not combat, and the tension is comprehension, not violence.
W9. THE HORROR IS AESTHETIC. Not gore for shock but murder-as-composition — the unease is intellectual and moral, the recognition that beauty and atrocity can be the same act to the wrong mind.
W10. TO SOLVE IT IS TO BE COMPLICIT FOR A MOMENT. Reading the tableau means, briefly, seeing the murders as the killer does — the deduction implicates the player in the artist's frame, which is the quest's lingering discomfort.
