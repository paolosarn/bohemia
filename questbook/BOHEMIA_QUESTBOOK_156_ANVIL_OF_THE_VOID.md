# BOHEMIA QUESTBOOK #156 — "A PARAGON OF HER KIND / THE ANVIL OF THE VOID (THE ENDS THAT EAT THE MEANS)"
**Game:** Dragon Age: Origins (2009)
**Studio:** BioWare
**Quest:** A Paragon of Her Kind — the dwarven quest. A succession crisis sends the player into the Deep Roads after a lost Paragon, and the search ends at the Anvil of the Void: a device that forges unstoppable golems by binding living souls into them. The best "the tool that could save everyone is made of atrocity" quest.
**Type:** INDIVIDUAL-QUEST DEEP DIVE (mined fresh 7/17 from the Dragon Age catalogue; corpus anchor Q42/Q136 for the game's larger machines)
**FORMAT:** v2 — cast + conversation node trees + branch map.
**Filed:** 7/17/26
**Why pulled:** Bohemia's Amalgamation IS an Anvil — a technology that promises to save everyone by consuming people, sold as salvation. This quest is the genre's cleanest specimen of the means-vs-ends machine: a weapon that could win the war and end the crisis, whose every unit is a murdered volunteer, defended by someone who believes the cost is worth it. It rhymes hard with SOMA's ARK (#34) and our Liberate ending: destroy the thing that promises to save you, or use it and become what it is.

## 0 CORE IDEA

**The search for a lost legend ends at a machine that forges invincible soldiers out of murdered souls, and the quest is whether a tool powerful enough to save your people is worth the atrocity it runs on.**

The dwarves need a king, and their broken succession law says only a living Paragon can name one — so the player is sent into the Deep Roads to find Branka, a Paragon who vanished years ago chasing a lost secret. The descent is a horror: Branka drove her entire House to death in the dark pursuing the Anvil of the Void, the forge that made the dwarves' legendary golems. And the Anvil's secret is the atrocity: each golem is a living dwarf whose soul was bound into the armor, conscious, enslaved, forever. Branka wants it — with it she could forge an army that would save the dwarves from the darkspawn tide and restore their dying civilization. Caridin, the ancient Paragon smith who built it and was himself forced into a golem, wants it destroyed — he has spent centuries in torment regretting what he made.

The machine is the oldest and hardest one: the ends and the means. Branka is not a cartoon villain — she is right that the dwarves are dying and the Anvil could save them; she is also willing to murder to have it, and the golems are proof of what "worth it" costs. Caridin is not naive — he made the thing, he knows exactly what it could do, and he wants it gone anyway, because he has lived inside the price. The player decides: help Branka claim the Anvil (a weapon that could win, built of slavery) or help Caridin destroy it (throwing away the tool that could save everyone, to end the atrocity). Whoever the player backs, the other dies, and a Paragon must be named to fix the succession the whole descent was for.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** a tool that could save everyone is still made of the people it consumed, and the question is never whether it works — it is whether you can live as the kind of thing that uses it.

## 1 CAST + WHAT EACH ONE WANTS

### BRANKA — the Paragon who'd pay any price
- **What she wants (stated):** the Anvil of the Void, to forge an army and save the dying dwarven civilization.
- **What she wants (real):** exactly that — she is not lying about the goal, which is what makes her terrible. She believes the salvation justifies the slaughter.
- **What she'll trade:** her House (already dead in the dark for this), her wife Hespith, the player's conscience — whatever the Anvil costs.
- **What she will never say out loud:** that she has already become the thing the Anvil requires; the murders are behind her, not ahead.
- **Function:** THE ENDS, personified: a good goal pursued past every line, the true believer in the tool.

### CARIDIN — the maker who wants it unmade
- **What he wants:** the Anvil destroyed, so no one is ever again bound living into metal.
- **What he'll trade:** the crown he can forge (a Paragon's right) to whoever helps him end it.
- **What he will never say out loud:** the full weight of centuries spent as a golem regretting his own genius — he shows it instead of saying it.
- **Function:** THE MEANS' TRUE COST, embodied: the creator who lived inside the price and chose, finally, against his own creation.

### THE GOLEMS / HESPITH — the murdered volunteers
- **What they want:** to have never been forged; Hespith, Branka's wife, is being turned into worse in the dark.
- **Function:** THE PRICE made ambulatory — the "army" is a crowd of conscious enslaved dead, and one of them is the antagonist's own wife.

### THE DWARVEN THRONE — the reason for the descent
- **What it wants:** a Paragon to break the succession deadlock and name a king.
- **Function:** THE STAKES that sent the player down: a dying civilization needing both a ruler and, maybe, a weapon.

### THE ANVIL ITSELF — the tool that is an atrocity
- The forge that promises salvation and runs on souls.
- **Function:** THE FORK, as an object: use it and win at the cost of your soul, or destroy it and lose the tool to save the wound.

## 2 FULL EVENT FLOW (STAGE BY STAGE)

### STAGE 1 — THE SUCCESSION [MANDATORY]
The dwarven throne is deadlocked; the law demands a living Paragon to name a king. The player is sent into the Deep Roads to find Branka, the only candidate.

### STAGE 2 — THE DESCENT [MANDATORY]
The Deep Roads horror: the trail of Branka's dead House, Hespith's mad warnings, the dawning scale of what Branka sacrificed to get this far. The cost is shown before the prize.

### STAGE 3 — THE ANVIL REVEALED [MANDATORY]
Caridin's truth: the golems are living dwarves bound into metal; the Anvil is an atrocity; he made it and regrets it. The legendary weapon is unmasked as slavery.

### STAGE 4 — THE FORK [MANDATORY]
Branka wants the Anvil to save the dwarves; Caridin wants it destroyed. The player must back one. The other, and often a fight, follows.

### STAGE 5 — THE CROWN [MANDATORY]
Whoever survives (or the player, via Branka's death) forges the Paragon's crown to name a king, resolving the succession the descent was for — with the Anvil kept or broken behind them.

## 3 THE CONVERSATIONS (THE ACTUAL MACHINE)

### NODE A-1 — Caridin. Entry: stage 3. The maker, indicting his own masterpiece.
He explains what the golems are and begs for the Anvil's destruction.
> "The golems are people?" [gate: none] -> the atrocity confirmed: conscious dwarves bound living into metal, forever
> "It could save the dwarves, though." [gate: none] -> he doesn't deny the power; he denies that the power is worth the price. THE ONE LINE DOING THE WORK, paraphrased: I made it, I know what it can do, and I have spent three hundred years learning what it costs
> "Why should I destroy the one thing that could win the war?" [gate: none] -> because a victory built of slaves is the thing you were fighting against
> (say nothing; look at the golems) [gate: none] SILENCE -> their conscious misery on the record before the choice

### NODE A-2 — Branka. Entry: stage 4. The ends, unrepentant.
She wants the Anvil and will not pretend the cost troubles her.
> "You killed your whole House for this." [gate: none] -> she does not flinch; the dwarves are dying and this saves them, and she has already paid what it took
> "Hespith. Your wife." [gate: none] -> the price made personal; she has spent even that
> "The Anvil is slavery." [gate: none] -> and slavery, she argues, is cheaper than extinction — the ends' whole case, stated cleanly
> NOVERB "Let's take the Anvil but never use it that way" — no line exists; the Anvil is what it is, and having it is being willing to use it. The removed verb is the fork's honesty: you cannot own the atrocity-tool and keep clean hands about it.

### NODE A-3 — the fork. Entry: stage 4-5.
The forge kept or broken; a Paragon dead either way.
> (side with Caridin, destroy the Anvil) [gate: none] -> the tool that could save everyone is thrown away to end the atrocity; Branka dies; Caridin forges the crown, then chooses his own end
> (side with Branka, keep the Anvil) [gate: none] -> the weapon is claimed; Caridin dies; the dwarves gain an army of slaves and the player owns that choice
> (a middle, if maneuvered) [gate: careful play] -> Branka's obsession still ends her; the Anvil's fate still forks; the game resists a clean "everyone lives"
WHAT THIS NODE COSTS: a Paragon's life whichever way, and either the tool or the soul — the descent's whole point was to force this trade.

## 4 THE BRANCH MAP

**COUNT: 2 core terminal outcomes (Anvil kept / Anvil destroyed), each costing one Paragon, times the king the surviving Paragon then crowns.**

- **DESTROY THE ANVIL (side Caridin):** the atrocity ends; the golem-army is forgone; the dwarves face the darkspawn without the weapon that could have won. Branka dies obsessed; Caridin forges the crown and unmakes his creation and himself. WRITES: the tool gone, the wound closed, the war harder.
- **KEEP THE ANVIL (side Branka):** the weapon is claimed; Caridin dies; the dwarves gain an army of enslaved souls and a chance to survive, and the player is the kind of thing that chose it. WRITES: the tool kept, the atrocity ongoing, the war winnable.
- **THE CROWN:** whichever Paragon survives (or the player via Branka's fall) names a king, resolving the succession — the political stakes fulfilled, the moral stakes never clean.
- **THE THROUGH-LINE:** every ending is a verdict on means-and-ends — the branches are two answers to "is a tool worth its atrocity," and the game refuses to make either free.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — BRANKA IS RIGHT AND THE GAME UNDERSELLS IT.** The dwarves ARE dying; the Anvil COULD save them; Branka's utilitarian case is genuinely strong — but the quest stacks the presentation so heavily toward "she's a monster" (the dead House, mad Hespith) that the ends-side is emotionally foreclosed before the argument is fair. The hardest version would make keeping the Anvil genuinely tempting.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** when a quest forks on means-vs-ends, the ENDS must be made genuinely tempting — the salvation real, present, and costed, not pre-buried under the villain's atrocities. If the "use the tool" path is obviously monstrous, it is a difficulty setting, not a dilemma (Q126's F1 law, at civilizational scale).

**F2 — THE GOLEMS DON'T GET TO SPEAK FOR THEMSELVES ENOUGH.** The murdered-volunteer horror is delivered mostly through Caridin and Hespith; the individual golems, the actual price, are largely mute scenery. The atrocity is told about more than it is heard from.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** the PRICE of an atrocity-tool must have its own voices — the consumed should testify directly, not only through the maker and the madwoman. Let the Amalgamation's uploaded speak for what was done to them (SOMA's abandoned Simon, #34, is the model).

**F3 — THE "EVERYONE LIVES" MIDDLE IS A HIDDEN SKILL PUZZLE.** A player can maneuver a less-bloody resolution, but only via non-obvious dialogue paths; the clean-ish outcome is gated behind meta-knowledge rather than the player's understanding, so most players hit a harder ending without knowing a gentler one existed.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a gentler resolution exists, its PATH must be discoverable in-world (a clue, a reachable insight), not walkthrough-only — so that the player who does the moral work can find the mercy, per the #26/#155 earned-outcome law.

## 6 WHY IT WORKS (W1-W10)

W1. THE TOOL IS MADE OF THE ATROCITY. The golem-army isn't guarded by an atrocity — it IS one; every unit a murdered soul. The weapon and the crime are the same object, which is the purest means-vs-ends form.
W2. THE COST IS SHOWN BEFORE THE PRIZE. The descent walks the player through Branka's dead House and Hespith's ruin before revealing the Anvil — you feel the price before you see what it buys.
W3. THE MAKER TURNS AGAINST HIS MASTERPIECE. Caridin built the Anvil and wants it destroyed, having lived inside its cost for centuries — the most credible possible witness against a tool: its inventor.
W4. THE ENDS HAVE A REAL CASE. Branka is not lying — the dwarves are dying and the Anvil could save them. The dilemma is real because the monstrous option might actually work.
W5. NO CLEAN FORK. Keep the tool and own the slavery; destroy it and forgo the salvation. Both cost a Paragon and a principle; the game refuses a free exit.
W6. THE PRICE IS PERSONAL. Branka spent her own wife; the atrocity has a face the antagonist loved. The abstraction of "souls bound to metal" gets a name.
W7. THE VILLAIN ISN'T ONE. Branka is a good goal pursued past every line, not evil for its own sake — the scariest antagonist is the one whose ends you can't dismiss.
W8. THE POLITICAL STAKES FRAME THE MORAL ONES. The whole descent is for a succession crown; the means-vs-ends fork is nested inside a governance need, so the choice has downstream weight beyond itself.
W9. THE MAKER PAYS LAST. Caridin, if he wins, unmakes his creation and then himself — the atrocity's author choosing to end with it. Letting go is a death he chooses.
W10. THE QUESTION SURVIVES THE CREDITS. Players argue the Anvil choice for years — the sign the dilemma was real. A tool worth its atrocity has no settled answer, and the quest is honest enough not to give one.
