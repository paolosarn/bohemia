# BOHEMIA QUESTBOOK #185 — "LAST VOYAGE OF THE USS CONSTITUTION (HELP A CREW OF ROBOTS SAIL A WARSHIP OFF A ROOFTOP 200 YEARS TOO LATE)"
**Game:** Fallout 4 (2015)
**Studio:** Bethesda Game Studios
**Quest:** Last Voyage of the USS Constitution — an 18th-century frigate blasted onto a rooftop by a nuclear war, crewed by battered robots who still believe they're mid-mission, want to repair their ship and "sail" it to the sea, and cannot be told the war is 200 years over, so the player either helps a doomed delusion reach its beautiful pointless goal or breaks the only thing keeping the crew whole.

**FORMAT:** v2 — cast + conversation node trees + branch map.
**Filed:** 7/18/26
**Why pulled:** Bohemia needs the quest that is entirely comic on the surface and entirely about a sustaining delusion underneath — robots who kept doing their duty for two centuries because no one ever told them to stop, whose mission is impossible and whose belief in it is the only thing holding them together, so the player must choose between serving the delusion to its lovely doomed end or shattering it with a truth that helps no one. The USS Constitution is the specimen: a crew mid-mission 200 years late, a captain who will not be corrected, a ship that must "sail," and a player caught between kindness-as-delusion and truth-as-cruelty. ## 0 CORE IDEA

**A wooden 18th-century warship, hurled onto the top of a building by the nuclear apocalypse, is still crewed by a company of damaged robots who never received an order to stand down and therefore still believe they are on active duty — their captain intends to repair the vessel and sail it to the sea to resume a mission two centuries dead, cannot be reasoned out of it, and the player must decide whether to help this magnificent delusion reach its beautiful pointless goal or to be the one who breaks it.**

The USS Constitution — a sailing frigate — sits impaled on a rooftop miles inland, where a bomb-blast dropped it. Its crew are Mister Handy-style robots who have been maintaining the ship, standing watch, and running drills for two hundred years, because the last order they got was to carry out a mission and no order ever came to end it. Captain Ironsides, the robot commander, is fully convinced the ship is seaworthy, the mission is live, and the only problem is a few repairs and some raiders in the way. He does not know the war is over. He cannot be told — the crew's entire coherence, their sanity, their reason to keep functioning, is built on the belief that they are still serving. The player is recruited to help: fix the ship, get the parts, drive off the scavengers, and help the Constitution "sail" — which, from a rooftop, means firing its rockets and launching itself in a doomed, glorious arc toward the distant sea it will never reach whole.

The machine is that the delusion is the crew's life-support, and the player can serve it or shatter it, and neither is clean. Helping is a kindness that is also a lie: you are propping up a belief you know is false, spending real effort to send a crew of robots on a suicidal flight toward a mission that ended before your grandparents were born. But the flight is also the only thing that would ever let them complete what they were built to want — a beautiful, pointless fulfillment. Shattering the delusion (siding with the scavengers, or simply refusing, or telling a truth the crew can't hold) is more "honest" and helps no one: it strands the crew in a purpose they can't finish and can't abandon, or destroys them, and the truth buys nothing but the satisfaction of having been correct. The quest is played for comedy — robot sea-shanties, a frigate blasting off a roof — and the comedy is the mercy: it lets the player feel the absurd tenderness of serving someone's beautiful lie without the game ever forcing them to grieve it. And underneath, it asks the real question: when a delusion is the only thing holding someone together, is helping them complete it a kindness or a cruelty, and is the truth worth what it breaks?

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** a delusion can be the only thing keeping someone whole, and the choice to serve it or shatter it is never clean — helping is a kindness built on a lie, the truth is a correctness that helps no one, and sometimes the most loving thing you can do is help someone's beautiful pointless mission reach its end rather than insist they face a truth that would only strand them.

## 1 CAST + WHAT EACH ONE WANTS

### CAPTAIN IRONSIDES — the commander who will not be corrected
- **What he wants (stated):** to repair his ship and sail it to sea to resume the mission.
- **What he wants (real):** to keep being what he is — a captain on active duty; the delusion is his self.
- **What he'll trade:** nothing about the mission; it is not negotiable, because it is his coherence.
- **What he will never accept:** that the war is over and the mission is dead.
- **Function:** THE SUSTAINING DELUSION EMBODIED: the being whose false belief is the only thing holding him whole.

### THE ROBOT CREW — the company still serving
- Damaged robots running drills and watches for two centuries on an order never rescinded.
- **Function:** THE COHERENCE AT STAKE: the crew whose sanity is the belief the player can serve or break.

### THE SCAVENGERS — the ones who'd strip the ship
- Raiders who want the Constitution's parts and would end the delusion by force.
- **Function:** THE TRUTH-BY-DESTRUCTION: the option that shatters the delusion without mercy or ceremony.

### THE PLAYER — the one who serves or shatters
- **What they want:** to do right by a crew whose mission is impossible and whose belief is their life.
- **Function:** THE CHOOSER: the one who decides whether to help the beautiful lie reach its end or break it.

### THE "VOYAGE" — the doomed, glorious flight
- The rocket-launch off the rooftop; the mission's only possible fulfillment, and its guaranteed doom.
- **Function:** THE BEAUTIFUL POINTLESS END: the delusion completed rather than corrected — kindness as absurdity.

## 4 THE BRANCH MAP

**COUNT: 2 terminal outcomes — help the Constitution launch (the delusion served to its glorious doomed end) or let it be stripped/stranded (the delusion shattered by truth or force) — with the constant that the crew's coherence IS the false belief, so serving it is a kindness built on a lie and correcting it is a truth that helps no one.**

- **THE VOYAGE:** the ship launched in a doomed glorious arc; the mission fulfilled the only way it could be; the crew whole to the end. WRITES: the delusion completed rather than corrected; effort spent on a beautiful pointless flight; kindness as absurd mercy.
- **THE WRECK:** the ship stripped or stranded; the delusion broken by scavengers or refusal; the crew ended or purposeless. WRITES: the truth served; a crew stranded in a mission they can't finish or abandon; correctness that bought nothing.
- **THE DELUSION-IS-LIFE CONSTANT:** in both branches, the false belief is the crew's coherence — you cannot correct it without killing it, and the quest never pretends the truth would leave them whole.
- **THE THROUGH-LINE:** the branch is serve-the-lie versus shatter-it — two answers to "whose delusion do you honor when the delusion is the only thing holding them together," and the quest, in comic armor, insists neither is clean.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — THE COMEDY CAN FULLY MASK THE THEME, SO THE DELUSION QUESTION NEVER LANDS.** The quest is so committed to the joke (robot sailors, a frigate blasting off a roof) that many players experience it purely as a gag and never feel the ache underneath — the profound "a delusion is holding them together" idea is available but easy to miss entirely, so the mercy-vs-truth weight is optional in a way that can neuter it.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a comic quest carries a serious question underneath, ONE beat must let the ache through — a line, a pause, a moment where the delusion's cost is briefly visible — so the theme reaches even the player who came for the joke. Comedy that never once cracks to show the wound is a gag that forgot it was about something (#163's the-costume-must-crack law).

**F2 — THE "SHATTER IT" PATH IS THINLY IMAGINED COMPARED TO THE VOYAGE.** Helping the ship sail is the lavish, memorable outcome; siding against the crew is a comparatively flat destruction, so the game clearly wants the player to serve the delusion — which tips a genuine mercy-vs-truth dilemma into a single intended answer with a punish-path attached.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest offers serve-the-delusion vs shatter-it, both must be fully IMAGINED — the truth-path given its own weight and consequence, not a flat punishment — so the dilemma is real rather than a preferred ending with a bad option beside it (#181's equal-outcomes law).

**F3 — IRONSIDES NEVER FLICKERS, SO THE DELUSION READS AS PROGRAMMING, NOT PERSON.** The captain is unshakably deluded with no moment of near-doubt, so the crew can read as malfunctioning machines rather than beings whose belief is load-bearing — which makes serving the delusion feel like humoring a broken toaster instead of honoring a fragile coherence.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest's weight depends on a delusion being load-bearing for a PERSON, that person must flicker once — a near-doubt, a defended certainty — so the belief reads as coherence held against a crack, not just a stuck subroutine. A delusion with no visible seam is a malfunction, not a mercy (#162's the-crack-makes-it-human law).

## 6 WHY IT WORKS (W1-W10)

W1. THE DELUSION IS LIFE-SUPPORT. The crew's belief that they're still on duty is the only thing holding them coherent — correcting it and killing it are nearly the same act, which is the quest's real engine under the jokes.
W2. THE MISSION IS IMPOSSIBLE AND BEAUTIFUL. Sailing a rooftop frigate to the sea is doomed and glorious — the fulfillment is absurd and genuinely moving at once.
W3. THE CAPTAIN CAN'T BE CORRECTED. Ironsides cannot hold the truth — the delusion is his self, not his error, which makes "just tell him" impossible.
W4. HELPING IS KINDNESS-AS-LIE. Serving the crew means propping up a false belief with real effort — a mercy that is also a deception, held honestly.
W5. TRUTH HELPS NO ONE. Shattering the delusion strands or destroys the crew and buys only correctness — the honest option that is also the cruel one.
W6. THE VOYAGE COMPLETES THE WANT. The doomed flight is the only way the crew's built-in purpose could ever be fulfilled — completion instead of correction.
W7. THE TRUTH LEAVES NO ONE GRATEFUL. The removed "tell them and be thanked" verb is the spine: some truths only strand the people you tell them to.
W8. THE COMEDY IS THE MERCY. The gag lets the player serve a beautiful lie without being forced to grieve it — the tone is itself a kindness to the player.
W9. TWO CENTURIES OF DUTY. The crew kept serving because no order came to stop — the horror and tenderness of a duty never rescinded.
W10. IT IS THE AMALGAMATION'S LIE IN SLAPSTICK. A comfort-belief that holds the broken together, served or shattered — Bohemia's whole "no one is ever lost" question, in a frigate on a roof.
