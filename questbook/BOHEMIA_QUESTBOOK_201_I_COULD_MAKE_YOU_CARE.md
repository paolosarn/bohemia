# BOHEMIA QUESTBOOK #201 — "I COULD MAKE YOU CARE (A BROKEN GENIUS BUILT A PARADISE THAT ONLY WORKS IF YOU LOBOTOMIZE THE PEOPLE IN IT)"
**Game:** Fallout: New Vegas — Old World Blues / the Veronica arc (2011)
**Studio:** Obsidian Entertainment
**Quest:** I Could Make You Care — a companion who belongs to a hidebound order of hoarders must decide whether to stay loyal to a dying, dogmatic family that will not change and is doomed by its own rigidity, or break with everything she loves to try to save it from the outside, and the player is the voice that tips her toward faith, reform, or leaving.
**Type:** INDIVIDUAL-QUEST DEEP DIVE (mined fresh 7/18 from the Fallout: New Vegas catalogue; corpus anchor Q03-class for the game's larger machines)
**FORMAT:** v2 — cast + conversation node trees + branch map.
**Filed:** 7/18/26
**Why pulled:** Bohemia needs the quest where someone loves an institution that is killing itself by refusing to change, and must choose between loyalty to a doomed family and betrayal-to-save-it — where staying faithful means going down with the ship and reforming means being cast out by the people you're trying to rescue. Veronica's arc is the specimen: a member of a hoarding, dogmatic order that hides from the world and will not adapt, a companion who sees the doom coming, and a choice between faith, reform from within, and leaving. It is the fold's loyalty-to-a-dying-institution machine fused with the conduct-book's when-is-betrayal-love question, and the "the family that won't change is doomed and you love it anyway" law our factions can carry.

## 0 CORE IDEA

**A companion belongs, heart and history, to an insular order that has survived by hoarding old technology, hiding underground, and enforcing a rigid dogma that forbids adaptation — and she can see, clearly, that this rigidity is killing them: the order is shrinking, calcifying, and doomed by its refusal to change, and she must decide whether to stay loyal and go down with the family she loves, try to reform it from within (and be branded a traitor by the people she's trying to save), or leave it entirely, and the player is the voice at her shoulder as she chooses between faith, reform, and exile.**

Veronica is a scribe of the Brotherhood of Steel, an order that once had a mission and now mostly has a bunker: they hoard pre-war technology, hide from a world they no longer engage, and enforce a dogma that treats any deviation as heresy. She loves them. They are her family, her identity, the people who raised her. And she is not blind: she can see that the order's rigidity is not strength but suicide, that a family which will not change in a changing world is a family digging its own grave, that the very traditions keeping them "pure" are killing them. Her personal quest is the crisis of that clarity. She wants to save the order, but saving it would require it to change, and it will excommunicate anyone who suggests change. So she faces three roads: stay loyal and faithful, accepting the doom out of love (going down with the ship); push for reform from within, knowing it means being branded a traitor by the very people she is trying to rescue (betrayal that is actually the deepest loyalty); or leave, abandon the family to its chosen fate, and build a life outside the thing that made her. The player is the companion voice that tips her, and the quest makes each road cost her something irreplaceable.

The machine is that loving a dying institution forces a choice where every option is a kind of betrayal, and the deepest loyalty may look exactly like treason. Veronica's tragedy is that there is no road that is both faithful and effective. Staying loyal is faithful but complicit in the doom. Reforming is effective but requires her to be, by the order's own definition, a traitor — to break the dogma to save the people, to be cast out by those she's trying to rescue. Leaving is honest but is the abandonment she most fears becoming. The quest refuses to let "love your family" and "save your family" be the same act, because a family that will not change makes those two things opposites: to save them you must betray what they insist on being, and to stay faithful to what they are you must let them die. The player's influence decides which betrayal Veronica can live with, and the honesty is that some institutions have made loyalty and survival mutually exclusive, so anyone who loves them is forced to choose which one to keep — and the person who tries to save a rigid family by changing it will be remembered by that family as the one who betrayed it, even though they were the only one who truly tried to keep it alive.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** when an institution you love has made rigidity its identity, loyalty and survival become opposites, and every road is a betrayal — staying faithful betrays their future, reforming betrays their rules, leaving betrays them entirely — so the person who most wants to save a dying family is often forced to become, in its eyes, the traitor, because the only way to keep a thing that won't change alive is to break what it insists on being.

## 1 CAST + WHAT EACH ONE WANTS

### VERONICA — the one who loves a doomed family
- **What she wants (stated):** to save the order; to belong to it and have it survive.
- **What she wants (real):** for loyalty and survival to not be opposites — which the order has made impossible.
- **What she'll trade:** her belonging, if reform requires it; her effectiveness, if faith requires it.
- **What she will never stop feeling:** that leaving is the abandonment she most fears.
- **Function:** THE LOVER OF A DYING INSTITUTION: the one forced to choose which betrayal to live with.

### THE ORDER (THE BROTHERHOOD) — the family that won't change
- **What it wants:** purity, tradition, the dogma intact; survival on its own rigid terms or not at all.
- **Function:** THE RIGID FAMILY: the institution that has made loyalty and survival mutually exclusive.

### THE DOGMA / THE ELDERS — the rules that forbid the cure
- The doctrine that treats adaptation as heresy; the enforcers who would excommunicate a reformer.
- **Function:** THE SUICIDE BY PURITY: the traditions killing the order in the name of keeping it pure.

### THE PLAYER — the voice at her shoulder
- **What they want:** to help Veronica choose the betrayal she can live with; to tip faith, reform, or exile.
- **Function:** THE TIPPING VOICE: the one who influences which loyalty she keeps.

### THE THREE ROADS — faith, reform, exile
- Stay loyal (down with the ship), reform from within (branded traitor), or leave (abandon the family).
- **Function:** THE THREE BETRAYALS: the roads that each cost her something irreplaceable, none clean.

## 4 THE BRANCH MAP

**COUNT: 3 terminal roads — stay loyal (faith kept, future betrayed), reform from within (family saved-if-possible, branded a traitor), or leave (honesty kept, family abandoned) — with the constant that the order has made loyalty and survival opposites, so every road betrays something and none is clean.**

- **STAY LOYAL:** Veronica keeps faith and goes down with the family; the future betrayed for love of what they are. WRITES: loyalty preserved, doom accepted; complicity in the grave out of devotion.
- **REFORM FROM WITHIN:** Veronica breaks the dogma to try to save the people; branded a traitor by those she's rescuing. WRITES: the deepest loyalty called treason; the rules betrayed to keep the family alive.
- **LEAVE:** Veronica abandons the order and builds a life outside; the family left to its chosen fate. WRITES: honesty kept, the abandonment she feared enacted; freedom bought with the family.
- **THE LOYALTY-VS-SURVIVAL CONSTANT:** in every road, the order's rigidity has made faith and survival mutually exclusive — no choice keeps both, and the quest never pretends one does.
- **THE THROUGH-LINE:** the branch is faith-reform-or-exile — three betrayals of a family that won't change, and the quest insists the one who most wants to save a rigid institution is forced to become, in its eyes, the traitor.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — THE REFORM ROAD'S "BRANDED A TRAITOR" COST IS TOLD MORE THAN SHOWN.** The devastating idea (saving the family gets you cast out by it) is largely stated in Veronica's dialogue rather than dramatized as a felt excommunication, so the sharpest truth (the deepest loyalty looks like treason) can land as a line rather than a wound the player watches her take.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest's point is that reform gets the reformer branded a traitor, the branding must be SHOWN — the family's rejection of the one saving them made felt — so "the deepest loyalty looks like treason" is a scene, not a summary. A betrayal-that-is-love told only in dialogue never lands as loss (#187's the-consequence-must-be-shown law).

**F2 — THE ORDER'S RIGIDITY CAN READ AS CARTOONISH STUBBORNNESS, WEAKENING THE LOVE.** If the institution is written as simply blind and dogmatic, it becomes hard to believe Veronica loves it, so the tragedy (loving a family that's killing itself) needs the order to have a real, sympathetic reason for its rigidity — otherwise the choice reads as "why not just leave the idiots."
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest turns on loving a self-destroying institution, the institution's rigidity must have a SYMPATHETIC root — a fear, a wound, a logic — so the love is believable and leaving isn't obviously correct. A family that's only stupid isn't a tragedy to lose (#189's the-flaw-must-be-understandable law).

**F3 — THE PLAYER'S TIPPING INFLUENCE CAN OVERRIDE VERONICA'S OWN AGENCY.** Because the player's speech tips her decision, the quest can make Veronica's life-defining choice feel like the player's to make rather than hers — so the profound personal reckoning becomes a persuade-check the player wins, sidelining her as the author of her own betrayal.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a companion faces a life-defining choice the dynast influences, the companion must remain the AUTHOR of it — the dynast's voice tipping, not deciding — so it's her reckoning, not the player's win. A companion whose defining choice is the player's to make was never really a person (#186's the-choice-stays-theirs law).

## 6 WHY IT WORKS (W1-W10)

W1. LOYALTY AND SURVIVAL ARE OPPOSITES. The order's rigidity has made keeping faith and staying alive mutually exclusive — the whole engine of the tragedy.
W2. SHE LOVES A DYING FAMILY. Veronica can see the doom and cannot stop loving them — the clarity and the devotion at war.
W3. EVERY ROAD IS A BETRAYAL. Faith betrays the future, reform betrays the rules, exile betrays them all — no clean option.
W4. THE DEEPEST LOYALTY LOOKS LIKE TREASON. To save the family she must be branded its traitor — the sharpest thing the quest says about rigid institutions.
W5. NO ROAD IS BOTH FAITHFUL AND EFFECTIVE. The removed "save it without betraying it" verb is the spine: the order made those two things impossible to hold together.
W6. THE DOGMA IS THE SUICIDE. The traditions keeping them pure are killing them — purity as a grave, rigidity as death.
W7. LEAVING IS THE FEARED SELF. Exile is the abandonment Veronica most dreads becoming — even the honest road costs her identity.
W8. THE PLAYER TIPS, DOESN'T DECIDE. The companion voice influences which betrayal she can live with — her reckoning, aided not authored.
W9. THE FAMILY WILL MISREMEMBER THE SAVER. The one who tried hardest to keep them alive is the one they'll call traitor — the cruel irony of reform.
W10. IT IS LOYALTY-TO-A-DYING-THING IN ONE SCRIBE. Whether to go down with, reform, or leave a doomed family — Bohemia's whole fold question of institutions that won't change, in one companion's bind.
