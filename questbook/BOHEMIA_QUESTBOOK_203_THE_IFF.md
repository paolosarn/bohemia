## 0 CORE IDEA

**The crew boards the hull of a dead god-machine — a titanic enemy vessel, killed long ago, drifting as a corpse — to salvage a single component they need, and inside the body of the thing they find the remains of the science team sent before them, who were slowly indoctrinated by the mere proximity of the dead horror into revering it, worshipping the very entity that would destroy their entire kind, turning on one another in its name, and leaving behind a record proving that this enemy does not need to be alive, or even conscious, to reach into the minds of anyone who comes near and convert them into loving their own killer.**

The Reaper is dead. Its hull is a derelict, a vast metal corpse the crew must enter to retrieve an IFF component. But the Reaper's most terrible weapon was never its guns; it was indoctrination — the ability to reshape the minds of those in its presence, gradually, until they revere it, defend it, and destroy themselves and each other in its service. And that weapon, it turns out, does not switch off when the Reaper dies. The science team sent to study the derelict came in as investigators and were slowly converted by nothing but proximity: their logs and bodies chart the descent from clinical curiosity to religious devotion to fratricidal madness, each entry more worshipful, until they were killing each other in the name of the thing that was already killing them. The crew, walking through this, faces two horrors at once: the record of what the dead god did to the people before them, and the dawning certainty that the same conversion is beginning to work on them, right now, simply because they are inside it. The component is real and needed; the price of retrieving it is exposure to a corruption that a corpse is still broadcasting.

The machine is that the horror does not need to be alive to consume you, and proximity to it is the danger, not confrontation. Every instinct says a dead enemy is a safe enemy — the thing is a corpse, salvage it and go. But this horror's power was never in its life; it was in its nearness, its influence, its ability to reshape whoever lingers. The predecessors' logs are the whole quest's engine: they are the crew's own future, pre-recorded, a step-by-step map of how curiosity becomes reverence becomes self-destruction, and they were people exactly as rational and forewarned as the crew is now. The crew reads their own fate and must retrieve the component and get out before the corpse finishes the same work on them. And the deepest cut is that the indoctrinated did not feel corrupted — they felt enlightened; the conversion does not present as an attack but as a revelation, so the victims defend it, which means the crew cannot trust the sense that they are "fine," because feeling fine is exactly what the corruption feels like from inside. The horror wins not by killing but by being adored, and a corpse can still be adored.

**The life lesson underneath, never spoken:** the things that destroy you do not have to be alive or even active to consume you — proximity, lingering, and exposure are the danger, and the worst corruptions do not feel like attacks but like revelations, so the people they take defend them and feel enlightened; the surest sign you are being consumed is that you feel fine, because being made to love the thing that kills you never once feels like being hurt.

## 5 HONEST FLAWS (BANKED)

**F1 — THE CREW'S OWN CONVERSION IS UNDERPLAYED, SO "IT'S HAPPENING TO YOU" DOESN'T LAND.** The logs vividly chart the predecessors' descent, but the crew's own beginning corruption is barely dramatized, so the crucial turn (the same thing is taking YOU right now) reads as a warning about others rather than a horror closing on the player — the "you can't trust feeling fine" idea stays external.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest's horror is a corruption spreading to the player's own side, the player's OWN exposure must be dramatized — a symptom, a wrong thought, a moment of unearned reverence — so "it's happening to you" lands as personal dread, not a cautionary tale about the last team (#194's the-corruption-must-touch-the-player law).

**F2 — THE COMPONENT-AS-GOAL CAN REDUCE THE HORROR TO A FETCH WITH SET DRESSING.** Because the mission objective is "get the IFF," a player can treat the indoctrination logs as flavor between combat and loot the component without the proximity-horror landing — so the profound "a corpse still consumes you" idea becomes atmosphere around a fetch quest.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest's horror is proximity-corruption, the OBJECTIVE must be entangled with the horror — retrieving the prize forcing real, felt exposure — so the salvage can't be done while ignoring the corruption. A fetch that lets the player skip the dread wasted its own premise (#196's the-goal-must-force-the-horror law).

**F3 — "FEELING FINE IS THE DANGER" IS STATED BUT NOT MECHANIZED.** The chilling idea (the conversion feels like enlightenment, so you can't trust your own okay-ness) is delivered in the logs but never made a mechanic the player experiences — the player never feels the seductive pull, so the insight stays a told fact rather than a felt trap.
**LAW FOR BOHEMIA:** if a quest's insight is that corruption feels like revelation, the SEDUCTION must be made felt — the player offered a genuinely appealing wrong thought — so "you can't trust feeling fine" is experienced, not just read. A seduction the player never feels is a warning, not a trap (#191's the-temptation-must-tempt law).

## 6 WHY IT WORKS (W1-W10)

W1. THE HORROR DOESN'T NEED TO BE ALIVE. A dead Reaper still corrupts by proximity — the corpse consumes, which is the whole engine and the deepest dread.
W2. PROXIMITY IS THE DANGER. Not attack, not death — nearness, lingering, exposure; the enemy's true weapon was always influence.
W3. THE PREDECESSORS ARE YOUR FUTURE. Their logs chart the exact descent the crew is beginning — the found future, pre-recorded.
W4. CONVERSION FEELS LIKE REVELATION. The indoctrinated felt enlightened, not attacked — so they defend it, which is the horror's cruelest trick.
W5. FEELING FINE IS THE SIGN OF BEING TAKEN. The removed "make it safe" verb is the spine: you can't trust your own okay-ness, because being consumed feels like clarity.
W6. THE HORROR IS ADORED, NOT FEARED. It wins by being worshipped — a corpse can still be loved, and being loved is how it kills.
W7. THE COMPONENT FORCES THE EXPOSURE. The needed salvage justifies the lethal lingering — the price-justifier that makes the crew stay where staying corrupts.
W8. IT CAN'T BE DEFEATED, ONLY FLED. The corpse still broadcasts; the crew survives by leaving, not by winning — the horror outlasts the mission.
W9. THE VICTIMS TURNED ON EACH OTHER. The conversion ends in fratricide in the horror's name — worship curdling into mutual destruction.
W10. IT IS PROXIMITY-CORRUPTS IN ONE CORPSE. A dead god that still eats minds by nearness — Bohemia's whole Amalgamation question of a thing that consumes by being adored, in a derelict hull.
